## Key Scholarly Sources (Print/Academic)

- **Gandhi, Mohandas K. — *An Autobiography: The Story of My Experiments with Truth* (1927)** — Primary source memoir. Gandhi's own account of his life through 1921. Essential for understanding his self-conception, though necessarily selective and self-justifying.

- **Fischer, Louis — *Gandhi: His Life and Message for the World* (1954)** — Early comprehensive biography by a journalist who knew Gandhi. Influential in shaping global perception, though hagiographic by modern standards.

- **Guha, Ramachandra — *Gandhi Before India* (2013) and *Gandhi: The Years That Changed the World* (2018)** — Definitive modern two-volume biography. Extensive archival research, balanced assessment of achievements and failures.

- **Dalton, Dennis — *Mahatma Gandhi: Nonviolent Power in Action* (2012)** — Focuses on Gandhi's political philosophy and the mechanics of satyagraha. Useful for understanding the strategic dimension of nonviolent resistance.

- **Tidrick, Kathryn — *Gandhi: A Political and Spiritual Life* (2006)** — Critical biography examining Gandhi's psychology, sexual experiments, and political contradictions.

## Controversies and Debates

### Gandhi's Sexual Experiments
- Gandhi slept naked with young women (including his grandniece Manu Gandhi) to test his celibacy vow. This has been variously interpreted as spiritual discipline (Gandhi's view), psychological disturbance (critics), or exploitative behavior (modern feminist critique).
- Sources: Tidrick (2006), Jad Adams *Gandhi: Naked Ambition* (2010)

### Gandhi and the Caste System
- Gandhi opposed untouchability but defended the varna (caste) system as an ideal social order. Ambedkar and other Dalit leaders criticized him as insufficiently radical on caste.
- Sources: B.R. Ambedkar *What Congress and Gandhi Have Done to the Untouchables* (1945)

### Gandhi and Partition
- Gandhi opposed the partition of India to the end, fasting to prevent it. Some historians argue his moral absolutism delayed practical solutions; others see his stance as principled and prophetic given the violence partition unleashed.

### Gandhi in South Africa
- Early Gandhi (1893–1906) accepted racial hierarchy, using "kaffir" for Black Africans and organizing Indians as a separate racial category. His views evolved, but this period complicates the saintly narrative.
- Sources: Ashwin Desai & Goolam Vahed *The South African Gandhi* (2015) — controversial revisionist account
