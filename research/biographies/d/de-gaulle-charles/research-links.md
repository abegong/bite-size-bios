# Charles de Gaulle — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did an obscure, twice-passed-over tank colonel — a man with no troops, no territory, and no legal mandate — talk a defeated nation into believing it had never surrendered, and then twice rebuild the French state in his own image, only to walk away from power both times over a point of principle?
- **Likely angle:** De Gaulle's life is an argument about legitimacy conducted in the first person. On 18 June 1940 he declared, almost alone at a BBC microphone, that he — not the legal government of Marshal Pétain — was France. Nearly nobody heard the broadcast; it was not even recorded. Everything afterward (Free France, the liberation, the Fifth Republic, the 1969 resignation over a lost referendum) is him collecting on that unbacked claim and insisting the debt be honored democratically. His own opening line frames it: "Toute ma vie, je me suis fait une certaine idée de la France" — "All my life, I have had a certain idea of France."
- **Main caution:** De Gaulle is the chief author of his own myth. The 18 June appeal, the "Paris liberated by itself" speech, and the Mémoires de guerre are masterpieces of retrospective narrative-shaping that historians (Rousso, Jackson) have partly dismantled. And the record has real stains kept off the monument: the Sétif repression of May 1945 under his provisional government, the 17 October 1961 massacre of Algerians in Paris under his presidency, the abandonment of the harkis, and the ambiguities of his path back to power in May 1958. Neither the marble statue nor a debunking counter-myth survives the sources; hold both.

  Use for: his own voice and self-conception — the "certaine idée de la France" opening; the account of June 1940; quotable throughout.  
  Notes: Literary self-mythologizing of the highest order; treat every self-serving claim as testimony, not fact. He died with only the first volume of the second memoir finished.

## Timeline Notes

- 1890, November 22: Born in Lille at his grandmother's house, rue Princesse, third of five children; raised in Paris. Father Henri de Gaulle, a professor of letters and history at a Jesuit college; family Catholic, monarchist-leaning, intensely patriotic, "of Parisian lawyers originally from Champagne" (Élysée). The child war-games as "France"; his brothers get lesser countries (family anecdote via Lacouture — label as such).
- 1905–1908: After France's anti-congregation laws, finishes secondary school with the Jesuits in Belgium; prep year at Collège Stanislas; admitted to Saint-Cyr 1908, ranked 119th. Nicknamed for his height (1.96 m) — "la grande asperge," the great asparagus.
- 1912: Posted as a young officer to the 33rd Infantry Regiment at Arras — commanded by Colonel Philippe Pétain. The mentor–protégé relationship that will end in mutual condemnation begins here.
- 1914–1916: Wounded three times; at Douaumont near Verdun (March 1916) he is bayoneted, left for dead, and captured. Five escape attempts from German fortress camps (Ingolstadt's Fort IX for incorrigibles — fellow inmate: future Soviet marshal Tukhachevsky, per the biographies); freed only by the Armistice. He called the captivity a uselessness that gnawed at him — the formative humiliation.
- 1919–1921: With the French military mission to Poland during the Soviet-Polish war. 1921: marries Yvonne Vendroux in Calais. Children: Philippe (1921), Élisabeth (1924), Anne (1928) — born with Down syndrome; his tenderness toward Anne is the great private counterpoint to the public glacier. She dies in 1948, age 20; he is later buried beside her.
- 1920s–30s: École de Guerre (1922) — brilliant, insubordinate, poorly graded. Books: *La Discorde chez l'ennemi* (1924), *Le Fil de l'épée* (1932, his philosophy of leadership and "the man of character"), *Vers l'armée de métier* (1934, the armored professional army France didn't build), *La France et son armée* (1938 — the book that ruptures with Pétain over credit). Breaks into politics' orbit via Paul Reynaud. Colonel, 1937, commanding the 507th tank regiment at Metz.
- 1940, May–June: Commands the improvised 4th Armored Division — real counterattacks at Montcornet (17 May) and Abbeville (27–30 May), among the few French offensive successes of the campaign. Promoted brigadier general (temporary) — Élysée dates the appointment 25 May, Fondation 1 June; use "at the start of June, aged 49, the youngest general in the army." June 5/6: Reynaud makes him under-secretary of state for national defense; missions to London; he urges fighting on from the empire. June 16: Reynaud falls; Pétain seeks armistice. June 17: de Gaulle flies to London on a British plane with almost nothing.
- 1940, June 18: The appeal, 10 pm BST on the BBC — four minutes, recorded at 6 pm, nearly cancelled by the British cabinet, not preserved. "Whatever happens, the flame of French resistance must not be extinguished and will not be extinguished." Few hear it live (see Controversies). June 22: second, recorded speech. June 28: Britain recognizes him as "leader of the Free French" (Élysée says 27 June — flag). August 2: a Vichy court-martial condemns him to death in absentia. August 3–5: the "À tous les Français" poster across London.
- 1940–1943: Building something from nothing — Chad and French Equatorial Africa rally (Governor Félix Éboué); the Dakar expedition fails humiliatingly (September 1940); Bir Hakeim (May–June 1942) gives Free France a battle honor. Jean Moulin, parachuted home, unifies the internal Resistance under the CNR (first meeting 27 May 1943, Paris; Moulin arrested and dies under torture weeks later). Constant war with Roosevelt, who distrusts him and backs rivals (Darlan, then Giraud — the forced Casablanca handshake, January 1943). June 3, 1943: French Committee of National Liberation in Algiers; de Gaulle outmaneuvers Giraud within months.
- 1944: June 3: CFLN becomes the Provisional Government (GPRF). Told of D-Day only on its eve; furious at Allied plans for AMGOT military government of France, which his Bayeux visit (14 June) helps make politically impossible. August 25: Leclerc's 2e DB (La Nueve leading) and the US 4th Infantry liberate Paris; von Choltitz surrenders; the Hôtel de Ville speech. August 26: the walk down the Champs-Élysées, shots fired at Notre-Dame as he stands unflinching. October 23: the Allies finally recognize the GPRF — a fact the Fondation notes with an exclamation mark.
- 1944–1946: Head of government — women's suffrage (ordinance April 1944, first vote 1945), social security, nationalizations; the épuration trials; he commutes Pétain's death sentence to life imprisonment. May 8, 1945: victory in Europe — and, the same day, the Sétif and Guelma repression in Algeria, thousands of Algerians killed under his provisional government (see Controversies). January 20, 1946: resigns abruptly over the parties' constitution-making — expecting, wrongly, to be recalled.
- 1946–1958: June 16, 1946: Bayeux speech — his constitutional blueprint (strong executive), rejected then, enacted in 1958. 1947: launches the RPF mass movement; early success, then decline; he shelves it from 1953. The "crossing of the desert" at Colombey-les-Deux-Églises: La Boisserie, solitaire, and the *Mémoires de guerre* ("All my life, I have had a certain idea of France").
- 1958, May 13 – June 1: Algiers insurrection; Committee of Public Safety (Massu; Salan calls for de Gaulle on 15 May); de Gaulle declares himself "ready to assume the powers of the Republic" (15 May) and at his 19 May press conference asks why, at 67, he would "begin a career as a dictator." Army seizes Corsica (24 May); "Operation Resurrection" stands ready to drop paratroops on Paris. President Coty calls on "the most illustrious of Frenchmen" (29 May); the National Assembly invests him legally, 1 June (329–224), with full powers for six months and a mandate to draft a constitution.
- 1958, June 4: Algiers, from the balcony of the Gouvernement général, to a delirious crowd of European settlers: "Je vous ai compris!" — "I have understood you." Two days later at Mostaganem, his only "Vive l'Algérie française!" Each side hears its own promise (see Controversies). September 28: constitution approved by referendum, 79.2% (Guinea alone votes no and becomes independent — Sékou Touré). December 21: elected president (indirect suffrage); takes office 8 January 1959.
- 1959–1962, Algeria endgame: self-determination offered (16 September 1959); settlers' "barricades week" (January 1960); January 1961 referendum, 75% yes to self-determination; April 1961 generals' putsch in Algiers (Challe, Salan, Jouhaud, Zeller) — de Gaulle, in uniform on television, "Françaises, Français, aidez-moi!", and the putsch collapses; OAS terror campaign begins. October 17, 1961: the Paris massacre of Algerian demonstrators under prefect Papon (see Controversies). February 8, 1962: nine dead at the Charonne métro station in an anti-OAS demonstration crushed by police. March 18, 1962: Évian accords (Fondation erroneously says 22 March); April referendum, 90.8% approval in France; Algerian independence July 1962. Aftermath: exodus of ~1 million pieds-noirs; thousands of harkis (pro-French Algerian auxiliaries) massacred in Algeria after being largely denied evacuation — a standing moral indictment.
- 1962, August 22: Petit-Clamart — OAS commando led by Bastien-Thiry machine-guns the presidential Citroën DS with Yvonne aboard; the car, tires shot out, drives through; ~140 rounds fired, nobody hit. Bastien-Thiry is the last man executed by firing squad in France (March 1963). De Gaulle uses the moment to propose direct popular election of the president — passed by referendum October 1962 (62.2%) via the constitutionally contested Article 11 route.
- 1958–1969, the Republic of grandeur: first French atomic bomb, "Gerboise bleue," Reggane, Sahara, 13 February 1960 (tests continue in the Sahara until 1966, after Algerian independence, under Évian side-agreements — contamination legacy still litigated); Élysée Treaty with Adenauer (22 January 1963); double veto of Britain's EEC entry (1963, 1967) — Britain as America's "Trojan horse"; recognition of Communist China (1964); withdrawal from NATO's integrated command (1966) while remaining in the Alliance; Phnom Penh speech against the Vietnam War (1966); "Vive le Québec libre!" from the Montreal city hall balcony (24 July 1967) — diplomatic firestorm; November 1967 press conference calling the Jewish people "an elite people, sure of itself and domineering" (see Controversies). December 1965: forced into a runoff by Mitterrand in the first direct presidential election, winning the second round with about 55% (Fondation gives 54.8% — check against official returns).
- 1968, May: student revolt, barricades, then a general strike of roughly ten million. His 24 May broadcast falls flat; the Grenelle accords don't end the strike. May 29: he vanishes — secretly flying to General Massu at Baden-Baden (see Controversies). May 30: back in Paris, a four-minute radio speech of pure defiance — he stays, dissolves the Assembly; a huge Gaullist counter-demonstration fills the Champs-Élysées; June elections give his party a landslide. But, as the Fondation itself concedes, "the relations between de Gaulle and the French people would never be the same."
- 1969, April 27–28: loses the referendum on Senate reform and regionalization, 52.4% no — a plebiscite on himself, as he had framed all of them. At ten past midnight a communiqué from Colombey: "I am ceasing to exercise my functions as President of the Republic. This decision takes effect today at noon." No speech, no return.
- 1969–1970: Colombey; writes *Mémoires d'espoir* (one volume done); trip to Ireland, visit to Franco's Spain (June 1970 — raised eyebrows). November 9, 1970: dies at La Boisserie of a ruptured aneurysm, at the card table, thirteen days short of 80. Pompidou to the nation: "General de Gaulle is dead. France is a widow."
- 1970, November 12: Per his 1952 testament — no state funeral, no presidents or ministers at the grave. At Colombey his coffin is carried on a light armored vehicle to the village church, buried beside Anne under a plain slab: "Charles de Gaulle 1890–1970." The same day, dozens of heads of state attend a memorial at Notre-Dame — the two funerals he had, in effect, scripted.

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- The BBC studio, 18 June 1940, about 10 pm: a one-star general nobody has heard of, condemned to death within weeks, tells France the war is not over — into a microphone with almost no listeners, no photograph, and no recording. The most famous speech in modern French history exists mainly as an act of faith, reconstructed later. (The famous microphone photo is from a later broadcast; even that is documented.)
- August 26, 1944: shots ring out inside Notre-Dame during the thanksgiving service; the crowd dives; the two-meter general stands motionless in the open. The previous day: "Paris outraged... but Paris liberated!" — a sentence doing the work of an army.
- August 22, 1962, Petit-Clamart: ~140 bullets into the DS at dusk, Yvonne in the back seat; the car speeds through on shot tires. From attempted murder he extracts a constitutional revolution (direct election of presidents) within two months.
- May 29, 1968: the President of the Republic disappears. Ministers can't find him. He is in a helicopter to a French army base in Germany — and to this day historians argue whether it was collapse or theater.
- The double funeral, 12 November 1970: kings and presidents at Notre-Dame; the man himself in a village churchyard beside his disabled daughter, under a stone with nothing but his name and dates.

### World Context

- A childhood in the Republic of revanche: Alsace-Lorraine lost, the Dreyfus Affair splitting Catholic-military France (his father, unusually for their milieu, thought Dreyfus innocent — good nuance via biographies), the Church-state separation that pushed his schooling to Belgium.
- Verdun's meat-grinder and the prisoner's war — then an interwar France that built the Maginot Line while ignoring his tank heresies.
- 1940: the fall of France in six weeks, Vichy's legal self-abolition of the Republic (the 10 July 1940 full-powers vote), and the question that defines him: where does legitimacy live when legality capitulates?
- The Big Three's war: Free France as a client insisting it was a great power; Roosevelt's contempt, Churchill's stormy patronage.
- Decolonization's hurricane: Indochina lost (1954, before his return), Suez, then Algeria — constitutionally part of France, one million European settlers, an eight-year war with torture on one side and terror on both.
- The Cold War and the Trente Glorieuses: a modernizing, booming France buying its independence with nuclear weapons and de Gaulle's theatrical diplomacy between the blocs.
- 1968: the worldwide youth revolt hitting the oldest head of government in the West.

### Key People

- Philippe Pétain: colonel-mentor at Arras, godfather-figure of his career, then the "false France" he replaced — the life's great tragic symmetry. De Gaulle commuted his death sentence in 1945.
- Yvonne de Gaulle: wife of 49 years, intensely private, beside him at Petit-Clamart.
- Anne de Gaulle: the daughter with Down syndrome for whom the public statue melted in private; he reportedly said she helped him surmount all failures ("Without Anne, perhaps I would never have done what I did" — attributed via family/biographies; label as reported). He is buried next to her.
- Winston Churchill: the patron who made 18 June possible and the ally he fought hardest. (The "heaviest cross I have to bear is the Cross of Lorraine" quip is usually credited to Churchill but was reportedly coined by General Spears — verify or attribute loosely.)
- Franklin Roosevelt: the adversary-ally who tried repeatedly to sideline him; the AMGOT plan for occupied-France-style government that de Gaulle's popular reception buried.
- Jean Moulin: his delegate who unified the Resistance and died under Klaus Barbie's torture — the sacrificial partner in the legitimacy-building enterprise.
- Philippe Leclerc: the Free French general whose 2e DB took Paris.
- Jacques Massu: paratroop general, 1958 kingmaker in Algiers, and the man de Gaulle flew to at Baden-Baden in 1968 ("Où êtes-vous, Massu?").
- Raoul Salan: called for his return in 1958, then led the 1961 putsch and the OAS against him — the settler betrayal arc personified.
- Michel Debré and Georges Pompidou: the constitution's drafter-PM and the successor whose competence during May '68 both saved and eclipsed him.
- André Malraux: culture minister and mythographer ("the Republic's wedding to grandeur").
- François Mitterrand: the persistent adversary who named the system "the permanent coup d'état" — then used it for fourteen years.
- Maurice Papon: Vichy functionary retained by the Republic, Paris police prefect on 17 October 1961, convicted in 1998 for crimes against humanity (for his Vichy record) — the massacre's face.
- Jean Bastien-Thiry: the would-be assassin of Petit-Clamart, last man executed by firing squad in France.

### Conflicts

- Legitimacy vs. legality: 1940 (a rebel against the legal government, condemned to death, claiming to *be* France) and 1958 (a legal investiture delivered under the shadow of an army coup) — the same paradox, twice, and he knew it: hence his obsession with referendums as ratification, and his resignation the one time the people said no.
- The soldier against the army: the tank prophet ignored by his own general staff; the president nearly overthrown by his own generals in 1961.
- Grandeur vs. means: a middle-rank power run as if it were a great one — nuclear force, NATO exit, vetoes — with the standing question of whether it was vision or vanity.
- Algeria's impossible triangle: settlers, army, and nine million Algerians — "Je vous ai compris" as the sentence that let him betray, or transcend, whoever needed betraying.
- The public glacier vs. the private man: the aloof "man of character" of *Le Fil de l'épée*, performing distance as doctrine — and the father of Anne.
- Memory vs. history: the resistance myth he built ("Paris liberated by itself") against Sétif, 17 October 1961, the harkis, and the whitened parade.

### Character and Motivation

- Self-cast from youth as the man of destiny; *Le Fil de l'épée* (1932) is practically a manual for the leader he intended to become — prestige through distance, "the man of character" embracing solitude.
- The wound of 1914–18: heroism cut short by capture; five failed escapes; the dread of being useless. 1940 is the escape that finally succeeded.
- Language as his weapon: like Churchill, he governed by phrase — "une certaine idée de la France," "la flamme de la résistance," "Je vous ai compris," "la chienlit," "Vive le Québec libre." He wrote every major speech himself and memorized television addresses.
- Ingratitude as policy: he theorized that France's interest excused any personal or allied grievance — hard on Churchill, brutal to supporters (the settlers of 1958, the RPF barons), indifferent to being liked.
- Catholic, fatalistic, and personally austere: paid for his family's meals at the Élysée; refused honors; the testament refusing all posthumous decorations and state funerals.
- Depressive undertow ("melancholy of great men") noted by biographers, surfacing at Baden-Baden and after 1969.

### Climax Candidates

- 18 June 1940 as founding act (but it works better as hook than climax, precisely because so little happened that day).
- 25–26 August 1944: the liberation speech and the Champs-Élysées walk — legitimacy redeemed in the flesh, with its mythmaking fully visible.
- April 1961: the putsch broadcast — the old general in his 1940 uniform, on the new medium of television, breaking a military coup with a speech; conscript soldiers listening on transistor radios refuse their generals. Arguably the purest demonstration of his singular weapon (legitimacy + voice).
- May 29–30, 1968 (recommended for a late climax): the disappearance and the four-minute comeback speech — the last performance of the 1940 trick, and the one that revealed its age.
- April 1969: losing a referendum he didn't need to call, and resigning at midnight — the anti-climax that proves the democratic point of the whole life.

### Legacy

- The Fifth Republic itself — his constitution, still in force, direct presidential election and all; every French president since inhabits an office cut to his measure.
- Gaullism as a permanent pole of French politics (national independence, strong state, sovereignty), claimed today across the spectrum — including by heirs of the far right that fought him and, remarkably, by the left that once called him a putschist.
- The infrastructure of memory: Paris's airport, the aircraft carrier, the Ordre de la Libération, the Mémorial at Colombey (1 million+ visitors since 2008), the cross of Lorraine on the hill; regularly voted the greatest Frenchman in polls.
- The historiographic correction: Rousso's "Vichy syndrome" named the resistance myth de Gaulle fostered; Jackson's 2018 life consolidated a warts-and-all portrait; official France has slowly acknowledged what the myth buried — Hollande on 17 October 1961 (2012), Macron's "inexcusable crimes" (2021), harki recognition laws (2022 reparations law under Macron — confirm details).
- Abroad: the model and vocabulary for "sovereignist" politics everywhere; in Algeria and among harki and pied-noir descendants, a far darker memory.

## Controversies and Debates

- **The 18 June appeal's real audience:** National memory made it the day France rose; historians (Jackson, and the trail in the Appeal article) show almost nobody heard it live — the BBC didn't record it, most French people weren't listening to London radio mid-exodus, and "most accounts of having heard it are false memories." Of ~100,000 French soldiers in Britain, ~7,000 joined; ~300 of 10,000 civilians. The 22 June recorded speech and the August poster are chronically confused with it. Present the appeal as a founding act whose power was retrospective — which is itself the more interesting story.
- **"Paris liberated by itself" (25 August 1944):** The Hôtel de Ville speech credits Paris's people and "the armies of France" while pointedly omitting the Allies (and Vichy as anything but a nullity) — deliberate state-building rhetoric, defensible as such, but factually tendentious: the US 4th Infantry fought for the city and Eisenhower had to be persuaded to divert to Paris at all. Compounding it: the 26 August parade was led by the 2e DB partly *because* it was majority-white, with non-white soldiers (about a quarter of its strength) swapped out at Allied command's insistence per 2009 BBC-reported documents — and the Spanish Republicans of La Nueve, first into Paris, went unacknowledged for 60 years. Attribute the "whitening" decision carefully (Allied High Command demand, French compliance).
- **Sétif and Guelma, 8 May 1945:** On VE Day, Algerian nationalist demonstrations turned violent (about 100 Europeans killed); the repression by army, militias, and air power killed thousands of Algerians (estimates range from ~1,500 official to 20,000–30,000 claimed; scholarly middle estimates in the thousands to ~10,000+). De Gaulle headed the provisional government. His personal role in ordering or condoning the scale is debated and under-documented; the event is absent from every Gaulliste institutional source. Do not omit; do not overclaim his direct hand — say "under his provisional government."
- **The May 1958 return — rescue of the Republic or velvet coup?** The forms were legal: Coty's invitation, Assembly investiture (329–224), a referendum. The context was force: an army insurrection demanding him, Corsica seized, Operation Resurrection ready to drop paratroopers on Paris if he were refused. Scholars conclude the threat of force was the decisive immediate factor; de Gaulle neither ordered the coup preparations nor entirely discouraged the plotters' hopes, insisting only on legal investiture ("Why would I begin a career as a dictator at 67?"). Mitterrand's "permanent coup d'état" charge and the Gaullist "legitimate rescue" narrative are both live in the literature; give both.
- **"Je vous ai compris" (4 June 1958):** The settlers heard a pledge for French Algeria; the words promise nothing. Historians debate whether the ambiguity was a conscious strategy of deception from the start (he moved to self-determination within 16 months) or genuine open-endedness that hardened as options closed. His Mostaganem "Vive l'Algérie française" (6 June 1958) is the strongest card for the betrayal reading. Preserve the ambiguity — it is the point.
- **17 October 1961 — a massacre under his presidency:** Police under Papon attacked ~30,000 peaceful Algerian demonstrators protesting a discriminatory curfew; men were beaten, shot, and thrown bound into the Seine; bodies surfaced for weeks. The state denied it for 37 years, then admitted ~40 dead (1998); House & MacMaster and Einaudi argue 200–300 (Brunet ~50). De Gaulle's personal knowledge and reaction remain partly opaque — a Papon report addressed to him was missing from the 1998 archive review; some accounts have him privately calling the acts "inadmissible," yet Papon stayed in office for years and no one was punished. Say plainly: it happened under his government, on his prefect's orders, with impunity — and that his own responsibility is debated between negligence, preoccupation (OAS war), and acquiescence. Pair with Charonne (8 February 1962, nine French communist demonstrators crushed at a métro entrance).
- **The harkis:** After Évian, de Gaulle's government restricted the evacuation of Muslim auxiliaries who had fought for France; thousands to tens of thousands (estimates vary wildly, roughly 10,000–150,000 claimed poles; scholarly estimates often tens of thousands) were killed in reprisals in Algeria, and those who reached France were interned in camps. De Gaulle's recorded remarks were coldly dismissive. Modern France has formally recognized the abandonment (2021–22). One of the least defensible episodes; handle with the ranges explicit.
- **November 1967 — "an elite people, sure of itself and domineering":** His press-conference characterization of the Jewish people, alongside the post-Six-Day-War arms embargo on Israel, drew accusations of antisemitism (Raymond Aron's famous rebuke) and delight from antisemites; defenders call it geopolitical realignment expressed with brutal condescension, not racial animus. Quote exactly and present both readings.
- **The Article 11 referendum of 1962:** Amending the constitution by direct referendum bypassed the Article 89 parliamentary route; the Council of State and most jurists called it unconstitutional; the Constitutional Council declined to review it. He won, and the precedent stands — the clearest case for the "plebiscitary" critique of his republic.
- **Baden-Baden, 29 May 1968:** Momentary abdication ("I withdrew because I no longer had a hold on anything," he reportedly told Massu) or staged vanishing to create the shock his return needed? Massu's, Pompidou's, and family accounts conflict; Jackson treats it as genuine wobble converted into theater. Keep it as an open question — it's better that way.
- **Myth-making as governance ("résistancialisme"):** Rousso's thesis — the postwar pretense that France had been a nation of resisters, with Vichy a parenthesis and collaboration marginal — was substantially de Gaulle's construction ("Vichy was null and void"). It rebuilt national self-respect and deferred the reckoning (Papon's late trial being emblematic). Frame the memoirs and anniversary liturgies accordingly.

## Drafting Handoff

- **Recommended hook:** The BBC studio, evening of 18 June 1940 — the unknown, freshly condemned general speaking to a country that isn't listening, in a broadcast nobody kept. Then the reveal that this near-nonevent became the founding date of modern France, and the question: how does a man make a claim like that stick?
- **Central arc:** Legitimacy as a self-fulfilling prophecy, redeemed democratically. Act I: the formed refusal (Lille to Verdun to the prison camps to the unread tank books). Act II: 1940–44, the claim ("I am France") made good at the Hôtel de Ville. Act III: exile, 1958's ambiguous restoration, Algeria's amputation, the monarchy-by-referendum. Act IV: 1968–69 — the old magic works once more, then fails, and he obeys the "no" and goes home to Colombey; the plain tombstone beside Anne as final image. Thread the counter-ledger (Sétif, 1961, harkis, the whitened parade) through Acts II–III rather than quarantining it at the end.
- **Use caution with:** every canonical quote (the "I have understood you" context; "the heaviest cross" quip is likely Spears's, not Churchill's; the Anne quotation is family-reported); date discrepancies among institutional sources (general's stars 25 May vs. 1 June 1940; Churchill recognition 27 vs. 28 June 1940; Évian 18 March, not the Fondation's 22 March; 1965 runoff share ~55%); death-toll ranges (17 October 1961: state ~40 vs. historians 200–300; Sétif: thousands, estimates diverging by an order of magnitude; harkis: say "thousands, with estimates ranging far higher"); Baden-Baden motive (unresolved); the claim Guderian studied his tank book (often repeated, weakly sourced — omit or hedge); anything from the Mémoires presented as fact.
- **Strongest source base:** the public record 1940–1969 — speeches (INA transcripts, Élysée texts), the institutional chronologies, and the well-documented demythologizing of 18 June and the liberation of Paris.
- **Weakest source base:** his interior life (he built the persona deliberately and the memoirs are literature); his personal knowledge of and reaction to 17 October 1961 (archives incomplete, key report missing); precise Sétif and harki casualty figures; this session could not consult Jackson or Lacouture at page level, nor reach any search engine (see Limitations).
