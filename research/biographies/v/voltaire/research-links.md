# Voltaire — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did François-Marie Arouet — a notary's son twice thrown into the Bastille and beaten in the street by an aristocrat's servants with no legal recourse — turn wit, money, and a printing press into a weapon that made one man on a farm at the Swiss border more feared by church and crown than any army, and what should that crusade against intolerance ("Écrasez l'infâme") weigh against the intolerance in his own pages?
- **Likely angle:** The revenge of the pen. The Rohan affair of 1726 — caned by lackeys while nobles watched, jailed for wanting to duel — taught Voltaire that in Old Regime France a commoner had no rights worth the name. Everything after (the English exile, the fortune deliberately amassed so no patron could silence him, Ferney "between France and Switzerland without depending on either," the Calas campaign) reads as the systematic construction of an independence from which he could fight back. He called it "the philosophy of action": "Rousseau writes for writing's sake... I write to act."
- **Main caution:** Voltaire is a double myth: republican France's secular saint of tolerance, and, in newer counter-memory, an antisemite, racial polygenist, and alleged profiteer of colonial slavery. Both files are real; neither cancels the other, and one key exhibit for the prosecution (a letter supposedly proving he invested in slave ships) is likely a forgery. Also: the single most famous "Voltaire quote" — "I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it" — is not by Voltaire at all. It was written by Evelyn Beatrice Hall in 1906. A bio that opens with it fails on page one.

## Timeline Notes

- 1694, 21 November: Born in Paris, youngest of five children of François Arouet, a well-off lawyer/minor treasury official, and Marie Marguerite Daumard. Baptized 22 November. (He later claimed to be born 20 February 1694, the illegitimate son of a nobleman named Rochebrune — self-mythology; treat the official date as standard and the claim as characteristic.) Family nickname "Zozo."
- 1704–1711: Brilliant pupil of the Jesuits at Collège Louis-le-Grand (Latin, theology, rhetoric). Father wants law; son wants literature; the fight defines his twenties.
- 1713: Secretary to the French ambassador at The Hague; scandalous affair with a Huguenot refugee ("Pimpette"); sent home.
- 1717–1718, 16 May–15 April: First Bastille imprisonment — 11 months, for satirical verses accusing the Regent of incest with his daughter. In prison he works on *Œdipe*.
- 1718: *Œdipe* triumphs at the Comédie-Française (opened mid-November); he adopts "de Voltaire" — anagram of AROVET L(e) I(eune), with overtones of *volter*, to turn quickly. The Regent and George I of Britain send medals.
- 1723: *La Ligue* (later *La Henriade*), epic of Henri IV as hero of toleration, printed clandestinely at Rouen and smuggled into Paris; survives smallpox that November.
- 1726, early: The hinge humiliation. The chevalier de Rohan-Chabot mocks the name change ("Monsieur de Voltaire? Monsieur Arouet?"); Voltaire retorts; days later Rohan's servants beat him in the street while Rohan watches. Society sides with the noble. Voltaire takes fencing lessons and demands a duel — and is put in the Bastille (17 April), without trial, at the family's request. Released 2 May on condition of exile; ships for England.
- 1726–1728: England (mostly Wandsworth and Covent Garden; merchant friend Everard Fawkener). Meets Pope, Gay, Swift; attends (perhaps) Newton's funeral; hears the falling-apple story from Newton's niece and later makes it famous. Absorbs constitutional monarchy, religious plurality (the Quakers), Shakespeare, and English prose. Cronk: he "came to England a poet and left it a prose writer."
- 1729: Back in France. At a dinner, mathematician La Condamine explains that a badly designed government lottery is paying out more in prizes than all tickets cost; their syndicate buys it out repeatedly. Voltaire's share: on the order of half a million to a million livres (figures vary — phrase loosely). Shrewd investment (annuities, army supplies, later loans to princes) makes him permanently, independently rich: no patron can ever starve him into silence.
- 1733–1734: *Letters Concerning the English Nation* (London 1733); French *Lettres philosophiques* (1734) — praise of English liberty read as indictment of France. Book condemned and publicly burned; arrest warrant; he flees to Cirey.
- 1734–1749: Cirey, château of Émilie, marquise du Châtelet — mathematician, Newton's future translator, married, 12 years his junior, his lover and intellectual partner for 15 years. They amass ~21,000 books, run physics experiments, analyze the Bible. *Elements of the Philosophy of Newton* (1738) helps win France for Newton over Descartes. 1745: royal historiographer; 1746: elected to the Académie française (after several failed attempts).
- 1749, September: Émilie dies days after childbirth. Voltaire, shattered, leaves for the court of Frederick the Great (mid-1750) — chamberlain, Order of Merit, 20,000 livres a year, rooms at Sanssouci.
- 1750–1753: The Prussian dream curdles: the Hirschel lawsuit (his own shady bond speculation), then the *Akakia* satire against Maupertuis, president of Frederick's Academy — Frederick has it burned. Voltaire leaves; at Frankfurt Frederick's agents detain him and his niece for weeks over a book of the king's poems. The two greatest celebrity correspondents of Europe never meet again — but the letters resume and continue to his death (700+ letters).
- 1753–1755: Banned from Paris by Louis XV (January 1754); wanders; buys "Les Délices" outside Geneva (1755). Lisbon earthquake (November 1755) shakes his residual optimism → *Poème sur le désastre de Lisbonne*.
- 1758–1759: Calvinist Geneva bans his beloved theatre; he buys Ferney (deed 9 Feb 1759, in the name of his niece and companion Madame Denis) just inside France, minutes from the border — "between France and Switzerland without depending on either." January–February 1759: publishes *Candide* — anonymous, instantly banned, instantly everywhere. "Il faut cultiver notre jardin."
- 1759–1778: The Patriarch of Ferney. Rebuilds the château; drains swamps, paves streets, builds a school, a fountain, a church inscribed DEO EREXIT VOLTAIRE ("Voltaire erected this to God" — to God alone, pointedly not to any saint); plants watchmaking, pottery, tanning, silk industries; village grows from ~200 to over 1,000. Hosts Boswell, Gibbon, Adam Smith, Casanova — "the Innkeeper of Europe." From 1760 signs letters "Écrasez l'infâme" (abbrev. "Écr. l'inf."): crush the infamous thing — fanaticism/persecuting superstition, not (as sometimes read) religion or Christ as such; that nuance is debated.
- 1762–1765, the Calas affair: Jean Calas, Huguenot cloth merchant of Toulouse, broken on the wheel 10 March 1762 for allegedly murdering his son Marc-Antoine to prevent conversion to Catholicism (the son almost certainly died by suicide). Voltaire, initially skeptical, investigates, interviews the family, and unleashes Europe: pamphlets, ghost-written memoranda, letters to every court, *Traité sur la tolérance* (1763). June 1764: the King's Council quashes the verdict; March 1765: Calas formally rehabilitated, the family compensated with royal money. The whole continent watches a writer overturn a parlement.
- 1764: *Dictionnaire philosophique portatif* — the pocket bomb (he denies authorship, as always). Sirven campaign begins (family condemned to death in absentia over a daughter's death; rehabilitated 1771 after nine years — "two hours to sentence... nine years to give them justice").
- 1766, 1 July: The chevalier de La Barre, a teenager of Abbeville convicted of blasphemy (mutilated crucifix, unremoved hat at a procession, irreligious songs), is tortured and beheaded, his body burned — with a copy of Voltaire's *Dictionnaire philosophique* nailed to the pyre. Voltaire campaigns for posthumous rehabilitation and fails in his lifetime (the Revolution rehabilitates La Barre in 1793). It frightens him (he briefly considers moving); it also fuels the last decade of "l'infâme" warfare. Also 1766–1778: 300-page defense of the executed general Lally — the reversal of that judgment reaches him on his deathbed.
- 1778, February: Returns to Paris after ~28 years, ostensibly for his tragedy *Irène*. Delirious reception — 300 visitors including Benjamin Franklin (whose grandson he blesses "God and liberty"); initiated a Freemason (Loge des Neuf Sœurs, 4 April); 30 March: at the Comédie-Française his bust is crowned with laurel on stage before the ecstatic house — the "apotheosis."
- 1778, 30 May: Dies in Paris, 83. Deathbed accounts are irreconcilable partisan literature: priests claimed recantation-then-agony; disciples claimed serene defiance ("This is no time to make new enemies," when asked to renounce Satan, is a later story — label it). His own February note is documented: "I die adoring God, loving my friends, not hating my enemies, and detesting superstition." Denied Christian burial in Paris; his nephew rushes the body to the Abbey of Scellières in Champagne and buries it hours ahead of the bishop's prohibition. Heart and brain removed and embalmed separately; the heart eventually rests at the Bibliothèque nationale (verify current display), the brain's trail is lost.
- 1791, 11 July: The Revolution brings him to the Panthéon — a vast procession (estimates around a million spectators), music by Grétry, the sarcophagus of the man the Old Regime would not bury. Rousseau, his great rival, follows in 1794. (The story that royalists stole and discarded both men's bones in 1814 was disproved when the tombs were opened in 1897 — the remains were there.)
- 1878: Ferney officially renamed Ferney-Voltaire. 1999: French state buys the château; now a Centre des monuments nationaux museum. His 6,000+-volume library, bought by Catherine the Great, sits intact in St Petersburg.

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- The Rohan beating, 1726: Europe's most celebrated young poet thrashed by a nobleman's servants in broad daylight — and it is the poet who goes to the Bastille. The injustice that manufactures the crusader.
- March 1762: an old man at Ferney hears of a Toulouse merchant broken on the wheel and starts asking questions everyone else considers closed — "Ignoring such a thing is to abandon humanity."
- The lottery dinner, 1729: a mathematician does arithmetic over dessert; Voltaire gets rich enough to say anything for the rest of his life. (Freedom of speech, purchased in cash.)
- 30 March 1778: the apotheosis — the dying man's bust crowned on the stage of the Comédie-Française while all Paris weeps; eleven weeks later the same city's church refuses him a grave.
- The Panthéon procession, 11 July 1791: the corpse smuggled out of Paris in 1778 returns with a million people in the streets.

### World Context

- Old Regime France: absolute monarchy, lettres de cachet (imprisonment without trial), censorship, the Bastille; a legal order where a Rohan cannot be sued by an Arouet.
- Post-Revocation persecution of Huguenots (Edict of Nantes revoked 1685): Protestant worship criminal, marriages void, pastors hanged — the substrate of Calas, Sirven, and the Toulouse parlement's fanaticism.
- The Republic of Letters and the print market: clandestine printing (Rouen, Amsterdam, Geneva), anonymity and denial as survival tools, the "little portable books at thirty sous" as revolution's medium.
- England 1726–28 as the counter-model: constitutional monarchy, Newton buried like a king, merchants honored, many religions coexisting.
- The Encyclopédie generation: Diderot, d'Alembert, and Voltaire as elder statesman of the philosophes; his campaigns invent the modern public intellectual (the direct line to Zola's "J'accuse" and Sartre — Cronk's framing).
- The Lisbon earthquake of 1755 as the century's theodicy crisis: the pivot from philosophical optimism to *Candide*.

### Key People

- **François Arouet** (father): the lawyer who wanted a lawyer; Voltaire's whole self-invention is a repudiation of the name.
- **Émilie, marquise du Châtelet**: the great partnership — physicist, translator of Newton's *Principia* (still the standard French version), his equal and anchor 1733–1749; her death in childbirth ends the only settled home of his middle life.
- **Madame Denis (Marie-Louise Mignot)**: his niece, secretly his lover (letters discovered 1957), mistress of Ferney, "the Innkeeper of Europe's" co-host; sold the château after his death.
- **Frederick the Great**: the philosopher-king dream and its shipwreck; 42 years of correspondence bracketing the Potsdam disaster and Frankfurt detention; after everything, mutual admiration ("a single man has united all their different merits in himself").
- **The chevalier de Rohan-Chabot**: the sneer that changed intellectual history.
- **Jean Calas and family**: the murdered-by-justice merchant whose name Voltaire made immortal; Madame Calas and the daughters forced into convents.
- **La Barre**: the executed teenager with Voltaire's dictionary burned on his pyre — proof the stakes were not rhetorical.
- **Jean-Jacques Rousseau**: the anti-self — feeling vs. wit, Geneva vs. Ferney; they despised each other in print and are buried facing each other in the Panthéon crypt.
- **Catherine the Great**: flattering imperial pen-pal; bought his library and installed Houdon's statue myth in Russia.
- **Evelyn Beatrice Hall**: the 1906 biographer whose paraphrase became "his" most famous sentence — a key legacy character, not a footnote.

### Conflicts

- Commoner vs. caste: the Rohan affair and everything downstream — a social order where wit and genius outrank nothing.
- Writer vs. censor: two Bastilles, an exile, decades of burned books, 175+ pseudonyms, lifelong strategic denial of authorship ("the actual author is a certain Demad").
- Deist vs. "l'infâme": he fought persecuting superstition while believing firmly in God ("If God did not exist, it would be necessary to invent him" — a rebuke to the atheists d'Holbach and company, not cynicism).
- Tolerance preacher vs. his own prejudices: the antisemitic articles of the *Dictionnaire philosophique*; the polygenist race passages; the correspondent who told Isaac de Pinto he was wrong to slander a whole nation — and then never fixed the text.
- Independence vs. complicity: fortune built partly on colonial-era finance (Compagnie des Indes-linked investments) even as *Candide*'s mutilated slave delivers the century's most famous anti-slavery line ("It is at this price that you eat sugar in Europe").
- Courtier vs. rebel: he wanted Versailles's and Potsdam's favor, got both, and detonated both.

### Character and Motivation

- Wit as reflex and weapon; the compulsion to answer every enemy (he never learned Churchillian silence — quarrels with Maupertuis, Rousseau, Fréron fill volumes).
- Self-invention: the made-up name, the possibly made-up noble father, the stage-managed image (Cronk: masks so relentless "the reader has finally no idea of where the 'real' Voltaire is") — his autobiography is his 20,000 letters.
- Money as armor: the lottery, speculation, lending to princes; he is the first great writer who never needed a patron, and he knew exactly what that bought.
- Hypochondria and stamina: perpetually "dying" for sixty years while out-working everyone (up to 50 cups of coffee a day, by Frederick's exaggerated count); working in bed, dictating.
- Action over system: no original philosophy, by design — "I write to act"; "Oh how I love this philosophy of action and goodwill" (1766). He aged from satirist into something like a one-man human-rights NGO.
- Genuine courage under real fear: after La Barre he knew a pyre could be his; he kept the border at his back and kept publishing.

### Climax Candidates

- The Calas campaign, 1762–65 (recommended): the mechanism of the whole life — fame, money, connections, pathos, and prose deployed to make a state confess an error. The rehabilitation of March 1765 is the Enlightenment's first great public-opinion victory.
- The 1778 return to Paris: apotheosis and death entwined — the crowning at the Comédie-Française, then the refused burial and midnight flight of the corpse; the Old Regime's simultaneous surrender and spite.
- The Panthéon, 1791, as posthumous climax: the Revolution claiming him as its prophet ("a splendid outburst," he had written of the coming upheaval in 1764 — which he never expected to look like this).

### Legacy

- The republican icon: defender of Calas, patron saint of laïcité and press freedom; invoked after every French blasphemy atrocity (notably post-Charlie Hebdo 2015, when the *Traité sur la tolérance* became a bestseller again — verify sales detail if used).
- The template of the engaged intellectual: Voltaire → Zola → Sartre (Cronk's line), and the modern "Voltairean" free-speech principle that is actually Hall's 1906 sentence.
- The counter-ledger since the late 20th century: Poliakov placed him at the head of a history of antisemitism; scholars document the racism and polygenism; activists tagged his Paris statue in 2020, the state quietly removed it, and only a replica returned in 2024 — a live, unresolved French argument about whether the Panthéon's first great man deserves the pedestal.
- The scholarly balance: Gay ("struck at the Jews to strike at Christianity") and Schwarzbach ("anti-biblical, not antisemitic") vs. Telushkin/Hertzberg (the hatred was personal and racial); on slavery, the *Candide* chapter and his praise of Montesquieu against "the promoters of slavery" vs. colonial investments and the polygenist texts — with the direct "slave-trade letter" likely a forgery. Present both columns; do not net them out.
- Physical afterlives: Ferney-Voltaire the town; the château museum (state-owned since 1999); the library intact in St Petersburg; Houdon's seated Voltaire; the Cabaret Voltaire lending his name to Dada, 1916.

## Controversies and Debates

- **The misattributed quote:** "I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it" = Evelyn Beatrice Hall, 1906, summarizing his stance in the Helvétius affair; she confirmed authorship in writing in 1939 ("my own expression"). The French version and the "1770 letter to abbé Le Riche" sourcing are both spurious (the real letter contains no such sentence). What is genuine: he did fight for the circulation of a book he scorned. The bio can use the quote only by attributing it to Hall.
- **Antisemitism:** ~30 of 118 articles in the *Dictionnaire philosophique* treat Jews negatively; ugly passages are numerous and quotable ("an ignorant and barbarous people..."). Defenses: Gay (anti-Christian strategy), Schwarzbach (anti-biblical, consistent with his anti-Christian rhetoric), personal-grievance readings (bankrupt financiers). Aggravating evidence: his reply to de Pinto conceded the injustice yet he never revised; Poliakov and Hertzberg trace influence into later French antisemitism (Renan). No side disputes that he also condemned persecution of Jews and never advocated violence. Present as: real, documented, contested in meaning, uncontested in text.
- **Race and slavery:** He was a polygenist (separate origins of races) and wrote passages ranking Africans below Europeans; historians dispute whether this primarily served his war on Genesis (Harvey) or reflected/served colonial investment interests (Giovannetti-Singh, Miller, Duchet). He invested in the Compagnie des Indes, which traded slaves; the oft-cited letter in which "Voltaire" congratulates a Nantes slave-trader on profits is considered a probable forgery (Reinhardt: "no satisfying source attests to the letter's existence"). Meanwhile *Candide* contains the era's most famous anti-slavery scene and he endorsed Montesquieu "against... the promoters of slavery." Verdict for the writer: indirect financial complicity likely, direct slave-ship investment unproven; the racist texts are genuine; so is the anti-slavery satire.
- **The deathbed:** Rival propaganda machines produced irreconcilable accounts (repentance vs. defiance vs. agony). Only the February note ("I die adoring God...") is safely documented; "no time to make new enemies" is beloved but unverifiable — label as legend if used.
- **"Écrasez l'infâme":** Scholars debate the referent — the Catholic Church, all revealed religion, or specifically persecuting fanaticism/superstition. Safest gloss: the apparatus of religious persecution and imposed superstition; he remained a theist.
- **Birth and paternity:** Official record 21 November 1694; his own claim of February 1694 and a noble biological father (Rochebrune) is unverifiable self-fashioning.
- **The lottery:** Sometimes told as "Voltaire rigged the lottery" — inaccurate; the syndicate legally exploited a design flaw (prize pool exceeding total ticket cost). Figures for his share vary (hundreds of thousands to ~1 million livres); phrase as "on the order of."
- **Wikipedia/institutional slips to avoid propagating:** en.Wikipedia's "tortured to death in 1763" for Calas (execution: 10 March 1762); Musée protestant's "Louis XIV" for the royal donations to the Calas family (Louis XV's court). La Barre's age appears as 19 (Voltaire Foundation) and 20/21 elsewhere — born 1745, executed 1766; say "a teenager" or "aged about twenty."
