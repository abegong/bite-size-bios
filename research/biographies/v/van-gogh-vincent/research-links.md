# Vincent van Gogh — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did a failed art dealer, failed teacher, and failed preacher become, in a single decade, one of the most influential painters who ever lived — and why did almost nobody notice until he was dead?
- **Likely angle:** Van Gogh's story is usually told as tortured genius. The stronger, better-sourced story is a working artist: a man who taught himself to paint through relentless, systematic labor, sustained almost entirely by his brother Theo's money and faith, and whose posthumous fame was deliberately built by his sister-in-law Jo van Gogh-Bonger.
- **Main caution:** Nearly every famous "fact" about Van Gogh — the ear, the madness, the suicide, "he never sold a painting" — is contested, simplified, or partly legend. Keep the myth and the evidence visibly separate. Do not retro-diagnose his illness; historians and doctors still disagree on what it was.

## Timeline Notes

- 1853, March 30: Vincent Willem van Gogh born in Groot-Zundert, North Brabant, Netherlands, eldest surviving child of Theodorus van Gogh, a Dutch Reformed minister, and Anna Cornelia Carbentus. (Born exactly one year after a stillborn brother also named Vincent.)
- 1857, May 1: Brother Theo born — the future art dealer, financier, and closest confidant.
- 1869: At 16, joins the art dealers Goupil & Cie in The Hague, a post arranged by his uncle "Cent."
- 1872: Correspondence with Theo begins — ultimately 903 surviving letters, 820 written by Vincent.
- 1873–1876: Goupil transfers him to London, then Paris; increasingly alienated from the art trade; dismissed in 1876.
- 1876–1879: Teaching and preaching attempts in England; fails the Amsterdam theology entrance exam (1878); missionary posting among coal miners in the Borinage, Belgium (1879), where he gives away his possessions and lives in a hut; church authorities dismiss him.
- 1880: The turn. At roughly 27, encouraged by Theo, he commits to art; walks long distances (including ~80 km to glimpse the painter Jules Breton's town, turning back without knocking); begins systematic self-training in drawing.
- 1881: Etten; rejected marriage proposal to his widowed cousin Kee Vos-Stricker ("No, nay, never"); rift with family.
- 1882–1883: The Hague; lessons from his cousin-in-law Anton Mauve until they quarrel; lives with Sien Hoornik, a pregnant seamstress and sometime prostitute, and her children — scandalizing family; leaves her in late 1883.
- 1883–1885: Nuenen, at his parents' parsonage. ~200 paintings in two years, dark earth tones: weavers, peasants. Father dies March 26, 1885. *The Potato Eaters* completed May 1885.
- 1885–1886: Antwerp; malnourished, ill; studies Rubens and buys Japanese ukiyo-e prints; brief academy enrollment ends badly (made to repeat, not expelled — a persistent myth).
- 1886, March: Moves in with Theo in Montmartre, Paris. Two years, ~200 paintings. Meets Toulouse-Lautrec, Émile Bernard, Signac, Seurat, Gauguin; palette transforms under Impressionism, Pointillism, and Japanese prints.
- 1888, February: Moves to Arles, Provence, dreaming of a "Studio of the South" artists' colony. Astonishing output: ~200 paintings in about 15 months — sunflowers, harvests, *The Night Café*, *Bedroom in Arles*, *Café Terrace at Night*, *Starry Night Over the Rhône*.
- 1888, May: Rents the Yellow House at 2 Place Lamartine.
- 1888, October 23: Gauguin arrives, funded by Theo. Nine weeks of productive, increasingly tense cohabitation.
- 1888, December 23: The crisis. After a quarrel with Gauguin, Van Gogh severs his left ear (nearly all of it, per Dr. Rey's later sketch) and delivers it to a young woman named Gabrielle at a local brothel. Hospitalized; Gauguin leaves for Paris; they never meet again.
- 1889, January–April: Recurring breakdowns; petition by Arles neighbors; paints *Self-Portrait with Bandaged Ear*.
- 1889, May 8: Voluntarily enters the asylum of Saint-Paul-de-Mausole at Saint-Rémy-de-Provence. In a year there paints ~150 canvases, including *The Starry Night* (June 1889), irises, olive groves, wheat fields.
- 1890, January–March: First real critical recognition — Albert Aurier publishes a glowing article; *The Red Vineyard* sells for 400 francs to painter Anna Boch at the Les XX exhibition in Brussels — the only painting known by name to be sold in his lifetime.
- 1890, May: Leaves the asylum, visits Theo in Paris, settles at the Auberge Ravoux in Auvers-sur-Oise under the informal care of Dr. Paul Gachet. ~70 paintings in ~70 days.
- 1890, July 27: Shot in the chest (consensus: self-inflicted, in a wheat field; a 2011 accidental-shooting theory is disputed). Dies July 29 with Theo at his bedside, aged 37.
- 1891, January 25: Theo, devastated and ill, dies six months later, aged 33.
- 1891–1925: Theo's widow, Jo van Gogh-Bonger, inherits hundreds of paintings and the letters; organizes exhibitions, sells strategically, and publishes the letters (major edition 1914), building Vincent's reputation.
- 1914: Theo's remains reburied beside Vincent at Auvers.
- 1973: Van Gogh Museum opens in Amsterdam.

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- Brussels, early 1890: Anna Boch pays 400 francs for *The Red Vineyard* — the only sale of his life reaching him months before his death, after a decade of labor and ~860 paintings.
- The Yellow House, December 23, 1888: the razor, the quarrel with Gauguin, the wrapped ear delivered to a frightened 19-year-old at the brothel door — told against what the myth gets wrong.
- A man walking: Van Gogh walking 80 km to Jules Breton's studio in Courrières in 1880 and turning back at the wall without knocking — the failed pilgrim weeks before he decides to become an artist himself.
- June 1889, Saint-Rémy: a patient in an asylum, painting the view from his barred window before sunrise — the picture (*The Starry Night*) that now anchors MoMA.

### World Context

- The Netherlands of his youth: provincial parsonage culture, Dutch Reformed piety, the family art-dealing network (three uncles in the trade).
- Paris in 1886–88: Impressionism cresting, Neo-Impressionism (Seurat, Signac) emerging, Japanese prints flooding Europe, Montmartre as the laboratory of modern art.
- The art economy: dealers like Goupil (and Theo at Boussod & Valadon) made careers; an unsold avant-garde painter could survive only on patronage — in Vincent's case, Theo's ~100+ francs a month.
- Mental illness in the 1880s: asylums like Saint-Paul-de-Mausole offered rest and confinement, not treatment; diagnosis was primitive, which is why his condition is still debated.

### Key People

- **Theo van Gogh** — younger brother, art dealer, financial and emotional lifeline; the letters to him are the spine of everything we know. Died six months after Vincent.
- **Jo van Gogh-Bonger** — Theo's widow; the architect of Vincent's posthumous fame; edited and published the letters.
- **Paul Gauguin** — the housemate whose nine weeks in Arles ended in catastrophe; lifelong sparring partner by mail afterward.
- **Anton Mauve** — established Hague School painter, cousin by marriage, first real teacher; the friendship broke over Sien.
- **Sien Hoornik** — the pregnant seamstress Van Gogh sheltered and nearly married; the episode cost him Mauve and deepened the family rift.
- **Émile Bernard, Toulouse-Lautrec, Signac, Seurat** — the Paris avant-garde circle that rewired his palette.
- **Dr. Félix Rey** — the young Arles physician who treated the ear (and whose 1930 sketch settled how much was cut).
- **Dr. Paul Gachet** — melancholic physician-collector at Auvers; subject of one of the most famous portraits ever painted.
- **Anna Boch** — Belgian painter who bought *The Red Vineyard*.
- **Albert Aurier** — critic whose January 1890 essay gave Van Gogh his first serious acclaim.

### Conflicts

- Vocation vs. failure: four failed careers (dealer, teacher, evangelist, and nearly, artist) before 27.
- Dependence vs. dignity: total financial dependence on Theo, which Vincent reframed as a partnership — Theo's money bought the paintings, which belonged to them both.
- Intensity vs. community: he longed for family, marriage, and an artists' brotherhood, and drove away nearly everyone — parents, Kee, Mauve, Gauguin.
- Art vs. market: his color and impasto were literally unsellable in the 1880s; he kept painting anyway, on theory and conviction.
- Illness vs. work: whatever his condition was (epilepsy, bipolar disorder, and other diagnoses have all been argued), he painted between attacks, not during them — lucidity, not madness, produced the work.

### Character and Motivation

- Ferociously literate — read Zola, Dickens, Hugo, the Bible; the letters show a deliberate, theorizing intelligence, not a naïf.
- Wanted art to console: to paint ordinary people and things "with that something of the eternal which the halo used to symbolize" (letter to Theo, September 1888 — verify exact wording at vangoghletters.org before quoting).
- Self-taught by system: drawing manuals (Bargue), perspective frames, color theory (complementary contrasts) — the "wild" style rested on method.
- Stubborn to the point of self-destruction: gave away his food in the Borinage, spent art money on models over meals in Antwerp.
- Saw himself as a link in a chain: expected recognition to come, if at all, after his death — and said so.

### Climax Candidates

- December 23, 1888 (the ear): the collapse of the Studio of the South dream — the standard choice, best told with Murphy's corrections.
- July 27, 1890 (the shooting): the wheat field, the staggering return to the inn, two days of calm dying with Theo present, "the sadness will last forever" (reported by Theo — flag as reported).
- Structural option: treat the *posthumous* rise — Jo's campaign, the 1914 letters — as the true climax: the story of a reputation built after death.

### Legacy

- ~2,100 works in ten years, ~860 oil paintings, most in the last two years.
- Direct ancestor of Fauvism and Expressionism; a founder of modern art's premise that color and mark carry emotion.
- The letters are a literary monument in their own right.
- Record prices (*Portrait of Dr. Gachet*, $82.5M in 1990) a century after he lived on Theo's allowance.
- The "tortured genius" myth: powerful, partly false, and itself part of the legacy — his illness interrupted the work; it did not create it.

## Controversies and Debates

- **What was his illness?** Contemporary doctors said epilepsy; later writers have argued bipolar disorder, temporal-lobe epilepsy aggravated by absinthe, lead poisoning, Ménière's disease, and more. No retrospective diagnosis commands consensus. Write "his illness" and describe symptoms, not labels.
- **The ear:** lobe or whole ear? Signac (who visited) said lobe; Dr. Rey's 1930 sketch, found by Bernadette Murphy in 2016, shows nearly the entire ear. Recipient long called "Rachel, a prostitute"; Murphy identifies her as Gabrielle, a young brothel employee. A minority theory (Kaufmann & Wildegans) holds Gauguin cut it with a sword; most scholars reject this.
- **Suicide or accident?** Consensus and contemporary evidence: self-inflicted gunshot. Naifeh & Smith (2011) proposed accidental shooting by teenager René Secrétan; Van Gogh Museum experts respect the biography but reject the theory. No gun or bullet was recovered at the time; the debate persists in the press.
- **"He never sold a painting":** false as stated. *The Red Vineyard* is the only painting known by name to be sold (400 francs, Anna Boch, 1890), and Theo placed or traded other works; he also earned some commissions (e.g., an uncle commissioned Hague drawings in 1882). Correct phrasing: he achieved almost no commercial success in his lifetime.
- **Gauguin's account:** much of what "everyone knows" about Arles comes from Gauguin's self-serving memoir *Avant et après* (1903), written 15 years later. Treat with care.

## Drafting Handoff

- **Recommended hook:** Open in Brussels/Arles, early 1890 — the 400-franc sale of *The Red Vineyard* reaching a man in an asylum who had painted ~860 canvases nobody wanted; then the question: how did this become the most beloved painter in the world within a generation of his death?
- **Central arc:** A man who failed at every conventional vocation converts religious intensity into artistic method, teaches himself to paint in ten years flat, is sustained by one believer (Theo), breaks under isolation and illness just as recognition arrives — and is then made immortal by his sister-in-law's editorial campaign. Frame: not a mad genius but a worker; the last section gives Jo van Gogh-Bonger her due.
- **Use caution with:** any diagnosis of his illness; the ear details (use Murphy); the death (state suicide as consensus, note the disputed accident theory); Gauguin-derived anecdotes; the "never sold a painting" line; deathbed quotes (reported by Theo, not verbatim records); the "80 km walk to Breton" (well-attested in a letter but verify framing).
- **Strongest source base:** the letters (vangoghletters.org) and Van Gogh Museum chronology; the ear incident (Murphy/Rey sketch).
- **Weakest source base:** exact motive on December 23, 1888; medical questions; some Nuenen anecdotes (assault accusation) rest on thin local records.
