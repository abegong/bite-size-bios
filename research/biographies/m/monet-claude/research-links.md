# Claude Monet — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did a broke, ridiculed painter — whose hasty harbor sketch was mocked into naming an entire movement — end his life half-blind in a garden he built himself, painting a room-sized monument to peace that France would not understand for another thirty years?
- **Likely angle:** Monet spent sixty years chasing the least paintable thing there is: the instant. Light on water, steam, fog, snow, a sunrise. The chase runs from the 1872 window at Le Havre (*Impression, Sunrise*) through the series paintings (grainstacks, cathedrals — the same object, different minutes) to the endgame at Giverny, where a bereaved old man with failing eyes offered France the *Water Lilies* the day after the Armistice of 1918 and then raced his own blindness to finish them. He won by dying: the Orangerie opened five months after his funeral.
- **Main caution:** Monet is his own chief mythographer. The starving-genius years, the origin of the *Impression* title, even his army service were retold and improved by Monet himself in late-life interviews. Meanwhile the private record is thinned in his favor — Camille's letters do not survive. Keep the art history solid and flag the autobiography as testimony, not fact.

## Timeline Notes

- 1840, November 14: Born in Paris (9th arrondissement), second son of Claude-Adolphe Monet and Louise-Justine Aubrée; baptized Oscar-Claude on 20 May 1841 at Notre-Dame-de-Lorette — his parents called him Oscar. (Later an atheist.)
- 1845: Family moves to Le Havre, where his father joins the family ship-chandling and grocery business. Monet grows up on the Normandy coast — the harbor he will later paint at sunrise.
- 1857, January: His mother, a singer and his ally in wanting an artist's life, dies; Monet is 16. His childless, well-off aunt Marie-Jeanne Lecadre becomes his protector and funder.
- c. 1856–58: A local celebrity at 15 for charcoal caricatures sold at 10–20 francs. Eugène Boudin, exhibiting in the same shop window, drags the reluctant teenager out to paint the coast in open air. Monet later: to Boudin "I owe everything."
- 1859: To Paris; Académie Suisse; café life at the Brasserie des Martyrs.
- 1861–62: Drawn for conscription (2 March 1861); rather than have his family buy a substitute (his own later accounts of why differ — a red flag for his self-mythologizing), he enlists in the 1st Chasseurs d'Afrique and serves in Algeria. Typhoid ends it; his aunt buys out the rest of his term; discharged 21 November 1862. He later said the African light "contained the germ of my future researches."
- 1862–64: Gleyre's Paris studio; meets Renoir, Sisley, and Bazille — the future core of Impressionism. Jongkind, met at Sainte-Adresse, is his other "true master."
- 1865: Salon debut — two seascapes, well received. Meets Camille Doncieux, 18, his model; she becomes his companion, the woman in *The Woman in the Green Dress* (Salon success, 1866) and *Women in the Garden* (rejected 1867).
- 1867, August 8: Camille bears their son Jean in Paris while Monet, cut off by his father over the liaison, is stashed at his aunt's in Sainte-Adresse. A stress-related eyesight scare briefly stops him working in sunlight — an eerie foreshadowing.
- 1868: Debts, seized canvases, an innkeeper's family to feed. In spring, at Bennecourt on the Seine, he throws himself into the river — reported by Monet himself to Bazille with unsettling breeziness ("I was so upset yesterday that I was stupid enough to throw myself into the water; fortunately no harm came of it"). Rescued economically by Le Havre patron Louis-Joachim Gaudibert. One seascape accepted at the 1868 Salon; then rejections until he stops submitting.
- 1870, June 28: Marries Camille. Franco-Prussian War: the family sits out the war in London (where he meets his future dealer Paul Durand-Ruel and studies Turner) and the Netherlands. Bazille is killed in action, 1870. Monet's father dies 1871.
- 1871–78, Argenteuil: the classic Impressionist decade — the Seine, sailboats, gardens, a floating studio boat; Renoir, Manet, Sisley, Caillebotte at his elbow.
- 1872, around November: From a window of the Hôtel de l'Amirauté, Le Havre, he paints the outer harbor at dawn in a few hours — cranes, smokestacks, mist, an orange sun added last. (The canvas is signed and later dated "72.")
- 1874, April 15–May 15: First exhibition of the Société Anonyme des artistes... in Nadar's former studio, 35 boulevard des Capucines — the artists' declaration of independence from the Salon. Asked for a catalogue title and reckoning the sketch "couldn't decently pass for a view of Le Havre," Monet says: call it *Impression*. Louis Leroy's satirical review "L'Exposition des impressionnistes" (*Le Charivari*, 25 April) sneers that wallpaper is more finished; days later the sympathetic critic Jules Castagnary uses "Impressionists" approvingly, and the name sticks. Attendance ~3,500. Monet prices *Impression, Sunrise* at 1,000 francs and fails to sell it.
- 1876–78: Camille falls seriously ill (uterine cancer diagnosed 1878, possibly aggravated by the birth of second son Michel, March 1878). Department-store magnate Ernest Hoschedé, a major patron, goes spectacularly bankrupt. In autumn 1878 the two families — Monet, Camille, two boys; Ernest, Alice Hoschedé, six children — pool households in the village of Vétheuil. Ernest drifts to Paris; Alice nurses the dying Camille.
- 1879, September 5: Camille dies at 32. Monet paints her on her deathbed — and later confesses to Clemenceau his horror at finding himself "systematically noting the colours" of his dead wife's face "according to an automatic reflex."
- 1878–83, the desperation years: begging letters to Manet, Zola, Duret, de Bellio, Charpentier for 50 or 100 francs; a landlady who won't renew; the icy winter of 1879–80 yielding the great ice-floe paintings. December 1881: Monet moves to Poissy *with Alice and her children* — a scandalous, ambiguous ménage while Ernest still lives. January 1883, painting at Étretat, his letters to Alice speak of wanting to die; on 21 February they reunite for good.
- 1883, April 29: Rents the house at Giverny, near Vernon; Alice and the eight children follow the next day. The 1880s bring solo campaigns — Bordighera, Belle-Île, Antibes — and slowly rising prices as Durand-Ruel opens the American market.
- 1889: Joint Monet–Rodin retrospective at Galerie Georges Petit seals his standing. He leads the public subscription that buys Manet's *Olympia* for France (1890) — the ex-outcast turned kingmaker.
- 1890: Buys the Giverny house outright. 1891: Ernest Hoschedé dies; 16 July 1892 Monet marries Alice (four days before her daughter Suzanne marries the American painter Theodore Butler).
- 1890–91: The Grainstacks/Haystacks — around 25 canvases of the same stacks through seasons and hours; fifteen shown at Durand-Ruel's in May 1891 sell out within days at record prices. The series method is born: "conceived as a decorative ensemble," the same motif as pure time-and-light. 1891–92: the Poplars (he pays to delay the felling of the trees mid-series — a detail worth verifying in Tucker). 1892–94: some thirty façades of Rouen Cathedral painted from rented shopfronts; twenty shown 1895 at 12,000–15,000 francs each.
- 1893: Buys the water meadow across the road and rail line, diverts an arm of the Epte to dig a lily pond over his neighbors' objections; Japanese bridge built 1895; pond enlarged 1901–02 and 1910; a paid staff of gardeners, one assigned to the lilies. The garden is a made thing — he plants his own subject.
- 1899–1904: London campaigns from the Savoy Hotel (Charing Cross, Waterloo Bridge, Houses of Parliament); Venice with Alice, 1908. 1900 and 1909: water-lily exhibitions — the 1909 "Séries de paysages d'eau" (48 canvases) is a triumph.
- 1911, May 19: Alice dies. 1914, February: his elder son Jean dies after long illness; Jean's widow — Alice's daughter Blanche Hoschedé-Monet, herself a painter — becomes the old man's housekeeper, companion, and painting guardian. Cataracts, first noticed around 1912, begin dimming and browning his world (1913: consults Liebreich in London; refuses surgery).
- 1914–18: At Clemenceau's urging he builds a huge third studio (finished 1916) and begins the *Grandes Décorations* — room-scale water lilies — while the front is close enough to hear. He refuses to flee Giverny: if the invaders come, he says, they will kill him among his canvases.
- 1918, November 12: The day after the Armistice, Monet offers France two large panels "as a symbol for peace" through Clemenceau; the gift grows into the whole cycle, formalized in April 1922, destined for the Orangerie's two purpose-built oval rooms (Monet works closely with architect Camille Lefèvre).
- 1922–25: The cataract endgame — right eye down to light-perception only; drops, then a two-stage operation in early 1923; rage at his surgeon, cyanopsia (a world stained blue), a black lens over the left eye, tinted Zeiss glasses; he destroys canvases whose "real" colors now appall him, retouches others, and by 1925, seeing 6/9 corrected, finishes the panels — but refuses to hand them over while he lives, retouching to the end.
- 1926, December 5: Dies at Giverny, 86, reportedly of lung cancer, Clemenceau at his bedside. He had insisted on simplicity: about fifty mourners. At the funeral Clemenceau pulls the black pall off the coffin — "No black for Monet!" — replacing it with a flowered cloth.
- 1927, May 17: Clemenceau inaugurates the Water Lilies rooms at the Orangerie — 8 compositions, 2 m high, 91 m of painting, lit from the sky, "the illusion of an endless whole, of a wave without horizon and without shore." Attendance is thin; the late Monet is out of fashion for decades.
- 1950s–60s: Abstract Expressionists rediscover the late work; Masson calls the Orangerie "the Sistine Chapel of Impressionism" (1952); MoMA buys a large *Water Lilies*. 1966: son Michel dies and leaves house and remaining canvases to the Institut de France — the Marmottan becomes the world's largest Monet collection; the restored Giverny house and gardens open to the public in 1980 and become a pilgrimage site. *Impression, Sunrise* itself was stolen from the Marmottan in 1985 and recovered in 1990 (verify details if used).

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- 12 November 1918: the morning after the Armistice, a 78-year-old half-blind painter writes to his friend the Prime Minister of France offering water lilies as a war memorial — no soldiers, no flags, just water and light.
- The hotel window at Le Havre, dawn, November 1872: a few hours' work, an orange sun dabbed on last, a title tossed off for a catalogue — and a movement gets its name from a sneer.
- Camille's deathbed, September 1879: the painter catching himself analyzing the colors of his dying wife's face "by automatic reflex" — the gift as a curse.
- Spring 1868: a failed painter steps into the Seine at Bennecourt — and swims. The next-day letter reporting it is almost a shrug.
- The old man in the round rooms that don't yet exist: Monet designing the Orangerie ovals he would never see finished, an infinity symbol on the axis of the sun.

### World Context

- Paris rebuilt by Haussmann; the Salon as the sole gatekeeper of careers; official art vs. the "refused." The 1874 exhibition as a startup — a joint-stock society of artists selling directly to the public.
- Trains, tourism, and paint in tubes: the technologies that made plein-air painting possible; Argenteuil as a suburb of sailboats and factory chimneys (Monet painted both).
- War as the punctuation of his life: 1870–71 (exile, Bazille dead), 1914–18 (painting at Giverny within earshot of the guns; the Water Lilies as a peace memorial).
- The market revolution: Durand-Ruel's gamble, American collectors, one-artist series shows — Monet as co-inventor of the modern art market as much as of a style.
- Japonisme: the prints Monet collected, the "Japanese" bridge, the flat mirror-world of the late pond paintings.

### Key People

- Camille Doncieux: model, mistress, wife, mother of Jean and Michel; the face of the 1860s–70s figure paintings; dead at 32; her letters do not survive — she is seen almost entirely through others.
- Alice Hoschedé: patron's wife, then Monet's companion in an arrangement Paris gossiped about, finally (1892) his wife; devout, jealous of Camille's memory even in death; ran the blended family of eight children.
- Ernest Hoschedé: the collector who bought Monet early, went bankrupt, lent his family, and died estranged — buried at Giverny.
- Blanche Hoschedé-Monet: stepdaughter and daughter-in-law, painter, the caretaker of his blind years — "the guardian angel of Giverny" (Clemenceau's phrase; verify wording).
- Eugène Boudin and Johan Jongkind: the two mentors who pulled him outdoors.
- Frédéric Bazille: best friend, financial lifeline, recipient of the suicide letter; killed in the war at 28.
- Paul Durand-Ruel: the dealer who nearly ruined himself backing the Impressionists and then made Monet rich via America.
- Georges Clemenceau: "the Tiger," wartime premier, doctor by training, Monet's bullying, devoted best friend of the last decades — extracted the donation, forced the surgery, hung the rooms, opened the museum, and tore the black cloth from the coffin.
- Louis Leroy and Jules Castagnary: the mocking critic and the sympathetic one — joint godfathers of the word "Impressionism."
- Gustave Geffroy: critic, friend, first biographer — and a source to handle with tongs.

### Conflicts

- The instant vs. the institution: a painter of fleeting effects who spent decades on permanent, monumental decoration — and built his own museum rooms.
- Poverty theater vs. real hardship: the begging letters are genuine and desperate, but Monet kept gardeners and a good table remarkably early; biographers note the performance in the pleading. Later he was among the wealthiest artists alive and haggled ruthlessly.
- The household triangle: living with his patron's wife while both spouses lived; propriety, faith (Alice's), and gossip vs. the practical tenderness of the arrangement.
- The eye vs. the brain: cataracts stealing exactly the faculty his art depended on; surgery refused out of terror, then endured, then half-regretted; canvases burned when he finally saw what he had painted.
- The gift withheld: France's greatest living painter promising the state a masterpiece and then refusing to deliver it for eight years, retouching, doubting, threatening to cancel — Clemenceau alternately coaxing and raging.

### Character and Motivation

- Monomaniacal about effects: series painting as obsession — dozens of canvases rotated as the light changed by the quarter hour; letters full of despair when weather betrayed him.
- A gardener as much as a painter: "I am good for nothing except painting and gardening" (attributed — verify wording); he diverted a river to make his subject.
- Volcanic and self-critical: repeatedly slashed, burned, or abandoned canvases (including, reportedly, on the eve of exhibitions); charming host, tyrannical about lunch at 11:30 sharp.
- The self-mythologizer: late-life interviews (Thiébault-Sisson and others) polished the legend — the title anecdote, the army story, the heroic poverty. His memory is a source; treat it as such.
- Grief processed through work: Camille's deathbed portrait; the willows and empty reflections of the war years painted after Alice's and Jean's deaths.

### Climax Candidates

- The Grandes Décorations race (recommended): 1914–1926 — bereavement, war, near-blindness, surgery, destruction of canvases, Clemenceau's friendship, and the decision to give France an ocean of peace he refused to surrender until death took it from his hands.
- The 1874 exhibition and the naming — the movement's big bang, with the delicious irony that the insult became the brand.
- The May 1891 Grainstacks sell-out: the moment the mocked painter conquered the market on his own terms.
- 17 May 1927, the Orangerie opening: triumph staged as anticlimax — the empty rooms, the out-of-fashion masterpiece, vindication deferred thirty years.

### Legacy

- The name itself: "Impressionism" — an entire epoch of art named, via mockery, after his painting.
- The Orangerie cycle as the hinge between Impressionism and postwar abstraction; the AbEx rediscovery in the 1950s; today the most-visited small museum experience in Paris.
- Giverny as a shrine: house and gardens (Fondation Claude Monet, opened 1980) drawing hundreds of thousands a year — an artwork you can walk through.
- The Marmottan (Michel Monet's 1966 bequest) and *Impression, Sunrise* as founding relic.
- Market apotheosis: a *Meules* canvas sold for $110.7 million in 2019 (verify current record before use); water lilies as both radical painting and gift-shop wallpaper — the bio can note the kitsch problem honestly.

## Controversies and Debates

- **Who really named Impressionism, and was 1874 a fiasco?** The legend: Leroy's savage review of *Impression, Sunrise* christened and crushed the group. The record: Leroy's *Charivari* piece (25 April 1874) was a comic dialogue mocking the whole show; Castagnary used "Impressionists" positively within days; the term drew on existing studio jargon ("impression" was already a painters' word — the Marmottan notice is explicit); reviews were mixed rather than uniformly hostile, attendance ~3,500, and the painting simply failed to sell at 1,000 francs. Recent 150th-anniversary scholarship (Orsay/NGA "Paris 1874") further deflates the fiasco myth. Monet's own title anecdote was told decades later. Present the naming as real but the martyrdom as embellished.
- **Date and even subject of *Impression, Sunrise*:** signed "Claude Monet," dated "72" only later; the Marmottan places it around November 1872 from the Hôtel de l'Amirauté; earlier scholars argued for 1873, and 19th-century viewers debated sunrise vs. sunset (astronomical reconstruction by Donald Olson supports a November 1872 dawn — verify before citing specifics).
- **The 1868 suicide attempt:** attested by a single primary source — Monet's own oddly offhand letter to Bazille (Wildenstein no. 25). French-language accounts add that he was a strong swimmer and unharmed. Biographers accept a genuine crisis (debts, seized paintings, an unmarriageable mistress and infant son) but differ on how lethal the intent was. Say "threw himself into the Seine" and let his own flippant sentence carry the ambiguity; do not diagnose.
- **The Camille/Alice arrangement:** From autumn 1878 Monet lived under one roof with his wife, his patron's wife, and both broods; Alice nursed Camille to her death, then stayed. Whether Monet and Alice were lovers before Camille died is unresolved (a persistent, unprovable rumor even makes Monet the father of Alice's youngest, Jean-Pierre Hoschedé, born 1877 — flag as speculation if mentioned at all). Camille's letters were destroyed — most biographers suspect Alice — so Camille is voiceless in the record. Ernest's role oscillated between complaisance and threats to reclaim his wife (the terror in Monet's 1883 Étretat letters). Handle with "the sources are one-sided" framing.
- **Poverty: how poor, how performed?** The 1870s begging letters are real, numerous, and abject; canvases were seized; the family did flit ahead of creditors. But scholars note Monet's spending habits (servants, gardens, wine) persisted through the "starving" years, and his later retellings sharpened the rags half of the rags-to-riches arc. Both truths fit: genuine precarity, theatrical pleading, and, from the 1890s, spectacular wealth he managed shrewdly.
- **Cataracts and the late style:** The clinical line (Ravin, Gruener): the hot, muddy palette and coarse handling of 1918–23 (the late *Japanese Bridge* canvases) track his lens pathology, and his post-operative destruction/retouching of those works shows he never intended them. The art-historical line: Monet had been simplifying and abstracting since the 1890s, worked increasingly from memory by design, and the "diagnosis" reading flattens a deliberate late manner. The bio should give the medical evidence its due (it is strong) while refusing the reduction — both the Orangerie panels and the "wild" canvases now prized at the Marmottan came out of the same afflicted years.
- **How many panels at the Orangerie?** Sources count differently: the museum describes 8 compositions totaling 91 m in two rooms; the cycle comprises 22 mural-scale canvas panels; Wikipedia's "27 panels displayed in May 1927" conflicts. Use the museum's numbers (8 compositions / two oval rooms) and avoid bare panel counts without a source.
- **Monet's own testimony:** the conscription story changed between his 1900 and 1920s tellings; the Boudin conversion, the title anecdote, and much of the standard color of the early years descend from late interviews with Geffroy, Thiébault-Sisson, and the Duc de Trévise. Prefer Wildenstein-documented facts; label reminiscences as such.

- **Recommended hook:** 12 November 1918 — the letter to Clemenceau the day after the Armistice, offering the water lilies to France as a monument to peace, written by a nearly blind widower who had already buried two wives and a son. - **Central arc:** A man who staked everything on seeing — chased the instant across sixty years, from the mocked harbor sketch to the sold-out grainstacks — is stripped, at the summit, of sight itself, and answers by building something the opposite of an instant: a permanent, encircling, horizonless room of water and light, delivered only by his death.
