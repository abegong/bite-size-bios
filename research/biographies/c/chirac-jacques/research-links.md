# Jacques Chirac — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did French politics' supreme opportunist — a man nicknamed "the bulldozer," accused of betraying every patron he ever had, and ultimately convicted of corruption — deliver two of the Fifth Republic's most morally consequential acts: the 1995 Vél d'Hiv speech admitting France's role in the Holocaust, and the 2003 refusal to join the Iraq war?
- **Likely angle:** Two Chiracs in one body. For forty years he treated ideas as vehicles and loyalty as optional (dumping Chaban-Delmas in 1974, sinking Giscard in 1981, being sunk in turn by Balladur in 1995), and ran Paris as a patronage machine. Yet weeks into his presidency, on 16 July 1995, he broke a fifty-year national taboo — "la France, ce jour-là, accomplissait l'irréparable" — and in 2003 he said no to Washington when saying yes was easier. The bio can ask which was the real man, and suggest the French answer: both, and they loved him anyway.
- **Main caution:** Chirac's death in 2019 produced a warm, nostalgic glow ("most admired political figure in France" in a 2010 poll) that can flatten the record: the fake-jobs conviction, the racially charged "bruit et l'odeur" speech of 1991, Françafrique friendships with dictators, serial betrayals, and a 12-year presidency historians find thin on domestic achievement. Conversely, do not reduce him to "the crook": the Vél d'Hiv and Iraq stands were real, deliberate, and consequential. Hold both.

## Timeline Notes

- 1932, Nov 29: Born Jacques René Chirac in the 5th arrondissement of Paris; only child (a sister died in infancy before his birth). Father Abel, a bank official who rose to aircraft-industry executive (adviser to Dassault founder Marcel Bloch); mother Marie-Louise Valette, doting to the point of pre-opening his candy wrappers. Grandparents were teachers from Sainte-Féréole in Corrèze — the rural roots he cultivated all his life.
- Schooling: Cours Hattemer, Lycée Carnot, Lycée Louis-le-Grand. Played rugby for Brive's youth team. At 16 sought a Sanskrit tutor and ended up nearly fluent in Russian. Briefly joined Communist circles, sold *L'Humanité*, signed the Soviet-backed Stockholm Appeal (1950) — later a problem for his first US visa.
- 1950: At 18, went to sea on a coal tramp steamer (Dunkirk–Algiers) intending to become a merchant-marine officer; his father retrieved him from the dock and enrolled him at Sciences Po.
- 1953: Harvard summer school; washed dishes and worked the counter at a Howard Johnson's; brief engagement to a Radcliffe student ended by her father's angry letter; worked at Anheuser-Busch in St. Louis.
- 1956, Mar 16: Marries Bernadette Chodron de Courcel (haute-bourgeoise, devout, iron-willed). Daughters Laurence (1958–2016; suffered from anorexia nervosa from her youth; died after a life out of public view) and Claude (b. 1962; later his communications chief and gatekeeper). The couple later took in Vietnamese refugee Anh Dao Traxel as a foster daughter.
- 1957–59: ENA. Cavalry officer training at Saumur; volunteered for Algeria — a passionate "Algérie française" man then — and called the war "the only time I had the feeling of command."
- 1962: Cour des Comptes; in April, joins PM Georges Pompidou's staff. Pompidou: "my bulldozer."
- 1965–67: Municipal councillor in Sainte-Féréole; 1967 elected deputy for Corrèze (Ussel) and made junior minister for employment at 34. In May 1968, a back-room negotiator of the Grenelle settlement (legend — treat as legend — has him carrying a gun to one meeting).
- 1968–74: State secretary for the economy; minister for relations with parliament; agriculture minister 1972–74 (fierce champion of French farmers); interior minister Feb–May 1974.
- 1974: Pompidou dies in office (Apr 2). Chirac leads 43 Gaullist deputies to back Giscard d'Estaing over the Gaullist candidate Chaban-Delmas — the first great betrayal. Giscard makes him PM (May 27) at 41.
- 1976, Aug: Resigns as PM, complaining Giscard denied him the means to govern. Dec 5: founds the RPR, a Gaullist machine in his own service.
- 1977, Mar 20: Wins the newly restored mayoralty of Paris against Giscard's candidate; holds City Hall 18 years — festivals, patronage, a huge staff, and the schemes that later convicted him.
- 1978, Dec: The "Appel de Cochin" — dictated from a hospital bed after a car crash — denounces the "parti de l'étranger" (the pro-European "foreign party"), his nationalist low point; he later swings to Europe's champion.
- 1981: First presidential run — 18%, eliminated; his conspicuously tepid support for Giscard in the runoff helps elect Mitterrand. Giscard never forgave him (and in his memoirs claimed an RPR staffer, phone-tested in a disguised voice, advised "certainly do not vote Giscard!").
- 1986–88: First "cohabitation" — PM under Mitterrand. Privatizations, abolition of the wealth tax; the Devaquet university bill withdrawn after student Malik Oussekine died at police hands (Dec 1986) — an event said to have marked him lastingly; Ouvéa cave assault in New Caledonia (May 1988, 19 Kanak separatists killed) days before the election. 1988: loses to Mitterrand, 46–54.
- 1988–95: "Crossing the desert." Challenged inside the RPR by Pasqua and Séguin; 19 June 1991: the Orléans "bruit et l'odeur" speech; 1992: campaigns for "yes" to Maastricht against much of his own party. 1993: the right wins; he installs his "friend of thirty years" Balladur as PM — who promptly runs for president against him, taking Sarkozy and much of the right.
- 1995: The great comeback. Campaigns against the "pensée unique" and on healing the "fracture sociale"; beats Balladur in round one (20.8%), Jospin in round two (52.6%); inaugurated May 17.
- 1995, June–Jan 1996: Resumes nuclear tests at Mururoa to worldwide outrage; ends testing "once and for all" (1 Feb 1996) and signs the CTBT; collects a mocking Ig Nobel Peace Prize.
- 1995, Jul 16: The Vél d'Hiv speech — first French head of state to acknowledge the French state's responsibility in the deportation of Jews, reversing the official position Mitterrand had restated as late as Sept 1994.
- 1995, Nov–Dec: Juppé's welfare/pension plan triggers the biggest strikes since 1968.
- 1996, Jan: Mitterrand dies; Chirac's televised tribute is remembered as generous and moving.
- 1997, Apr 21: Dissolves the National Assembly a year early to consolidate his majority — and loses it. Five years of cohabitation with PM Lionel Jospin (1997–2002), the longest ever; the presidency reduced to foreign policy and sniping. Universally rated his worst blunder.
- 2000: Presidential term cut from seven years to five by referendum. (Color: on 25 July 2000 the crashing Concorde passed within ~30 feet of Chirac's just-landed 747 at Roissy.)
- 2002, Apr 21–May 5: The shock — Le Pen (16.9%) edges Jospin (16.2%) into the runoff against Chirac (19.9%). Two weeks of mass demonstrations; "vote for the crook, not the fascist"; re-elected with 82.2%, the largest score in French presidential history — and, as he knew, not a personal mandate. Founds the UMP; Raffarin PM.
- 2002, Jul 14: Survives an assassination attempt at the Bastille Day parade (far-right gunman Maxime Brunerie, rifle in a guitar case, wrestled down by bystanders).
- 2003: Leads opposition to the Iraq invasion with Germany and Russia; threatens a UN Security Council veto; "Iraq today does not represent an immediate threat that justifies an immediate war" (18 Mar 2003); Villepin's 14 Feb 2003 UN speech draws rare applause. Franco-American relations crater ("freedom fries"); much of world opinion later judges him vindicated.
- 2004: Law banning conspicuous religious symbols in state schools; Chambon-sur-Lignon speech honoring rescuers of Jews.
- 2005: May 29 — voters reject the EU constitutional treaty he championed (his referendum, his defeat); Raffarin replaced by Villepin. Sept — a "vascular incident" (mild stroke). Oct–Nov — nationwide riots after two boys die fleeing police in Clichy-sous-Bois; state of emergency; interior minister Sarkozy builds his profile on it.
- 2006: CPE youth-jobs law withdrawn after huge protests (promulgated but never applied); Clearstream affair engulfs Villepin; Chirac denounces "a dictatorship of rumours" and denies the alleged secret Japanese bank account. Jan 2007: presides over the Panthéon ceremony for the Righteous of France.
- 2007: Mar 11 — announces he will not seek a third term; May 16 — hands over to Sarkozy, whom he privately loathed (memoirs, 2011: "irritable, rash, impetuous, disloyal, ungrateful"). Takes his seat on the Constitutional Council; moves into a Quai Voltaire duplex lent by the family of Rafik Hariri (itself an eyebrow-raiser).
- 2007–11: Presidential immunity gone, the Paris fake-jobs case revives: preliminary charge Nov 2007; ordered to stand trial Oct 2009; tried March 2011 in absentia (medical report: severe memory impairment); 15 Dec 2011 — convicted of diverting public funds, abuse of trust and illegal conflict of interest; two-year suspended sentence; no appeal. First former French head of state convicted since Pétain (1945). Notably, the prosecution had asked for acquittal.
- 2008: Launches the Fondation Chirac (conflict prevention, access to water and medicines, cultural diversity); it has since been folded into the Fondation OPALS.
- 2010s: Frail, increasingly absent; Bernadette confirms memory problems and the end of public appearances (2014); repeated hospitalizations. In 2012 he let it be known he would vote for Socialist François Hollande over Sarkozy — a last flick of the knife.
- 2016, June: The Musée du quai Branly, his museum of non-European arts (opened 2006), is renamed Musée du quai Branly – Jacques Chirac.
- 2019, Sept 26: Dies at home in Paris, aged 86. Sept 30: national day of mourning; requiem at Saint-Sulpice attended by roughly 69 past and present heads of state and government from ~175 delegations; nationwide minute of silence; buried at Montparnasse Cemetery beside his daughter Laurence. Lebanon and Andorra declare their own mourning days.

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- 16 July 1995, a blazing Sunday by the site of the vanished Vélodrome d'Hiver: a president elected two months earlier as a right-wing machine politician says the sentence no French head of state had ever said — that France itself, not some abstract "Vichy," delivered its Jews to their murderers. "La France, ce jour-là, accomplissait l'irréparable."
- 15 December 2011: a Paris courtroom convicts a former president of the Republic — the first since Pétain — and the defendant is not there; his memory, doctors say, is gone. The judge convicts him even though the prosecutor asked for acquittal.
- 5 May 2002: re-elected with 82.2% of the vote — the biggest landslide in French history — by millions holding their noses ("vote for the crook, not the fascist"), and he knows exactly what the number is worth.
- 1950: an 18-year-old shoveling-out on a coal tramp steamer between Dunkirk and Algiers, dreaming of the merchant marine, until his banker father appears on the dock and marches him back to the elite track he will ride to the Élysée.

### World Context

- The Fifth Republic after de Gaulle: Gaullism as an inheritance to be fought over; Chirac captured the brand (RPR, 1976) while shedding most of the doctrine.
- Mitterrand's long presidency (1981–95) invents "cohabitation," and Chirac is the first PM to live it (1986–88) — then suffers its longest version as president (1997–2002).
- The rise of the Front National from the mid-1980s reshapes the mainstream right: Chirac both panders ("bruit et l'odeur," 1991) and builds the republican dam (refusing FN alliances; the 2002 runoff).
- Vichy memory politics: the fifty-year official fiction that the Republic bore no responsibility (de Gaulle through Mitterrand), cracked by historians (Paxton, Rousso), the Klaus Barbie/Touvier/Papon trials, and finally broken by Chirac's 1995 speech.
- European integration's high tide and reflux: Maastricht (1992), the euro-convergence austerity that fueled the 1995 strikes, and the 2005 French "non" that halted the constitutional treaty — on his watch, at his urging, against him.
- Post-Cold War order: Chirac's "multipolar world" — friendships with Putin and Hu, the 2003 Iraq refusal, "freedom fries," and a France asserting rank as its relative power ebbed. Plus the unreformed shadow: Françafrique (Foccart recalled to the Élysée; tributes to Eyadéma; "multipartism is a kind of luxury," Abidjan 1990).

### Key People

- Georges Pompidou — the patron who invented him ("my bulldozer") and pushed him into electoral politics in Corrèze.
- Valéry Giscard d'Estaing — the president he served, resigned on, undermined, and (in Giscard's firm belief) destroyed in 1981; a lifelong vendetta of the right.
- François Mitterrand — the adversary who outplayed him in 1986–88 and beat him in 1988, yet whom Chirac eulogized with real grace in 1996; also the predecessor whose Vichy denial Chirac reversed.
- Bernadette Chirac — formidable, devout, publicly loyal through acknowledged infidelities; a politician in her own right in Corrèze.
- Claude Chirac — daughter, image-maker, closest adviser. Laurence Chirac — the private sorrow; her long illness is linked by many profiles to his quiet, unpublicized work for sick and disabled people.
- Alain Juppé — "the best among us," his PM 1995–97, convicted first (2004) in the fake-jobs affair; loyalty rewarded and costly.
- Édouard Balladur and Nicolas Sarkozy — the friends who betrayed him in 1995 (as he had betrayed others); his contempt for Sarkozy never healed.
- Dominique de Villepin — the aristocratic protégé whose UN anti-war speech carried Chirac's Iraq policy to the world.
- Lionel Jospin — the Socialist he beat in 1995, endured as PM for five years, and eliminated (via Le Pen) in 2002.
- Jean-Marie Le Pen — the far-right constant: courted rhetorically in the 1980s–90s, faced down in 2002.
- Jacques Foccart — the Françafrique fixer Chirac twice recalled; emblem of the Africa policy he never reformed.
- Marie-France Garaud and Pierre Juillet — the early svengalis behind the Appel de Cochin; Garaud's exit line, that they had thought him marble but he proved to be the faience of bidets, is one of French politics' great insults.

### Conflicts

- Opportunism vs. conviction: a man "of the same faience bidets are made of" to disillusioned allies, yet author of the Vél d'Hiv speech and the Iraq refusal — the bio's engine.
- The chameleon problem: Cochin nationalist (1978) → Maastricht European (1992); privatizing liberal (1986) → scourge of neoliberal "pensée unique" (1995); "bruit et l'odeur" (1991) → the anti-racist president of 1995–2007. Interpretations differ: unprincipled drift, or a politician whose only fixed points were France, power, and a genuine humanism about people.
- The republican dam vs. the pander: he refused FN alliances and won the 2002 anti-Le Pen plebiscite — but had spent the early 1990s borrowing FN themes.
- Warmth vs. the machine: authentic generosity (unpublicized help for the sick and disabled; the foster daughter; the "president who rides a Vespa" common touch) alongside 18 years of clientelist City Hall rule that ended in a criminal conviction.
- Ambition vs. governance: three decades scheming for the Élysée; twelve years there with, critics say, remarkably little structural reform to show — the 1997 dissolution and 2005 referendum as self-inflicted amputations.

### Character and Motivation

- Prodigious physical energy and appetite — rillettes sandwiches and beer, marathon handshaking at the Salon de l'Agriculture, the "bulldozer" work rate; a ferocious temper (the Thatcher outburst) usually sheathed in bonhomie.
- A cultivated secret self: the public "paysan de Corrèze" persona concealed a connoisseur of Asian, African and pre-Columbian art (the quai Branly museum is the monument to the hidden Chirac), a onetime student of Sanskrit and Russian who translated Pushkin in his youth (the translation claim is his own — flag it).
- Deliberate opacity about the inner life: "It is of little importance who he is in private... It is only the political personality that should interest us" (2000). His memoirs are polished performances; no confession exists.
- What he wanted: the presidency, plainly, for most of his life — and, once there, to be loved, which by the end, paradoxically and despite everything, he was ("most admired" in polls after leaving office, while his successor languished).

### Climax Candidates

- The Vél d'Hiv speech (recommended): the machine politician's moment of unforced moral clarity, two months into the office he had chased for twenty years.
- The 2002 runoff: 82.2% as both triumph and indictment — a nation using him as a shield, and his own concession (in memoirs) of responsibility for the disaster that made it necessary.
- 2003: the UN veto threat — a middle power saying no to a superpower, cheered abroad, vindicated in much later opinion, and costing France dearly in Washington.
- The 2011 conviction as dark coda: the absent old man, the destroyed memory, the first conviction of a French ex-head of state since Pétain — whose crime, collaboration, Chirac had been the first president to name.

### Legacy

- The affectionate verdict: within years of leaving office he was France's most admired political figure; his death brought a genuine national moment (day of mourning, Saint-Sulpice, ~69 world leaders). The French formula: a flawed man who "represented France well."
- The durable acts: the Vél d'Hiv speech permanently changed official French memory of Vichy (every successor has built on it); the Iraq refusal became the reference point for French foreign-policy independence; the quai Branly museum and the school religious-symbols law (contested) mark culture and laïcité.
- The stain: the 2011 conviction, and a broader historiographical judgment that his presidency underdelivered — "hard to point to major reforms" (Guardian); "the most unpopular occupant of the Élysée in the Fifth Republic's history" (The Economist, 2006) at his second-term nadir.
- Memory-shapers: his own memoirs and Claude Chirac's image machine; the Guignols de l'Info puppet ("Super Menteur") which weirdly humanized him; the association/museum in Sarran (Corrèze); the foundation (now merged into OPALS).

## Controversies and Debates

- **The Paris City Hall fake-jobs system and the 2011 conviction:** Established: as mayor (1977–95) his administration paid RPR activists for municipal jobs that did not exist; convicted 15 Dec 2011 (diverting public funds, abuse of trust, illegal conflict of interest), two years suspended; Juppé convicted 2004; investigations also covered lavish "frais de bouche," HLM favoritism, cheap water-concession deals, and a 25% payroll expansion. Contested: how personally culpable he was — the prosecution itself sought acquittal, no one alleged he enriched himself personally while in office (NYT), and defenders note the era's party-financing norms were rotten across the spectrum; critics (Guardian) call him "a willing and active participant in a system of corruption that attained heroic proportions." A 1999 ruling gave him immunity as president, which he used to refuse all testimony for eight years — itself controversial. Present the conviction as fact, the moral weight as debated.
- **"Le bruit et l'odeur" (Orléans, 19 June 1991):** The words and setting are well documented (Le Monde, 21 June 1991; INA footage): "overdose" of immigrants, the polygamous family on welfare, "the noise and the smell," "it is not racist to say this" — to applause and laughter. Debate: emblem of the respectable right legitimizing FN themes (the dominant reading; Zebda's song fixed it in memory) vs. defenders' claim he was describing, not endorsing, a worker's resentment, and that the same man refused FN alliances and gave the 1995 speech. Do not soften the quote; do give the FN-pressure context and his earlier multicultural statements.
- **The 1997 dissolution:** Universally judged his worst political blunder — dissolving a friendly Assembly a year early and handing government to Jospin for five years. Why he did it is still argued: to pre-empt euro-austerity backlash before the 1998 deadline, Villepin's urging, Juppé's unpopularity. A clean case study in hubris; no factual dispute, only motive.
- **The Vél d'Hiv speech itself:** Now consensual, it was contested then — orthodox Gaullists (Séguin among others) and Mitterrand's camp held that Vichy was not the Republic and France owed no apology. Also note a textual trap: the Élysée transcription says "450 policiers et gendarmes"; most reproductions say "4,500" (the roundup in fact mobilized several thousand police). Verify before quoting numbers.
- **Iraq, Saddam, and Osirak:** His 2003 stand is inseparable from a longer history: as PM in 1975 he hosted and visited Saddam Hussein ("my friend" era), and France sold Iraq the Osirak reactor (bombed by Israel in 1981) — used by American critics in 2003 to paint the veto threat as commercially motivated. The principled reading (no proven WMD, no UN mandate, prescient warnings about regional chaos) has largely prevailed, but present both.
- **Françafrique:** He recalled Foccart, kept the networks warm, paid tribute to dictator Eyadéma, backed dynastic successions, and in 1990 called multiparty democracy "a kind of luxury" for Africa — jarring against his quai Branly-era rhetoric of cultural respect and his 2005 condemnation of the 1947 Madagascar repression. Under-covered in English obituaries; needs a balancing source.
- **Le Pen's claim that Chirac once sought his support:** Asserted by Le Pen, called "not unconvincing" by the Guardian's obituarist, denied by chiraquiens. Report as a claim, not a fact.
- **Clearstream (2006):** Chirac was never charged; the affair was Villepin vs. Sarkozy. The rumored secret Japanese bank account (300M francs) was investigated and never substantiated; Chirac publicly denounced "the dictatorship of rumour." Keep it one line, clearly labeled unproven.
- **Mururoa 1995:** Fact: he resumed tests to global fury, then ended French testing permanently and signed the CTBT. Debate is over judgment, not facts; his own defense ("look back at 1935... people were against France arming itself") is quotable.

- **Central arc:** The bulldozer's education.
