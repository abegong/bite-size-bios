# Charlemagne — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did a Frankish warrior-king who could not write his own name — and who ordered 4,500 Saxon prisoners beheaded in a single day — come to be crowned emperor in Rome on Christmas Day 800, spark a renaissance of learning, and end up claimed twelve centuries later as "the father of Europe" by France, Germany, and the European Union all at once?
- **Likely angle:** The coronation of 800 as hinge: a moment nobody fully controlled (did Charlemagne plan it, or did Pope Leo III spring it on him, as Einhard claims?) that re-created the idea of empire in the West and set the terms for a thousand years of European politics. The counter-melody is Aachen: the real Charlemagne ruled from a hot-springs palace on the modern Franco-German border, swam better than any of his courtiers, kept writing tablets under his pillow, and never returned to Rome after 801.
- **Main caution:** Charlemagne is one of the most heavily mythologized figures in European history — over 1,000 medieval legends (Becher's count), the crusader-king of the *Song of Roland*, the disputed saint, Napoleon's model, the Nazis' problem-then-poster-child, and the EU's mascot. The genuine 8th-century evidence is thin, courtly, and partisan (Einhard wrote panegyric on a Suetonian template; the Royal Frankish Annals are court propaganda that initially hid Frankish defeats). Separate the attested from the legendary at every step. Also: this site's Paris framing needs honesty — Charlemagne's capital was Aachen, not Paris; Paris was a secondary city of his empire, and even the statue outside Notre-Dame was nearly rejected by the Paris council on the grounds that he had little to do with the city (see Controversies).

## Best Sources

- **Einhard — *The Life of Charlemagne* (Vita Karoli Magni, c. 817–830s), trans. Samuel Epes Turner (Harper & Brothers, 1880), full text at the Internet Medieval Sourcebook, Fordham University**  
  https://sourcebooks.fordham.edu/basis/einhard.asp  
  Type: primary  
  Use for: the foundational portrait — appearance ("large and strong... his height... seven times the length of his foot"), the swimming at Aachen's warm springs, the writing tablets kept under his pillow ("as he did not begin his efforts in due season, but late in life, they met with ill success"), the coronation of 800 and Einhard's famous claim that Charles "would not have set foot in the Church the day that they were conferred... if he could have foreseen the design of the Pope," the death scene (28 Jan 814, pleurisy, seven days abed), the epitaph text, and the Roncevaux entry naming "Roland, Governor of the March of Brittany."  
  Notes: Einhard was a court insider writing after Charlemagne's death, modeling the work on Suetonius' *Twelve Caesars* — admiring, selective, and openly ignorant of the early life ("It would be folly, I think, to write a word concerning Charles' birth and infancy... for nothing has ever been written on the subject"). His age-at-death figure (72) helped create the erroneous 742 birth year. Turner's 1880 translation is dated but serviceable. Accessed 2026-07-20.

- ***Royal Frankish Annals* (Annales regni Francorum, covering 741–829), trans. Bernhard Walter Scholz in *Carolingian Chronicles* (University of Michigan Press, 1970)**  
  Orientation and manuscript history: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Frankish_Annals (accessed 2026-07-20)  
  Type: primary  
  Use for: year-by-year chronology of the reign; the 782 entry recording that 4,500 Saxons were "beheaded [decollati] in one day upon the river Aller in the place called Verden"; the coronation account in which Leo prostrates himself before the new emperor (a Roman ritual of submission — the annals' counter-spin to papal versions).  
  Notes: produced by persons close to the king; early sections omit or soften Frankish defeats (Roncevaux appears honestly only in the later "revised" version). Exists in original and revised recensions with meaningfully different emphases — cite which version when it matters. Scholz's print translation is the standard English text.

## Timeline Notes

- c. 747–748: Born, probably 2 April 748 — the current scholarly consensus, following Karl Ferdinand Werner (1973) and Matthias Becher (1992), from an addition to the *Annales Petaviani* ("in that year King Charles was born" under 747, Easter-dated to 748) and Lorsch Abbey's commemoration of 2 April. The old date of 742 rests on Einhard's "seventy-second year," the annals' "about 71," and the epitaph's "septuagenarian" — all possibly echoing Psalm 90's threescore-and-ten. Birthplace unknown (Vaires-sur-Marne, Quierzy, and Herstal all proposed; nothing provable). Eldest son of Pepin the Short and Bertrada.
- 751: Pepin deposes the last Merovingian, Childeric III, and is made king — whether papal sanction preceded or followed the coup is disputed. 754: Pope Stephen II travels to Francia and anoints Pepin *and* his sons Charles and Carloman; the boy Charles is sent to escort the pope.
- 768, 24 September: Pepin dies; the kingdom is divided. 9 October: separate coronations — Charles at Noyon, Carloman at Soissons. The brothers' joint rule is tense (Carloman abandons the Aquitaine campaign; their mother brokers Charles's marriage to a Lombard princess, which he repudiates within a year).
- 771, 4 December: Carloman dies suddenly. Charlemagne seizes his brother's kingdom; Carloman's widow and sons flee to the Lombard court.
- 772: First Saxon campaign — destruction of the Irminsul, the Saxons' sacred pillar, at Eresburg. Beginning of more than 30 years of nearly continuous Saxon war (Einhard: no war "was carried on with such persistence and bitterness, or cost so much labor").
- 773–774: Siege of Pavia. Easter 774 in Rome — oaths with Pope Adrian I over St. Peter's relics. June 774: Lombard kingdom annexed; Charlemagne takes the title King of the Lombards — a takeover contemporaries found "extraordinary," "without parallel." His nephews, captured at Verona, vanish from the record (see Controversies).
- 778, 15 August: The Spanish expedition ends with the rearguard ambushed by Basques at Roncevaux Pass; among the dead, "Roland, Governor of the March of Brittany" (Einhard) — the seed of the *Song of Roland*, which three centuries later transforms a Basque ambush into a crusade against Saracens.
- 782, October: The Massacre of Verden — after Widukind's rebellion destroys a Frankish army at the Süntel, Charlemagne has 4,500 surrendered Saxon prisoners beheaded in one day on the river Aller. Issued around the same time, the *Capitulatio de partibus Saxoniae* imposes the death penalty for pagan practices — "a program for the forced conversion of the Saxons."
- 783: His wife Hildegard (mother of nine of his children, married at ~13) dies from childbirth complications on 30 April; his mother Bertrada dies 12 July. He remarries (Fastrada) within the year.
- 785: Widukind submits and is baptized, with Charlemagne as godfather — the war's symbolic turning point, though Saxon revolts recur to 804.
- 787–788: Bavaria absorbed; his cousin Duke Tassilo deposed and sent to a monastery. 791–796: Avar wars — the Avar Ring falls and its enormous treasure is carted to Aachen. 792: His eldest son Pepin "the Hunchback," sidelined from the succession, conspires against him and is confined to a monastery.
- 790s: Aachen becomes the fixed residence — palace, hot springs, and the Palatine Chapel ("the basilica of the Holy Mother of God at Aix-la-Chapelle, built in the most admirable manner" — Einhard). Court scholars gathered from everywhere: Alcuin of York, Theodulf of Orléans, Peter of Pisa, Paul the Deacon, Einhard. The Carolingian Renaissance in full swing: *Admonitio generalis* (789), the *Epistola de litteris colendis*, school reform, standardized Latin, and Carolingian minuscule — the script behind modern lowercase type; ~90,000 manuscripts copied in the 9th century.
- 799, April: Pope Leo III is ambushed in Rome by enemies who try to blind him and cut out his tongue; he escapes and flees north to Charlemagne at Paderborn — the pope as suppliant at the Frankish court (the scene of the Paderborn Epic).
- 800, 23–25 December: In Rome, Leo purges himself by oath; at Christmas Mass in Old St. Peter's, he crowns Charlemagne and the Romans acclaim him *Imperator Romanorum* — the first emperor in the West since 476. Einhard claims Charles would not have entered the church had he known (see Controversies). Charlemagne never adopts the title "Emperor of the Romans," styling himself instead "emperor governing the Roman empire... king of the Franks and the Lombards" — a formula calibrated not to enrage Constantinople.
- 801–813: The imperial phase — sedentary rule from Aachen (he leaves Italy in summer 801 and never returns to Rome); the 802 general oath of loyalty and reformed *missi dominici*; law-code revisions; diplomacy with Harun al-Rashid, whose gift elephant Abul-Abbas reaches Aachen in 802 ("he sent the only one that he had" — Einhard); Byzantine recognition of the imperial title finally in 812.
- 806: The *Divisio Regnorum* divides the realm among three sons — and pointedly says nothing about who inherits the imperial title.
- 810–811: The dynasty collapses inward — sons Pepin of Italy and Charles the Younger both die (with Pepin the Hunchback, and a sister and daughter besides).
- 813, 11 September: At Aachen, Charlemagne crowns his sole surviving son Louis co-emperor with his own hands — no pope involved. A statement, whether about 800 or just about practicality, that drafters should present with both readings.
- 814, 28 January: Dies at Aachen of pleurisy after seven days of illness (self-prescribed fasting included), "in the forty-seventh year of his reign"; buried the same day in the Palatine Chapel. His epitaph calls him "the Great and Orthodox Emperor" — and, misleadingly, a man who "died at the age of seventy."
- Afterlives: 843 — the Treaty of Verdun splits the empire among his grandsons into West Francia (→ France), East Francia (→ Germany), and a contested middle strip; the Franco-German rivalry is, in caricature, his estate in probate. 936–1531 — German kings crowned in his chapel at Aachen. 1000 — Otto III opens the tomb. 1165 — Frederick Barbarossa has him canonized by the antipope Paschal III (never recognized by Rome; venerated locally anyway, and in France from Charles V onward — French schoolchildren long kept the Saint-Charlemagne as their patronal feast, 28 January). 1215 — reinterred by Frederick II in the golden Karlsschrein, where he still lies. c. 1100 — the *Song of Roland*. 1804–06 — Napoleon poses as his successor while dissolving the Holy Roman Empire Charlemagne founded. 1878–1882 — the Rochet brothers' equestrian bronze lands on the parvis of Notre-Dame over the objections of the Paris council. 1935 — Nazi Sachsenhain memorial to the Verden victims casts him as "butcher of the Saxons"; 1942 — Hitler reverses course and celebrates his 1200th birthday as Germanic unifier. 1950 — first Karlspreis awarded at Aachen for services to European unity (laureates from Coudenhove-Kalergi and Churchill to, in 2026, Mario Draghi).

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- Christmas Day 800, Old St. Peter's: the king of the Franks rises from prayer, the pope sets a crown on his head, the Roman crowd acclaims him emperor — and his own biographer insists he would never have walked in had he known. Whichever way the surprise question is resolved, the scene carries the whole story.
- The tablets under the pillow: the most powerful man in the West, ruler of an empire, secretly practicing his letters at night "late in life" — and failing. The empire of the mind he built for others was one he could not fully enter himself.
- Verden, October 782: 4,500 bound prisoners and one day of beheadings on the river Aller — the atrocity that Voltaire, the Nazis, and modern historians have all had to reckon with.
- Aachen, 802: an elephant named Abul-Abbas, the only one Harun al-Rashid had, lumbering into a north-European palace town — the reach of the empire in a single image.
- Paris, 1878–1882: the city council debating whether to refuse a colossal statue of Charlemagne because he was a tyrant with "little to do with the history of the capital" — the perfect ironic frame for a Paris-centric bio series to admit the connection is thin, then explain why he matters anyway.

### World Context

- Post-Roman western Europe: no emperor in the West since 476; literacy, cities, and administration shrunken; the Merovingian kings reduced to figureheads behind their mayors of the palace — Charlemagne's family's own path to the throne (751) was a palace coup blessed after the fact.
- The papacy squeezed between Lombards, Byzantium (mid-iconoclasm), and Roman street politics — desperate for a protector and finding one north of the Alps; the Frankish-papal alliance (Pepin's anointing, the Donation of Pepin) is the mechanism that produces 800.
- Byzantium as the real empire: in 797 Empress Irene blinds and deposes her own son and rules alone — the Lorsch annals argue the imperial throne was therefore "vacant," one contemporary justification for crowning a Frank. The coronation ignites the centuries-long "problem of two emperors."
- The pagan north and east: Saxons, Avars, Danes — conversion by mission and by sword; the frontier wars are religious wars.
- The Abbasid caliphate under Harun al-Rashid as the third superpower, useful to Charlemagne against both Córdoba and Constantinople.

### Key People

- Pepin the Short and Bertrada: the usurper father anointed by a pope, and the politically active mother who brokered (and outlived) the Lombard marriage.
- Carloman (brother): the co-king whose sudden death in 771 made everything possible; his widow and sons fled to the Lombards and — after Verona, 774 — disappear from history.
- Hildegard: the teenage bride who became his most important queen — nine children in twelve years, dead at ~25; followed by Fastrada (harsh reputation in the sources) and Luitgard, plus concubines; Einhard is loyally discreet about the irregular household.
- Alcuin of York: schoolmaster of Europe, head of the palace school, author of the *Imperium Christianum* idea — and critic of forced conversion (he protested the Saxon tithe and mission-by-terror; a voice for the bio's conscience).
- Einhard: the tiny, brilliant courtier-biographer whose Suetonian *Vita* fixed the image of the man for all time.
- Popes Adrian I (partner, whose death made Charlemagne weep) and Leo III (the battered, compromised pope whose rescue set up the coronation).
- Widukind: the Saxon resistance leader — fled to the Danes, blamed by the Saxon chiefs for the rebellion, finally baptized in 785 with Charlemagne standing godfather.
- Desiderius and Tassilo: the Lombard king and Bavarian duke — the two cousins-in-law/rivals he deposed into monasteries.
- Roland (Hruodland): a one-line casualty in Einhard who becomes, in legend, the greatest knight in European literature.
- Harun al-Rashid and Empress Irene: the caliph who sent an elephant; the empress he reportedly considered marrying (per Theophanes) before her fall.
- Louis the Pious: the last son standing, crowned by his father's own hands in 813 — inheritor of an empire that would not hold together.

### Conflicts

- The sword and the cross: a genuinely pious ruler (daily mass, church reform, correct liturgy) who converted Saxony at the edge of the blade — Verden and the *Capitulatio* against Alcuin's protests; the central moral tension.
- Emperor vs. Frank: he took Roman imperial dignity but avoided the Roman title, never went back to Rome, dressed in Frankish clothes (Einhard stresses he refused foreign dress), and crowned his heir himself — a man using empire without being absorbed by it.
- The family: a brother conveniently dead, nephews vanished, a firstborn son disinherited and packed off to a monastery after plotting patricide, daughters kept unmarried at court (Einhard: he could not bear to part with them, tolerating scandal instead) — dynastic warmth and dynastic ruthlessness in the same household.
- Learning vs. limits: the patron of Europe's educational revival who mastered Latin speech and loved Augustine read aloud at dinner, yet (probably) never learned to write and (disputedly) to read.
- Succession: the 806 division ignored the imperial title; by 813 death had made the question moot — one heir, and an empire that would fracture within a generation of his death anyway.

### Character and Motivation

- Physical vitality as a ruling instrument: tall ("seven times the length of his foot"; the 1861 tomb-measurement tradition and Hollister give ~6'3"), tireless rider and hunter, a swimmer "such an adept that none could surpass him," bathing at Aachen with a hundred courtiers at a time; moderate with wine, impatient with fasting, fond of roast meat straight from the spit (Einhard).
- Sociable and voluble: loved company at bath and table, "warm and talkative" but also "hard, cruel, and violent" when crossed (Hollister's summary); wept openly at the deaths of his children and of Pope Adrian.
- A drive to order and correctness: capitularies on everything from estate management to spelling; he collected the old heroic songs of the Franks, began a grammar of his native tongue, renamed the months in Frankish — a barbarian-king's confidence that his own culture deserved preservation alongside Latin.
- Motivation: contemporaries framed it as the *renovatio* of Christian Rome and the care of the Christian people; modern historians add sheer dynastic acquisitiveness. Both are true; neither alone explains him.

### Climax Candidates

- The coronation of 800 (recommended): the scene with maximum stakes, maximum ambiguity, and the longest shadow — stage it honestly with the surprise question open.
- Verden 782 as dark climax of the Saxon wars — if the bio's arc is the cost of unification.
- 11 September 813: the old emperor placing the crown on Louis's head in his own chapel, no pope in sight — the quiet answer to Christmas 800, eleven weeks before his final illness.
- 28 January 814: the death at Aachen and same-day burial in his own chapel; then the empire's slow-motion fracture to Verdun 843 as epilogue.

### Legacy

- "Father of Europe" is not a modern PR coinage: his own court poet called him "Europae venerandus apex, pater optimus" c. 800 (Paderborn Epic), and Bullough's classic 1970 survey took *Europae Pater* as its title. But its modern currency is a deliberate post-1945 choice — historiography pivoted from nationalist Charlemagne to European Charlemagne (a reading first sketched by Friedrich Schlegel in the Romantic era).
- Both France and Germany number their kings from him ("Charles I" / "Karl I"); Napoleon and Barbarossa each claimed him; the 16th-century French-German quarrel over his "nationality" ran hot through the 19th century and turned lethal in the 20th (Nazi Sachsenhain vs. Hitler's 1942 rehabilitation; an SS division of French volunteers was named "Charlemagne").
- The institutional afterlife: Aachen Cathedral (Germany's first World Heritage Site, 1978) with the Karlsschrein and the (unverifiable) throne; the Karlspreis for European unification, awarded since 1950 in the town hall built on his palace; EU buildings named for him.
- The French thread, honestly told: Paris was Merovingian, not Carolingian — his line lost West Francia to the Capetians in 987, and his capital sits today in Germany. Yet France venerated him from Charles V, schoolchildren kept his feast, the *Song of Roland* is the founding monument of French literature, and the Rochet statue stands (after nearly being refused) beside Notre-Dame. The French Charlemagne is a memory-construction — which is itself a good story.
- Scholarly consensus: Davis's line that he "invented medieval rulership"; the Carolingian Renaissance as "one of the most durable and resilient elements" of his legacy (Contreni) — Carolingian minuscule literally shapes the letters this dossier is typed in.

## Controversies and Debates

- **Birth year and place:** 742 (old tradition, from Einhard's age-72 and the epitaph — possibly Psalm-90 convention) vs. 747 vs. **2 April 748** (Werner 1973 + Becher 1992; now the majority view — Nelson 2019, pp. 28–29, treats it fully). Birthplace simply unknown; popular sources still print "742, probably Aachen" (e.g., World History Encyclopedia, the Met's essay title). The bio should say "probably 748" and resist naming a birthplace.
- **The Massacre of Verden (782):** The Royal Frankish Annals — a *pro-Frankish* source — state 4,500 Saxons were beheaded in one day. Since the 1870s some scholars proposed a copyist's error (*delocati*, "deported," for *decollati*, "beheaded"); this exoneration is generally rejected. Some regard 4,500 as exaggerated (Mayr-Harting: even a tenth would have been a catastrophe in Saxon memory); Fried: "the basic truth of the event is not in doubt"; Barbero: "perhaps the greatest stain on his reputation." Its afterlife is its own story: praised as piety in the 1500s, damned by Voltaire, memorialized by the Nazis against Charlemagne (1935) and then flipped when Hitler adopted him (1942). Present the event as certain, the number as approximate, and the exculpations as failed.
- **Was the coronation a surprise?** Einhard says Charlemagne would not have entered St. Peter's had he known Leo's design. Some historians accept this as broadly truthful (or as irritation at the *manner* — the pope crowning the emperor set a precedent popes would exploit for centuries); others read it as a literary humility topos. Collins argues the coronation was planned from the 799 Paderborn meeting; Fried says Charlemagne intended the title "by 798 at the latest"; Alcuin's *Imperium Christianum* language and the Paderborn Epic's imperial phraseology predate 800. Also disputed: whose interests it served (Leo needed a protector and status after his near-lynching; the Lorsch annals justified it by the "vacancy" of a woman, Irene, on the Byzantine throne — Pirenne dissents). Do not resolve; stage it with the debate visible.
- **Could he read?** Einhard records only the failed late-life writing attempts and the love of books read aloud. Fried thinks it likely he could read; Dutton finds the evidence "circumstantial and inferential at best" and doubts he ever properly mastered it. "He could not write, and whether he could read is debated" is the safe formulation — do not say "illiterate" flatly.
- **The fate of Carloman's sons:** captured with Verona in 774, never heard of again. Nelson invokes the Princes in the Tower; Fried offers monastery or murder. Say "their fate is unknown" and let the implication sit.
- **French or German?** Both and neither — a Frank from the Rhine-Meuse heartlands who spoke a Germanic dialect (plus Latin, and understood some Greek). The nationality quarrel dates to the 16th century and peaked in the 19th–20th; the modern "father of Europe" framing is partly a conscious postwar repair job. The bio can use the Paris statue fight (left: a tyrant with "little to do with the history of the capital"; Viollet-le-Duc championing it; installed "provisionally" in 1882 and never removed) as the emblem of contested memory.
- **Sainthood:** canonized in 1165 by the antipope Paschal III at Barbarossa's instigation — never valid in Rome's eyes, yet the cult persisted at Aachen and in France. "A saint only by antipope" is accurate and vivid.
- **Roland and the legend:** the historical Roncevaux (778) was a Basque ambush on a baggage train, suppressed in the annals' first version; the *Song of Roland* (c. 1100) remade it as martyrdom against Saracens, and the Notre-Dame statue armors the legend in bronze (with a fictional Olivier and a crown made 150 years after his death). Always distinguish the layers.

## Drafting Handoff

- **Recommended hook:** Open on the Paris statue — a snowy parvis, an emperor in bronze whom the city council tried to refuse because he "had little to do" with Paris — then cut to who the man actually was: Christmas Day 800 in Rome. This gives the series its Paris anchor *and* its honesty in the first paragraph. Alternative pure-history hook: the coronation itself, with Einhard's protest quoted and immediately questioned.
- **Central arc:** A Frankish heir doubles his kingdom by war and luck (dead brother, vanished nephews), converts a people by the sword at hideous cost, gathers Europe's scholars to a hot-springs palace to rebuild learning he himself can barely access — and on one Christmas morning is handed (or takes) the Roman Empire back. Then the long dissolve: one surviving heir, a fractured empire within thirty years of his death, and a memory so powerful that France, Germany, the Church, Napoleon, the Nazis, and the EU have all fought to own it. The life is the unification; the afterlife is the custody battle.
- **Use caution with:** the birth data (say "probably 2 April 748; place unknown"); "illiterate" (use the write/read distinction); the coronation surprise (present both readings); Verden (number "some 4,500, possibly exaggerated"; event certain; do not use "genocide"); the Aachen throne (cathedral itself says his personal use is unverifiable); Otto III's tomb-opening legend (the seated-corpse story is later embellishment — if used, label as legend); the 6'3" height (Einhard's "seven feet-lengths" plus later measurements — say "well over six feet" loosely); anything from Notker (legend, two generations later); "Holy Roman Emperor" (anachronistic for 800 — he was crowned "emperor"; the Holy Roman Empire label belongs to Otto I's line onward; if the site's framing requires "first Holy Roman Emperor," add "as later tradition counted him").
- **Strongest source base:** the reign from 768 onward (Royal Frankish Annals + Einhard + capitularies, cross-checked by Nelson/Fried/McKitterick); Aachen and the burial (cathedral, Karlsschrein); the legacy chain (Verdun, Aachen coronations, Karlspreis — all well documented).
- **Weakest source base:** birth, childhood, and education (Einhard explicitly refuses the subject); the interior man (all sources are courtly or hostile centuries later); the Paris statue details (French Wikipedia article flagged as under-sourced — corroborate the council-debate specifics before quoting figures); anything about his wives' perspectives (essentially no female-voiced sources).
