# Victor Hugo — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did a royalist prodigy who wrote odes for the Bourbon court become the exiled conscience of the French Republic — a man who refused an emperor's amnesty for eleven more years of rock-bound exile ("When liberty returns, I will return"), and whose funeral drew a crowd larger than the living population of Paris behind a pauper's hearse?
- **Likely angle:** Hugo's life is the nineteenth century in one body: born in 1802 "when the century was two years old" (his own line from *Les Feuilles d'automne*), dead in 1885 as the grandfather of the Third Republic. The spine of the story is the exile — the moment in December 1851 when the most famous writer in France chose nineteen years on Channel rocks over accommodation with Napoleon III, and turned banishment into moral authority. Everything before (Hernani, Notre-Dame, Léopoldine) builds him; everything after (Les Misérables, the return, the Panthéon) cashes the choice.
- **Main caution:** Hugo is the greatest self-mythologizer in French literature — Graham Robb's biography opens by demolishing Hugo's own story of his conception "in mid-air" on a mountain peak. Many beloved anecdotes (the "?" / "!" telegram, various last words) are apocryphal or embellished, sometimes by Hugo himself. And the secular saint had a shadow ledger: a wife driven into Sainte-Beuve's arms, a mistress kept in near-seclusion for fifty years, coded notebooks recording sexual encounters into his eighties, a mistress jailed for the adultery he committed with impunity as a peer, and an 1879 speech urging Europe to "take" Africa. Neither the saint nor the satyr alone is the man.

## Timeline Notes

- 1802, February 26: Born in Besançon, third son of Major (later General) Léopold Hugo — a soldier of the Revolution and Empire serving Joseph Bonaparte — and Sophie Trébuchet, a Nantes royalist whose sea-captain father had made slave-trade voyages (Robb). The parents' marriage is a running war; they separate definitively in 1818. Hugo's childhood alternates between his mother's Paris (the Feuillantines garden) and his father's postings.
- 1811–12: A year in Madrid, where his father is a governor for King Joseph; Victor at the Collège des Nobles. Spain stocks his imagination (Hernani and Ruy Blas are Spanish stories).
- 1816: Diary, age 14: "I shall be Chateaubriand or nothing."
- 1817–1822: Prodigy years — Académie française competition mention at 15 (the academicians doubted his age); Toulouse Floral Games prize 1819; royal pension for an ode on the Duc de Berry's assassination (1820); *Odes et poésies diverses* (1822). A royalist and Catholic poet, rewarded by the Bourbon court.
- 1822, October 12: Marries childhood sweetheart Adèle Foucher (his mother had opposed it; she died in 1821). Five children: Léopold (1823, died in infancy), Léopoldine (1824), Charles (1826), François-Victor (1828), Adèle (1830).
- 1825: Chevalier of the Legion of Honor; invited to Charles X's coronation at Reims.
- 1827: Preface to *Cromwell* — the manifesto of French Romanticism.
- 1829: *Le Dernier Jour d'un condamné* — the anti-death-penalty cause he keeps for life; *Marion de Lorme* banned by censors.
- 1830, February 25: *Hernani* premieres at the Comédie-Française — the "battle of Hernani," Hugo's young Romantic claque (Gautier in his scarlet waistcoat, by later legend) shouting down the Classicists night after night. Romanticism takes the citadel; five months later the July Revolution takes the throne.
- 1831: *Notre-Dame de Paris* — the novel that makes the cathedral a character and helps ignite the Gothic-restoration movement (Viollet-le-Duc's restoration begins 1844).
- 1832: *Le Roi s'amuse* banned after one night (the trial radicalizes him on press/theater freedom — museum timeline). Family moves to 6 Place Royale (now Place des Vosges), the apartment that is today's museum. (Verdi later turns the banned play into *Rigoletto*, 1851.)
- 1833: During *Lucrèce Borgia* rehearsals meets the actress Juliette Drouet — mistress, copyist, travel companion, and near-recluse for his sake for fifty years; some 20,000 surviving letters from her.
- 1835–1843: The high Romantic decade — *Ruy Blas* (1838); elected to the Académie française (1841, after several failed attempts); *Les Burgraves* flops (1843), and he turns from theater.
- 1843, September 4: Léopoldine, 19, married seven months and reportedly pregnant (say "by some accounts"), drowns in the Seine at Villequier with her husband Charles Vacquerie, who dies trying to save her. Hugo, traveling in the south with Juliette Drouet, learns of it on 9 September from a newspaper in a café at Rochefort. He publishes no new book until exile; the grief poems (*Demain, dès l'aube...*, *À Villequier*) appear in *Les Contemplations* (1856), a book he structured "Autrefois / Aujourd'hui" — before and after 4 September 1843.
- 1845: Made a Peer of France in April; on 5 July caught in flagrante with Léonie Biard (née d'Aunet) — she gets two months in prison and six in a convent; he is untouchable as a peer. Weeks later he begins *Les Misères*, the draft that becomes *Les Misérables*.
- 1848: In the February Revolution he first backs a regency, then rallies to the Republic; elected to the Constituent Assembly in June. During the June Days workers' rising, insurgents briefly occupy his own apartment (his 1876 account). September 15: "You have overthrown the throne... Now overthrow the scaffold." He supports Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte's presidential candidacy — a fact to keep, because it makes the rupture mean something.
- 1849–1851: Breaks with the conservative majority — the 1849 "misery" speech ("I am of those who think... we can destroy misery"); presides over the Paris Peace Congress (1849), calling for a "United States of Europe"; July 1851, mocks the president's ambitions: because we had Napoléon le Grand, "must we have Napoléon le Petit?"
- 1851, December 2–11: Louis-Napoléon's coup. Hugo and the left deputies attempt resistance in the streets (barricades, proclamations; his own accounts in *Histoire d'un crime* — heroic, and self-heroizing; Robb is drier). With arrest threatened he escapes to Brussels on 11 December under a false workman's passport. Exile lasts nineteen years.
- 1852: Expelled from Belgium after *Napoléon le Petit*, the pamphlet that sells clandestinely by the tens of thousands in France. August: to Jersey (Marine Terrace). The Paris furniture is auctioned off.
- 1853: *Les Châtiments*, the great verse indictment of the Second Empire. September: Delphine de Girardin introduces the turning tables; for about two years the exiles transcribe séances — the first spirit to "speak" is Léopoldine. (See Controversies.)
- 1854: Campaigns (unsuccessfully) against the Guernsey hanging of John Tapner; draws *Le Pendu* ("The Hanged Man"), later reused as an homage to John Brown.
- 1855, October: Expelled from Jersey after the exiles' paper *L'Homme* prints an open letter attacking Queen Victoria's rapprochement with Napoleon III. Moves to Guernsey.
- 1856: *Les Contemplations* is a triumph; with the proceeds he buys Hauteville House in Saint Peter Port — the only house he ever owns — and spends three years turning it into a habitable manifesto, with the glass "Lookout" study on the roof (installed 1862) facing the sea, and on clear days, France.
- 1859: Refuses Napoleon III's amnesty: "Faithful to the engagement I have made with my conscience, I shall share to the end the exile of liberty. When liberty returns, I will return." December: open letter pleading for John Brown's life.
- 1861–62: Finishes *Les Misérables* (largely at Hauteville House); published 1862 in Brussels and Paris to colossal, Europe-wide success. The same year he begins weekly dinners for poor Guernsey children at Hauteville House.
- 1866–1869: *Les Travailleurs de la mer* (1866, dedicated to Guernsey — it puts the word *pieuvre* into French); *L'Homme qui rit* (1869). Wife Adèle dies in Brussels, 1868; still banned, he accompanies the coffin only to the French border. Sons found the opposition paper *Le Rappel* in Paris (1869).
- 1870, September: Sedan destroys the Empire; the Republic is proclaimed 4 September; Hugo returns to Paris on 5 September to a hero's welcome after nineteen years and one day short of two decades of exile (he had planted an oak of the "United States of Europe" at Hauteville House on 14 July). Stays through the Prussian siege, eating zoo animals sent to him ("eating the unknown," his diary says of the menu).
- 1871: Elected deputy for Paris (February); resigns within a month at Bordeaux. Son Charles dies suddenly in March; Hugo is burying him in Paris the day the Commune's barricades rise. In Brussels during the Bloody Week he offers asylum to hunted Communards — a mob attacks his house shouting "Death to Victor Hugo!" and Belgium expels him. He condemns both the Commune's follies and the ferocity of the repression, then spends years pressing for amnesty.
- 1872: Daughter Adèle brought home from Barbados, insane; institutionalized at Saint-Mandé (later Suresnes; she dies 1915). Loses re-election. Retreats to Guernsey for a last year to write *Quatrevingt-treize* (published 1874). Census-taker asks his religion: "No. A Freethinker."
- 1873: Son François-Victor (translator of Shakespeare) dies — the fourth of his five children he has outlived.
- 1876: Elected senator; first major fight is amnesty for the Communards (finally won in 1880).
- 1877: *L'Art d'être grand-père* — the national grandfather image, built on Georges and Jeanne, the grandchildren he is raising after Charles's death. *Histoire d'un crime* published as the anti-Bonapartist warning: "This book is more than timely; it is urgent."
- 1878, June 27: Stroke (mild but real); effectively ends new composition — later "new" books are assembled from the enormous manuscript trunk. Moves to Avenue d'Eylau.
- 1881, February 25–27: National festival for his 80th year (technically his 79th birthday) — some 600,000 marchers file past his window for six hours; the avenue is renamed Avenue Victor-Hugo. Letters could be addressed "To Mister Victor Hugo, in his avenue, Paris."
- 1883, May 11: Juliette Drouet dies. His will's literary clause (1881 codicil): manuscripts to the Bibliothèque nationale, "which will one day be the Library of the United States of Europe."
- 1885, May 22: Dies of pneumonia at 124 Avenue Victor-Hugo, aged 83. Reported deathbed sayings — the note "Aimer, c'est agir" ("To love is to act") written days before is documented; the whispered "I see black light" is reported by household memoirs (label as reported). May 24: Chamber votes a national funeral 415–3. May 27: decree in the *Journal officiel* designates the Panthéon, taking the building from the Church for good. May 31–June 1: the coffin lies overnight under the Arc de Triomphe, veiled in black crape, guarded by relays of schoolboys and cuirassiers; next day, per his will, the "corbillard des pauvres" — the plain pauper's hearse — carries him down the Champs-Élysées to the Panthéon behind 21 cannon salvos, 19 orators, 1,168 delegations, and a crowd of roughly two million, possibly three — more than the population of Paris.
- Posthumous: shares Panthéon vault with Dumas (moved 2002) and Zola (1908). Musée Victor Hugo opens at Place des Vosges 30 June 1903 (Paul Meurice's creation); Hauteville House given to Paris 1927; *Les Misérables* the musical (Paris 1980, London 1985) globalizes the legend; bicentennial 2002 (BnF "l'homme océan" exhibition).

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- The pauper's hearse under the Arc de Triomphe, 1 June 1885: the state gives him a Pharaoh's funeral; his will insists on the wagon that carried the nameless poor. Two million people watch the cheapest hearse in Paris pass under the biggest arch in Europe. The whole double character of the man in one image.
- The café at Rochefort, 9 September 1843: the most famous writer in France, on holiday with his mistress, opens a newspaper over a drink and reads that his daughter drowned five days earlier.
- The battle of Hernani, 25 February 1830: a theater premiere run like a military operation — young Romantics packed into the pit at 3 p.m., howling matches every night for months — as a proxy war for everything old against everything new.
- The Lookout at Hauteville House: an aging exile writing *Les Misérables* standing at a lectern in a glass box on his roof, looking across the water at the coast of the country he refuses to re-enter.
- 1859, the amnesty refusal in one line: "When liberty returns, I will return."

### World Context

- Napoleon's Empire and its afterlives: Hugo's father a general of the Empire, his mother a royalist — the parents' war is France's war, and Hugo lives every regime: First Empire, Restoration, July Monarchy, Second Republic, Second Empire, Third Republic.
- Romanticism as generational insurgency: censorship of the stage, the Academy as fortress, *Hernani* as storming party; the 1830 and 1848 revolutions rhyming with the literary battles.
- The industrializing city and its poor: the world of *Les Misérables* — galleys, foundling children, barricades — was reportage as much as melodrama; his 1849 "destroy misery" speech states the program.
- The Second Empire: press censorship, plebiscitary Caesarism, prosperity and repression — the regime Hugo made it his exile's work to delegitimize by pamphlet and poem smuggled in like contraband.
- The Channel Islands: British soil within sight of France, a hive of European exiles, séance fashion arriving from America in 1853; Guernsey's fishermen and sea furnish *Les Travailleurs de la mer*.
- The early Third Republic inventing its saints: schoolbooks, statues, street names, and finally the Panthéon — Hugo's funeral was the young Republic's founding liturgy.

### Key People

- Adèle Foucher: childhood sweetheart, wife of 46 years, mother of five; wearied by his fame and appetite, she had a long liaison with his best friend, the critic Sainte-Beuve (c. 1830–37); died 1868 with Hugo unable to enter France for the burial at Villequier.
- Juliette Drouet: ex-actress, mistress from February 1833 to her death in 1883; followed him into exile, copied his manuscripts, saved him from arrest in December 1851 (per her own and family accounts — Robb assesses); wrote ~20,000 letters; he never married her.
- Léopoldine Hugo: the adored eldest daughter whose drowning at 19 split his life and his greatest poetry collection in two.
- Adèle Hugo (the younger): the daughter whose obsessive pursuit of Lt. Pinson across the Atlantic ended in fifty years of asylums — the family's buried tragedy; Truffaut filmed it in 1975.
- Charles and François-Victor Hugo: the sons — Charles a journalist jailed under the Second Republic, séance medium in Jersey; François-Victor the translator of Shakespeare; both dead (1871, 1873) before their father.
- Georges and Jeanne: the grandchildren of *L'Art d'être grand-père*; Hugo kept guardianship after Charles's widow remarried (politician Édouard Lockroy).
- Napoleon III: the adversary who made him; Hugo branded him "Napoléon le Petit" from the Assembly rostrum before the coup and spent nineteen years proving the epithet.
- Sainte-Beuve: friend, champion, then betrayer (the affair with Adèle) — the great literary friendship-poisoning of the age.
- Delphine de Girardin: society wit who brought the turning tables to Marine Terrace in 1853.
- Paul Meurice: disciple and "Hugo's man in Paris" through the exile — publisher-agent, later creator of the museum (1903).
- Victor Schœlcher: the abolitionist (author of the 1848 French emancipation decree) at whose banquet Hugo made the 1879 Africa speech — the two causes, abolition and colonization, sharing one stage.
- Sarah Bernhardt, Rodin, Bartholdi: the celebrity constellation of the last years (the 1877 *Hernani* revival; the bust sittings; the 1884 visit to the Statue of Liberty in the workshop — his last public outing).

### Conflicts

- Royalist prodigy vs. republican prophet: the longest political migration in French letters — pensioned by Charles X, buried by the Third Republic. Critics in his own time called it opportunism; he called it growth ("the conscience of a man ripening").
- The private ledger vs. the public conscience: the tribune of the poor and of women's dignity kept a wife, a captive mistress, serial lovers, and coded notebooks; Léonie Biard went to prison for their shared adultery while the Peer of France went home.
- Grief vs. art: Léopoldine's death silenced him for years, then produced *Les Contemplations* — mourning as career-defining masterpiece, with the queasy edge that he monetized and mythologized the loss.
- Exile as martyrdom vs. exile as brand: banishment cost him Paris but made him unassailable — "the Empire's one free man." He refused amnesty in part because return would have spent that authority (frame both readings).
- Ego vs. generosity: the man who reportedly considered renaming Paris after himself (contemporary joke — treat as satire, not fact) also gave systematic charity, fed poor children weekly for two decades, and left 50,000 francs to the poor in his will.
- Faith: anti-clerical but not atheist — fought the Church all his life ("I refuse the oration of all churches") yet insisted "I believe in God" in the same clause; the séances sit inside this lifelong metaphysical hunger.

### Character and Motivation

- Ambition announced at 14 ("Chateaubriand or nothing") and never disguised; a capacity for work that produced some 40 volumes, 4,000 drawings, and 21 published poetry collections.
- Self-mythologizing as reflex: the mountain conception, the heroics of December 1851, the curated grief — Robb's rule: check every Hugo anecdote against a non-Hugo source.
- Physical vitality into old age — the swims, the walks, the documented sexual appetite (notebooks in code even in his eighties); contemporaries joked about it; biographers confirm it.
- Genuine, sustained moral causes with receipts: death penalty (from 1829 to his last years, credited in abolitions in Geneva, Portugal, Colombia), amnesty for the Communards, free education, European union, children's welfare.
- The grandfather persona: the last decade's self-reinvention — white beard, Georges and Jeanne on his knee, national birthday parades — a public-relations masterpiece that was also apparently sincere.
- Belief in progress as religion: last public address (1879): "In the twentieth century war will be dead, the scaffold will be dead... man will live." Use with tragic irony available but not cheap.

### Climax Candidates

- The funeral, 31 May–1 June 1885 (recommended): the night vigil under the Arc, the pauper's hearse, the six-hour river of people — the Republic burying its conscience in a church it deconsecrated for him.
- The return, 5 September 1870: nineteen years of "When liberty returns, I will return" redeemed on the platform of the Gare du Nord the day after the Republic is proclaimed.
- *Les Misérables*, 1862: the exile detonates the century's biggest novel inside the Empire that banned him.
- The refusal of amnesty, 1859: the quiet climax — one sentence that converts a punishment into a chosen station.

### Legacy

- The Third Republic's founding saint: schoolbooks, street names in nearly every French town, the Panthéon as his afterlife address; his funeral fixed the template of republican civic religion.
- The works' afterlives: *Notre-Dame de Paris* helped save the cathedral itself (and returned globally via Disney, 1996, and the 2019 fire coverage); *Rigoletto* from his banned play; *Les Misérables* as perhaps the most-adapted novel on earth — the musical alone seen by well over 100 million people.
- Political afterlife: quoted by French presidents at every crisis; his "United States of Europe" speech (1849) is a founding text in EU rhetoric; his abolitionism and death-penalty campaign are cited in international human-rights genealogies.
- The reappraisals: feminist scholarship on Juliette Drouet and Adèle H. (the cost of orbiting him); postcolonial readings of the 1879 Africa speech; the séance journals moving from embarrassment to serious study (Gallimard's 2014 edition).
- The drawings: 4,000 ink-and-wash works (praised by Delacroix; van Gogh admired them) — a posthumous second reputation as a visual artist, now a museum staple.

## Controversies and Debates

- **The 1879 Africa speech vs. the abolitionist record:** At an 18 May 1879 banquet marking the abolition of slavery, beside Victor Schœlcher, Hugo declared "God offers Africa to Europe. Take it" — civilizing-mission colonialism in full voice, framed by him as the sequel to emancipation. The same man wrote to Maria Weston Chapman (1851) that the United States "must renounce slavery, or renounce liberty," campaigned for John Brown's life in 1859, and let his drawing *Le Pendu* circulate as a Brown memorial. Present both as authentically his: a nineteenth-century universalist whose "universal" was Europe. Do not exonerate via context alone, and do not erase the abolitionism.
- **The Jersey séances (1853–55):** The transcripts are real and voluminous (*Le Livre des Tables*); the debate is interpretation. Readings on offer: raw grief for Léopoldine (the first "spirit"); collective family authorship with medium-son Charles unconsciously channeling Hugo's own style (the spirits talk remarkably like Hugo); genuine conviction (he ordered the records kept, and elements surface in his metaphysical poetry); social contagion of the 1853 table-turning craze. Frequently repeated claims that the séances "drove Jules Allix mad" or caused Adèle's collapse are post hoc and should be phrased as speculation. Hugo stopped after 1855 and kept the journals unpublished in his lifetime.
- **Womanizing and the double standard:** Documented beyond dispute (Robb; the coded notebooks; the Biard case where the woman alone was jailed in 1845 thanks to his peerage immunity; Juliette Drouet's fifty-year sequestered devotion; liaisons with servants and admirers into his eighties). The debate is emphasis: hagiographies minimized it for a century; some modern treatments make it the whole story. The bio should state it plainly, note who paid the costs (Adèle wife, Léonie, Juliette, arguably Adèle daughter), and resist both cover-up and reduction.
- **December 1851 — hero or self-mythologizer?** Hugo's own *Histoire d'un crime* paints him directing barricade resistance with a price on his head. Robb and others show the resistance was real but brief, his role genuine but smaller and safer than advertised, and the "25,000-franc price on his head" unverified. Say: he resisted, he fled with a false passport, and he wrote the legend himself.
- **Politics — conversion or opportunism?** His trajectory (Bourbon pensioner → Orléanist peer → elected with conservative support 1848 → backer of Louis-Napoléon's candidacy → republican martyr) was mocked by contemporaries on right and left. Defenders note each shift tracked real events (the 1832 censorship trial, the 1849 misery debates, the coup) and that exile — refusing the 1859 and subsequent amnesties at real personal cost — settled the sincerity question. Give the arc with the 1848 Louis-Napoléon endorsement included; it is the fact hagiographies drop.
- **Crowd figures and superlatives:** Funeral attendance is given as "more than two million" (English sources) to three million (French funeral historiography); Paris's population was about 2.2 million. Use "on the order of two million, by some counts three — more than the city's own population." Similarly the 1881 parade ("hundreds of thousands" per the museum; "600,000" in many accounts) — prefer the museum's phrasing.
- **Quote lore:** The "?" / "!" telegram about *Les Misérables* sales is flagged apocryphal even by Wikipedia. "I see black light" (deathbed) is from household reminiscence — label reported. The Nietzsche jibe about the funeral ("veritable orgy of bad taste") is documented and usable as the dissenting voice. Verify any French quotation against *Actes et paroles* or *Choses vues* before printing.
- **Léopoldine's pregnancy:** Often stated that she was newly pregnant when she drowned; sourcing is uneven — phrase as "by some accounts" or omit.

## Drafting Handoff

- **Recommended hook:** The night of 31 May 1885 — Paris in the dark around the Arc de Triomphe, the coffin of an 83-year-old poet lying under a black-veiled arch built for an emperor's armies, schoolboys standing guard in relays; then, at dawn, not a gilded catafalque but the plain black cart of the paupers, because his will demanded it: fifty thousand francs to the poor, their hearse, no priests, "I believe in God." Then flash back 34 years to the same man fleeing France with false papers.
- **Central arc:** A prodigy who wanted to be "Chateaubriand or nothing" gets everything young — royal pensions, the Academy, the peerage — and is hollowed by grief (Villequier, 1843) and implicated by comfort, until December 1851 forces the one choice that can't be curated: submit or leave. Nineteen years on Atlantic rocks transmute France's most decorated insider into its incorruptible outsider — *Les Châtiments*, *Les Contemplations*, *Les Misérables* all detonated from exile — so that when the Empire falls, the man who returns is no longer just a writer but the Republic's conscience, and his funeral is the Republic's true coronation. Counter-movement to keep running underneath: the women and children who paid the private bill for the public monument.
- **Use caution with:** the conception-on-the-mountain and other family legends (Robb debunks); his own December 1851 heroics (*Histoire d'un crime* is a source and a performance); the "?"/"!" telegram (apocryphal); deathbed words (reported); Léopoldine's pregnancy (by some accounts); crowd numbers (give ranges); Juliette Drouet's death date if cross-checking the museum timeline (its "1853" is a typo for 1883); the 1879 Africa speech (quote it accurately and date it — do not soften, do not let it swallow the abolitionist record); the séances (transcripts factual, interpretations and "madness" consequences speculative).
- **Strongest source base:** The chronology and material world (Maisons de Victor Hugo timeline, apartment, and Hauteville pages — the museum literally owns the rooms); the funeral (fr. historiography with contemporary accounts, will text verbatim); the exile publications and dates.
- **Weakest source base:** In this pass, no direct consultation of Robb beyond chapter 1, Hovasse, or *Choses vues*/*Actes et paroles* texts (cited from established knowledge); no dedicated scholarly source consulted on the Hernani premiere specifics (red waistcoat etc. — standard but legend-encrusted) or on exact 1851 escape details; the 1879 speech read via Wikipedia's citation of the abolitions-memorial project, not the original text.
