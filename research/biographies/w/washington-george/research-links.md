# George Washington - Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** Why did the most powerful man in America repeatedly give power back — and how should that legacy sit beside the fact that he enslaved hundreds of people?
- **Likely angle:** Washington's defining achievement was not winning battles (he lost more than he won) but establishing, through personal restraint, the precedent that power in a republic is temporary: resigning his commission in 1783, and walking away from the presidency in 1797.
- **Main caution:** Washington is buried under two centuries of myth (the cherry tree, wooden teeth, near-sainthood). Strip the legend, keep the genuinely remarkable acts, and do not smooth over slavery — he owned human beings his whole adult life, pursued escapees, and freed his enslaved workers only by will, only after Martha's death.

## Timeline Notes

- 1732, February 22: Born at Popes Creek, Westmoreland County, Virginia (February 11 under the old-style Julian calendar), eldest child of Augustine Washington and his second wife Mary Ball Washington. Family were middling planters — comfortable, not grand.
- 1743: Father dies; George is eleven. Formal education ends around fifteen; no college, unlike most other founders. Half-brother Lawrence becomes mentor; through Lawrence's marriage, George enters the orbit of the powerful Fairfax family.
- 1748–1752: Works as a land surveyor on the Virginia frontier; begins acquiring land. 1751: only trip abroad, to Barbados with the ailing Lawrence; survives smallpox there (leaving him immune — later significant for army command). 1752: Lawrence dies; George eventually acquires Mount Vernon.
- 1753–1754: Sent by Virginia's governor to warn the French out of the Ohio Country. May 1754: his ambush of a French party at Jumonville Glen (the French officer Jumonville is killed) helps ignite the French and Indian War / Seven Years' War. July 1754: surrenders Fort Necessity — his only formal surrender.
- 1755: Braddock's expedition destroyed on the Monongahela; Washington has horses shot from under him and bullets through his coat, emerges a colonial hero. Commands Virginia's frontier forces; resigns 1758, frustrated at being denied a royal commission.
- 1759: Marries the wealthy widow Martha Dandridge Custis; her Custis estate brings 84 enslaved people under his control. Settles at Mount Vernon as planter, land speculator, and member of the House of Burgesses. No children of his own; raises Martha's.
- 1760s–1774: Shifts tobacco to wheat; grows resentful of British trade debt and imperial policy. Emerges as a leading Virginia voice against British measures; delegate to the First and Second Continental Congresses.
- 1775, June: Unanimously appointed commander in chief of the Continental Army (he attended Congress in uniform). March 1776: forces the British from Boston with cannon on Dorchester Heights.
- 1776, August–December: Catastrophic defeats around New York; army nearly disintegrates. December 25–26, 1776: crosses the Delaware, surprises the Hessians at Trenton; wins again at Princeton — the revival of the cause.
- 1777–1778: Defeats at Brandywine and Germantown; winter at Valley Forge — disease, hunger, and the drilling of the army under Steuben. Survives the Conway Cabal's murmurs against his command.
- 1781, October: With French army and fleet (Rochambeau, de Grasse), traps Cornwallis at Yorktown. Effective end of the war.
- 1783, March: Newburgh Conspiracy — officers, unpaid and angry, flirt with turning the army against Congress. Washington defuses it in person on March 15; putting on his glasses, he remarks he has "grown gray" and now "almost blind" in his country's service. Officers weep; the coup evaporates.
- 1783, December 23: Resigns his commission before Congress at Annapolis and goes home — a voluntary surrender of power that stunned Europe. George III reportedly said that if Washington did this he would be "the greatest man in the world" (secondhand, via the painter Benjamin West — phrase carefully).
- 1787: Presides over the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia; his presence lends the document legitimacy.
- 1789, April 30: Inaugurated first president in New York after unanimous electoral vote (repeated in 1792 — the only president elected unanimously). Builds the executive branch from nothing: the cabinet (Jefferson at State, Hamilton at Treasury, Knox at War), the norm of "Mr. President" over monarchical titles.
- 1790s: Hamilton–Jefferson feud births the first parties, to Washington's dismay. April 22, 1793: Proclamation of Neutrality in the French revolutionary wars. 1794: personally leads ~13,000 militia (part of the way) against the Whiskey Rebellion — then pardons the convicted. 1795: Jay Treaty firestorm; Washington is attacked in the press for the first time.
- 1796, September 19: Farewell Address published — declines a third term; warns against party spirit, sectionalism, and permanent foreign alliances. March 4, 1797: hands power to John Adams and goes home. First peaceful transfer of executive power.
- 1799, December 12–14: Rides his farms for hours in snow, hail, and rain; develops a violent throat infection (likely epiglottitis). Doctors bleed him of roughly five pints — about 40% of his blood. Dies at Mount Vernon between 10 and 11 p.m. on December 14, with Tobias Lear at his side. Last words reported by Lear: "'Tis well."
- 1799 will: Frees the 123 enslaved people he owns outright — but only upon Martha's death; only his valet William Lee is freed immediately. The will could not free the Custis-estate enslaved people (by law). Martha, uneasy living among people whose freedom awaited her death, frees them early, in 1801 (she dies 1802; the Custis enslaved people are then divided among grandchildren).
- 1799 December 26: Henry "Light-Horse Harry" Lee's eulogy: "First in war, first in peace, and first in the hearts of his countrymen."

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- Annapolis, December 23, 1783: the victorious general, with the world watching, hands his commission back to a nearly powerless Congress and rides home to his farm — the act that made everything after possible.
- Newburgh, March 15, 1783: Washington fumbling for his spectacles before his mutinous officers — "I have grown gray in your service" — and dissolving a coup with one gesture.
- The night crossing of the Delaware, December 25, 1776, with the Revolution weeks from collapse.
- The deathbed scene, December 14, 1799: the man who survived two wars killed in two days by a sore throat and his own doctors.

### World Context

- Colonial Virginia: a tobacco-and-slavery gentry society where status meant land, enslaved labor, and a seat in the House of Burgesses; ambitious provincials like Washington chafed against British ceilings (he was refused a royal army commission).
- The imperial crisis of the 1760s–70s: taxes, debt, and the colonists' sense of being treated as second-class Britons.
- Republican fears of standing armies and Caesarism: everyone knew how revolutions usually ended — with a general on horseback. Washington's refusals were legible against Cromwell and, later, in contrast to Napoleon.
- The fragile 1790s: no one knew whether a continental republic could survive; every act of the first presidency set a precedent, and Washington knew it ("I walk on untrodden ground").

### Key People

- Martha Dandridge Custis Washington: wealthy widow, wife of 40 years, partner in Mount Vernon; burned nearly all their correspondence.
- Lawrence Washington: adored older half-brother, mentor, and door into the Fairfax world.
- Alexander Hamilton: wartime aide-de-camp, then Treasury secretary and architect of the administration's economic program; drafted much of the Farewell Address.
- Thomas Jefferson: secretary of state, then leader of the opposition; his feud with Hamilton split the founding generation into parties.
- Marquis de Lafayette: surrogate son; their bond frames the French alliance.
- Horatio Gates and the Newburgh officers; Charles Lee; Benedict Arnold (the betrayal, 1780).
- William Lee: enslaved valet through the entire war, the only person freed outright in Washington's will.
- Ona Judge: enslaved woman of Martha's who escaped in Philadelphia in 1796; Washington pursued her, unsuccessfully, for years.
- Tobias Lear: secretary and eyewitness to the death.

### Conflicts

- Ambition vs. restraint: a fiercely ambitious, image-conscious man whose greatest moves were renunciations.
- The general vs. the army's misery: keeping an unpaid, starving army loyal to a bankrupt Congress for eight years.
- Liberty vs. slavery: the revolutionary who spoke of liberty while owning hundreds; private doubts about slavery vs. lifelong participation, dependence on enslaved labor, and pursuit of runaways.
- Unity vs. faction: a president who hated parties presiding over their birth.
- Temper vs. self-command: contemporaries describe volcanic anger under rigid discipline.

### Character and Motivation

- Driven by honor and reputation more than money or ideology; he curated his image deliberately and understood theater (the uniform at Congress, the spectacles at Newburgh, the exits).
- Not an intellectual like Jefferson or Adams — his genius was judgment, endurance, and knowing when to let go.
- Physically commanding (over six feet), superb horseman, personally brave under fire to the point of recklessness.
- On slavery: expressed growing private unease after the Revolution ("to see the plan adopted by which slavery in this country may be abolished by slow, sure, & imperceptible degrees" — sentiments in his letters), but never acted publicly, and acted privately only posthumously and partially.

### Climax Candidates

- The Annapolis resignation (structural climax of the "gives power back" arc).
- The Farewell Address and the 1797 handover (the republican precedent — two terms held until FDR, later the 22nd Amendment).
- Yorktown (military climax).
- The will and the deathbed (moral climax and its limits).

### Legacy

- The office of the presidency itself — cabinet, two-term norm, civilian control of the military, peaceful transfer of power — is his largest monument.
- Mythologized almost immediately: Parson Weems's cherry tree fable (invented, 1806 edition), the marble demigod, the capital and state named for him.
- Modern reassessment centers slavery: Mount Vernon itself now documents the 577 lives; debates over monuments and school names.
- The "American Cincinnatus": the farmer-general who went home. The precedent held — and where it has been tested, his example is still the reference point.

## Controversies and Debates

- **Slavery:** The central modern debate. Facts: enslaver from age 11 (inherited ten people); 317 enslaved at Mount Vernon in 1799, of whom he owned 123; used dogged legal and extralegal means to recover runaways (Ona Judge, Hercules); rotated enslaved servants out of Pennsylvania to evade its gradual-emancipation law; freed his own enslaved people by will only. Defenders note he was the only slaveholding founding-era president to free all those he owned; critics note he could have done it in life and profited from slavery to the end. Do not flatten either way.
- **Jumonville Glen (1754):** What exactly happened — ambush, "assassination" (the French charge), the killing of the wounded Jumonville by Washington's Indian ally Tanacharison — is contested in the sources. Frame with care: the 22-year-old's blunder helped start a world war.
- **The George III "greatest man in the world" quote:** Secondhand via Benjamin West; use "reportedly."
- **Cherry tree, wooden teeth, silver dollar across the Potomac:** All myth. The dentures were ivory, metal, and human teeth — including teeth purchased from enslaved people (Mount Vernon's ledgers record such a purchase in 1784).
- **His generalship:** Historians agree he lost most of his battles; the debate is whether he was a mediocre tactician and great strategist (keeping the army alive as the war-winning strategy) — the mainstream view — or simply lucky in his enemies and his French allies.
- **The death:** Whether the massive bloodletting killed him or merely hastened an already fatal epiglottitis is debated in the medical literature.

## Drafting Handoff

- **Recommended hook:** Annapolis, December 23, 1783 — the surrender of power, framed by what everyone expected a victorious general to do instead. Fold in the George III remark (as "reportedly").
- **Central arc:** An ambitious provincial who wanted rank and glory learns, through an eight-year war he mostly lost, that his real power is trust — and spends the rest of his life spending that trust to make a republic possible, then giving it back. Counter-arc running underneath: the liberty he embodied was built on people he owned, and his reckoning with that came too late and only partway.
- **Use caution with:** Jumonville details; the George III quote; any Weems material (label as myth); exact enslaved-population numbers (use Mount Vernon's: 577 lifetime, 317 in 1799, 123 owned by GW); last words ("'Tis well" per Lear).
- **Strongest source base:** Mount Vernon digital encyclopedia, Founders Online primary documents, Lear's account, Chernow/Ellis/Wiencek.
- **Weakest source base:** Interior life — Martha burned the letters; personal motivation is inference from behavior and third-party accounts.
