# Auguste Rodin — Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did a near-sighted craftsman's son, rejected three times by the École des Beaux-Arts and accused of cheating the first time anyone noticed his art, become "the most illustrious sculptor of his day" — and how should that triumph sit beside the women whose labor and lives fed it, above all Camille Claudel, and beside a body of work much of which was modeled, carved, enlarged, and cast by other hands?
- **Likely angle:** Rodin's career is a run of scandals that he converted, one by one, into fame: The Age of Bronze (accused of casting from life because his figure was too alive), the Burghers of Calais (too unheroic), the Balzac (so radical the commission was cancelled — and now routinely called the birth of modern sculpture). The arc bends toward the supreme irony that the man accused of faking a body ended by persuading the French state that even casts made after his death are "original Rodins."
- **Main caution:** Two competing mythologies. The romantic one — lone genius liberating sculpture, tragic muse Camille Claudel driven mad by love — flattens Claudel into an appendage and hides the industrial scale of Rodin's studio (assistants carved his marbles; he sometimes never touched them). The newer counter-myth — Rodin the exploiter who stole Claudel's ideas and had her locked up — is also wrong on the facts: her family, not Rodin, committed her, and he quietly sent her money and planned a room for her work in his museum. Keep the workshop economics, the sexual conduct, and the genuine artistic revolution all in frame.

## Timeline Notes

- 1840, November 12: Born in Paris, working-class 5th arrondissement; father Jean-Baptiste Rodin, a police clerk; mother Marie Cheffer. Severely near-sighted — a schoolboy who could barely see the blackboard, drawing instead.
- 1854–1857: The Petite École (school of drawing and mathematics) under Horace Lecoq de Boisbaudran, who taught drawing from memory; fellow pupils Jules Dalou and Alphonse Legros. Wins a first prize in drawing.
- 1857 onward: Rejected three times by the École des Beaux-Arts sculpture section — entrance standards were not high; his 18th-century-inflected modeling apparently offended Neoclassical judges. Spends nearly two decades as an artisan-ornamenter: cornices, caryatids, decorative pieces.
- 1862: His adored sister Maria, two years older, dies (peritonitis) after a failed engagement to a suitor Rodin had introduced. Grief-stricken and guilty, he enters the Congregation of the Blessed Sacrament as a lay brother. Father Pierre-Julian Eymard (later canonized) recognizes both his talent and his unsuitability for the order and sends him back to art. Rodin's bust of Eymard (1863) survives.
- 1864: Meets Rose Beuret, 20, a seamstress — his companion for the next 53 years. Submits *The Man with the Broken Nose* (an old street porter, the back of the head lost when the clay froze and fell away) to the Salon: rejected. A marble version is accepted in 1875. Enters the studio of Albert-Ernest Carrier-Belleuse as chief assistant.
- 1866: Son Auguste-Eugène Beuret born; Rodin never gives him his name and largely neglects him.
- 1870–71: Franco-Prussian War; brief National Guard service, discharged for near-sightedness. Follows Carrier-Belleuse to Brussels (Bourse decorations); stays six years, later in partnership with Antoine Van Rasbourgh. Rose joins him.
- 1875–76: Two-month trip to Italy — Donatello and Michelangelo. "It is Michelangelo who has freed me from academic sculpture."
- 1877: *The Age of Bronze*, modeled on Belgian soldier Auguste Neyt, shown in Brussels (untitled) then at the Paris Salon. Critics allege *surmoulage* — casting from the living body. Rodin fights back with letters to the press, photographs of Neyt by Gaudenzio Marconi, and demands for an inquiry; a committee of sculptors eventually exonerates him. The scandal makes his name; the state buys the bronze (2,200 francs — roughly what the casting had cost him). Returns to Paris; mother dead, father blind and senile.
- 1878: *St. John the Baptist Preaching* — deliberately larger than life (2.01 m) so no one could repeat the accusation.
- 1880: Through Undersecretary Edmund Turquet, wins the state commission for a bronze portal for a planned Museum of Decorative Arts — *The Gates of Hell*, after Dante's Inferno. With it comes a free studio at the Dépôt des Marbres. The museum is never built; the Gates absorb him for decades, generating 200+ figures — *The Thinker* (conceived 1880 as the poet-Dante on the lintel), *The Three Shades*, *The Kiss*, *Ugolino*, *Fugit Amor*. He stops working on the portal c. 1890, shows it stripped of projecting figures in 1900, and never casts it; the first bronze Gates are posthumous (1920s).
- 1883: Supervising Alfred Boucher's class of young sculptresses, meets Camille Claudel, 18 (he is 43). She joins his studio c. 1884 as assistant — entrusted with the hardest parts, hands and feet of the Burghers — and becomes his model, collaborator, and lover. His father dies the same year.
- 1884–85: Calais commissions the monument to the six burghers of 1347 (Mayor Omer Dewavrin); Rodin models them not as a heroic allegory but as six isolated men walking to expected death, ropes around their necks. The committee protests ("this is not how we envisaged our glorious citizens"); Rodin refuses to change the design. Completed 1889; unveiled Calais 3 June 1895 — on the high railed pedestal Rodin opposed. His wish (ground level, among the living) is honored only after his death.
- 1886, October 12: The extraordinary "contract" with Claudel: she will be his only pupil; he swears fidelity and, by most accounts, promises marriage after a trip to Italy. He never leaves Rose.
- 1888: State commissions the marble *Kiss* (shown 1898; Rodin calls it his "huge knick-knack"). 1889: joint exhibition with Monet at Galerie Georges Petit; *Burghers* shown to acclaim.
- 1890–92: Rodin and Claudel at the Château de l'Islette (Touraine); her *Bust of Rodin* acclaimed at the 1892 Salon. Rodin refuses to leave Rose; Claudel's rage shows in savage caricatures of the couple; Rodin, alarmed, withdraws; they part definitively in 1898.
- 1891: The Société des Gens de Lettres, prodded by its president Émile Zola, commissions the Balzac monument (Balzac died 1850; the project had languished 40 years). Rodin promises 18 months; takes seven years — reads all of Balzac, travels Touraine, orders a replica of the writer's monkish dressing gown, models Balzac nude, fat, defiant, before arriving at the cloaked visionary.
- 1895: Buys the Villa des Brillants at Meudon at auction — his home with Rose thereafter.
- 1898, May: The plaster *Balzac* at the Salon de la Société Nationale des Beaux-Arts, beside the marble *Kiss*. Uproar — a "sack of coal," a "snowman," a "seal"; the Société rejects the work as an unrecognizable "rough model"; press parodies multiply. A defense manifesto and subscription draws Monet, Debussy, Clemenceau — but the affair unfolds amid the Dreyfus storm (Zola's "J'accuse" was January 1898) and the subscription reads as Dreyfusard; Rodin, no Dreyfusard, refuses the money, repays the commission, and hauls the plaster to his garden at Meudon. He never sees it in bronze (first cast 1939, Boulevard Raspail). He accepts no further public commissions.
- 1900: At the Exposition Universelle he stakes everything on a self-financed personal pavilion at the Place de l'Alma — some 150 works, the fragmentary Gates included. It secures world fame; collectors, dealers, and portrait clients (some 200,000 francs a year from busts alone) follow.
- 1902–06: Rilke arrives (1902) to write his monograph, serves as secretary 1905–06, is abruptly fired over letters he answered in Rodin's name, later reconciles. Busts of Shaw, Wyndham, Mahler, Clemenceau; drawings of Isadora Duncan and, in July 1906, the Cambodian royal dancers. Colossal *Thinker* installed before the Panthéon (1906; moved to the museum 1922). Affair with Welsh painter Gwen John, who writes him thousands of letters. Honorary doctorate, Oxford, 1907.
- 1908: Rents the ground floor of the Hôtel Biron, the derelict 18th-century mansion Rilke told him about; Rose stays at Meudon. Affair with the American-born Duchesse de Choiseul (to 1912). Edward VII visits Meudon.
- 1913, March 10: Camille Claudel, reclusive and paranoid (convinced "the Ferret" and his gang are stealing her work and poisoning her), is committed to Ville-Évrard eight days after her father's death, at her family's request, on a form signed by one doctor and her brother Paul. Transferred to Montdevergues (near Avignon) in September 1914; despite doctors' repeated advice that she could be released to her family, her mother and brother keep her confined until her death, 19 October 1943 — thirty years, no sculpture, buried in a communal grave. Rodin, aging and soon incapacitated, sends money through Mathias Morhardt and asks that the future museum reserve a room for her works (realized in 1952 through Paul Claudel's donation).
- 1914–16: War. Rodin donates works to Britain (V&A) in 1914; publishes *Les Cathédrales de France*. Strokes in 1916 leave him diminished while flatterers and dealers circle. In three donations (1916) he gives the state everything — works, archives, antiquities collection, and reproduction rights — in exchange for the Hôtel Biron as his museum; Parliament votes acceptance amid noisy opposition (moralists and clericalists objecting to the scandalous sculptor and the secularized convent).
- 1917: January 29 — marries Rose Beuret at Meudon after 53 years; she dies about two weeks later (14 or 16 February; sources differ). November 17 — Rodin dies at Meudon, 77, after lung congestion; his secretary Marcelle Tirel later claimed the unheated villa killed him and that the museum's director had refused to let the dying donor live at the Hôtel Biron. Buried beside Rose at Meudon beneath a cast of *The Thinker*, his chosen headstone.
- 1919: Musée Rodin opens at the Hôtel Biron. Posthumous empire follows: Philadelphia's Rodin Museum (1929, Mastbaum collection), Tokyo (Matsukata), San Francisco (Spreckels), Stanford (Cantor). French law (from 1956) limits editions to twelve casts that still count as originals; the museum casts from his plasters to this day — which is why *The Burghers of Calais* stands in fourteen cities.

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- Paris Salon, 1877: officials whispering that the unknown 36-year-old's statue is too perfect to be sculpture — he must have poured plaster over a living man. Rodin's whole career detonates from an accusation of fraud: the one thing his figure could not be forgiven for was being alive.
- May 1898: the Balzac unveiled — laughter, "snowman," "sack of coal" — and Rodin loading the most important sculpture of the century onto a cart and taking it home to his garden, repaying the money, done with public monuments forever.
- January 29, 1917: a 76-year-old man marrying his 72-year-old companion of 53 years in a freezing villa; she dies in a fortnight, he within the year — having already given everything, including the right to keep making his works after death, to France.
- Camille Claudel at the asylum window for thirty years while, across France, a room in his museum waits for her sculptures — a double image for the cost column of the ledger.

### World Context

- Second Empire and Third Republic Paris: the Salon system as gatekeeper; state commissions as the sculptor's economy; the Beaux-Arts monopoly Rodin failed into and then broke.
- Sculpture as industry: clay original → plaster → pointing machines, praticiens carving marble, foundries casting bronze editions. Rodin didn't invent the system; he pushed it to its limit — and it is why "authenticity" is the permanent Rodin question.
- The 1870 defeat and the Republic's monument mania ("statuomania") — every town wanted bronze heroes; Rodin's Calais and Balzac broke the genre's rules from inside it.
- The 1889 and 1900 Expositions Universelles as fame machines; the Dreyfus Affair splitting the art world in 1898, entangling the Balzac scandal.
- Women in art: the École des Beaux-Arts closed to women until 1897; Claudel trained at the Académie Colarossi and could rise only through a male master's studio — the structural trap beneath the personal tragedy.

### Key People

- Rose Beuret: seamstress, model, mixer of clay, keeper of wet cloths on his sculptures through every absence and affair; married 53 years in, dead in a fortnight. His letter: "I think of how much you must have loved me to put up with my caprices... I remain, in all tenderness, your Rodin."
- Camille Claudel: pupil, assistant (hands and feet of the Burghers), model, lover, rival genius; some heads signed by Rodin were modeled by her (Musée Rodin's own admission). Her line: "I've been making sculptures for seven years and I am Monsieur Rodin's pupil."
- Father Pierre-Julian Eymard: the saint who sent the grieving lay brother back to sculpture.
- Albert-Ernest Carrier-Belleuse: employer, exploiter, later reconciler (the Sèvres job) — Rodin's own model of a factory-master artist.
- Auguste Neyt: the Belgian soldier whose photographed body helped acquit Rodin of "cheating."
- Edmund Turquet and Omer Dewavrin: the officials who bet on him (Gates commission; Calais).
- Émile Zola: got him the Balzac; Zola's Dreyfus bomb then poisoned the politics of defending it.
- Rainer Maria Rilke: secretary, evangelist, author of the monograph; fired and forgiven; carried "il faut toujours travailler" into modern poetry.
- Gwen John and the Duchesse de Choiseul: the later affairs — devotion exploited, and an adventuress who ran the aging studio until friends expelled her.
- Léonce Bénédite: first curator, who coaxed the dying Rodin into letting the Gates be reconstructed and cast.
- Paul Claudel and Mme Claudel: the family that signed Camille in and kept her in against medical advice.

### Conflicts

- Truth to life vs. respectability: his realism read as fraud (Age of Bronze), then as insult (Calais's unheroic heroes), then as madness (Balzac). Every breakthrough arrived disguised as a scandal.
- The atelier paradox: the apostle of the artist's touch ran a workshop where assistants enlarged, carved, and cast; his genius was partly editorial — choosing, combining, leaving off. This is both his modernity and the root of the forgery/authenticity mess.
- Rose vs. Camille: loyalty to the woman of the lean years against passion for the only artist who was his equal in the room; his refusal to choose destroyed one woman slowly and the other quickly.
- The monument vs. the fragment: hired to make public heroes, he kept making headless walking men and partial bodies — art for its own sake smuggled inside civic commissions.
- The old man and the vultures: strokes, flattering duchesses, dealers, forgers, and a state donation fought over in Parliament while he faded.

### Character and Motivation

- Work as religion: slow, myopic, obsessive; studied his models moving freely, modeled by profiles, kept clay figures for decades and recombined them. To Rilke and Gsell he preached patience and labor as the whole secret.
- Thin-skinned about legitimacy: the triple Beaux-Arts rejection and the surmoulage charge left a permanent need to prove he could do it honestly — St. John made over-life-size, the Gates' figures made small, "to prove completely that I could model from life as well as other sculptors."
- Sensual to the point of scandal: the erotic drawings, the models, the affairs; contemporaries called the studio atmosphere pagan. A biography must neither leer nor launder.
- Peasant shrewdness about money and fame after 1900 — and real generosity kept quiet (paying Claudel's rent anonymously, promoting Maillol, Meštrović, and his own assistants' careers).

### Climax Candidates

- The Balzac season, May 1898 (recommended): the century's most advanced sculpture jeered, rejected, and repossessed by its maker — with the Dreyfus Affair howling in the background and Rodin refusing both the commission and the defense fund.
- The Age of Bronze inquiry, 1877–80: a nobody forced to prove with photographs that he had not stolen a body — acquitted into fame.
- The 1900 Alma pavilion: the outsider building his own museum at the World's Fair gates and winning.
- The 1916–17 endgame: donation, marriage, two deaths, and a state museum — triumph and desolation in one winter.

### Legacy

- Consensus: the most important sculptor since Michelangelo (to whom he was compared alive) and the hinge to modern sculpture — the partial figure, the expressive surface, the assemblage. Brancusi (who fled his studio: "nothing grows in the shade of great trees"), Bourdelle, Claudel, Matisse, Moore all downstream.
- Reputation dipped after his death (modernists found him theatrical), rebuilt from the 1950s by Elsen and others; now inescapable — The Thinker and The Kiss are global shorthand for thought and love, far beyond art history.
- The Musée Rodin as living estate: original posthumous casts fund the museum; among the top-ten most-faked artists (Guy Hain forgery ring convicted, 1990s); a verified bronze *Ève* made $18.9M in 2008.
- The Claudel recovery since the 1980s (biographies, the 1988 film, the 2017 Musée Camille Claudel) has permanently changed how Rodin's story is told — she is now a subject, not a footnote, and his bio must reflect that.

## Controversies and Debates

- **The Age of Bronze life-casting accusation (1877):** Critics and some jurors alleged surmoulage — a cast taken from the model's body. Evidence for Rodin: Marconi's photographs of Auguste Neyt showing the statue's departures from the body; testimony of Belgian colleagues; his documented profile-by-profile method; a committee of sculptors exonerated him and the state bought the work. No serious scholar now credits the charge — but keep the irony that the accusation was the making of him, and that he spent years designing works (over-life-size St. John, under-life-size Gates figures) specifically to refute it. Present as a false charge with real consequences, not an open question.
- **Camille Claudel — muse, victim, or erased collaborator?** The facts: 25-year age gap, teacher/employer power imbalance, the 1886 fidelity "contract" he broke, at least one probable pregnancy/abortion claimed in later accounts (weakly documented — phrase cautiously or omit), works modeled by her but signed by him (Musée Rodin concedes specific heads), his refusal to leave Beuret, the 1898 break, her descent into paranoia aimed at him ("The Ferret"), the 1913 committal by her family — not by Rodin — against repeated medical advice thereafter, and his quiet money and the reserved museum room. The romantic-genius reading (tragic muse) and the exploitation reading (career vampirism, then society discarding an inconvenient woman) both live in the literature; the Musée Rodin/curatorial line stresses her independent genius and warns against both. On the asylum: doctors said she could leave; the family refused; but modern experts reviewing her file (per Musée Camille Claudel's curator) say she was genuinely ill. Do not write "Rodin had her committed" — false; do not write "she was sane and imprisoned" — oversimplified; the confinement's cruelty (30 years, no visits from her mother, communal grave) is not in dispute.
- **The Balzac (1891–98):** Rejected by its commissioners as an unfinished "rough model"; parodied viciously; defended by a who's-who manifesto. Complications: the defense subscription's Dreyfusard coloring led Rodin — politically timid, personally anti-Dreyfusard by most accounts — to refuse the fund and withdraw the statue himself. Later verdict: Kenneth Clark called it "the greatest piece of sculpture of the 19th century, perhaps, indeed, the greatest since Michelangelo." Rodin reportedly called it "the sum of my whole life" (verify exact wording via Gsell before quoting). Present as vindicated-radicalism, but keep 1898's genuine hurt and his retreat from public commissions.
- **Workshop practice and authorship:** Rodin modeled clay; assistants (Bourdelle, Despiau, Pompon, Lebossé the enlarger, and dozens more — plus Claudel) enlarged, carved marble, and oversaw casting. Marbles "by Rodin" are mostly praticiens' work after his models; he supervised and finished to varying degrees. This was standard 19th-century practice, but Rodin's scale and his cult of the master's touch make the tension pointed. Frame as method, not scandal — but state it plainly.
- **Posthumous casts and forgeries:** Rodin willed the state his plasters *and reproduction rights*; the Musée Rodin has cast "original" bronzes ever since (French law since 1956 caps editions at twelve). Museum/estate position: these are authentic originals under the artist's own testament (Philadelphia's collection is largely such casts). Critics' position: works cast decades after death, at sizes (the colossal Gates, some enlargements) or in states the artist never approved, with surfaces and patinas he never saw, are reproductions — especially given how much of Rodin's art lay in surface. Market reality: lifetime casts command premiums; fakes abound (Guy Hain ring, Ernest Durig's forged drawings). Present both sides; note that "how many Thinkers are real?" has no uncontested answer.
- **Date discrepancies:** Rose Beuret's death — 14 February (Musée Rodin timeline) vs. 16 February 1917 (Wikipedia and most references). Claudel's committal — 10 March 1913 (documented, Wikipedia/Ayral-Clause) vs. "July 1913" (Musée Rodin essay, likely error). Burghers commission — 1884–85 (standard) vs. the Musée Rodin work page's internally inconsistent 1894/1895. Villa des Brillants purchase — 1895 (auction; standard) vs. 1897 (Wikipedia). Resolve or hedge each in drafting.

## Drafting Handoff

- **Recommended hook:** The 1877 Salon and the surmoulage accusation — the unknown artisan charged with faking life because his statue breathed, proving his honesty with photographs of a soldier's body, and discovering that scandal was a career. It sets up every later theme: the obsession with truth to life, the need for legitimacy, the pattern of outrage-then-vindication, and (ironically) the authenticity wars that outlived him.
- **Central arc:** A tradesman shut out of the academy remakes sculpture by refusing to lie about the human body — each honest work greeted as fraud, insult, or madness until the world catches up — and ends as an institution who bequeaths not just his works but the right to keep making them, so that his art literally never stops being produced. Counter-movement: the human cost — the son ignored, Rose in the background for five decades, and Camille Claudel, the one equal, ending in a thirty-year silence her lover did not cause but his world did nothing to prevent.
- **Use caution with:** "Rodin had Claudel committed" (false — family did); any claimed Claudel pregnancy/abortion (weakly documented); exact famous quotes ("sum of my whole life," "Michelangelo freed me" — verify against Gsell/sources and label reported); Rose Beuret's death date (say "about two weeks after the wedding"); the Burghers commission dates (use 1884–85, not the museum page's 1894); Tirel's cold-villa deathbed story (single memoir source — "his secretary later claimed"); the Dreyfus coloring of the Balzac subscription (real but needs careful, brief handling); erotic-life material (state honestly, no prurience).
- **Strongest source base:** The major works and their scandals (Musée Rodin pages, verified); the Claudel relationship (Musée Rodin's own detailed essay plus the Claudel record); the bequest and posthumous-cast system (bequest text, Philadelphia, forgery record).
- **Weakest source base:** This pass had no working web search and lost the Met essay, Orsay, Stanford, and Tate to blocks — no independent journalistic or American-institutional voice yet on the posthumous-cast debate; interior life beyond Rilke's hagiography and Rodin's own curated conversations; the son Auguste-Eugène; financial specifics.
