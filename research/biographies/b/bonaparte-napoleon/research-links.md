# Napoleon Bonaparte - Research Links

## Research Frame

- **Core question:** How did a minor Corsican noble who barely spoke French at ten become Emperor of the French at thirty-five — exporting the Revolution's legal equality across Europe in the same decades he crowned himself, restored colonial slavery, and marched perhaps a million Frenchmen to their deaths — and how much of the Napoleon we know did Napoleon himself invent from a rock in the South Atlantic?
- **Likely angle:** The self-made man as self-made myth. Napoleon's life is the extreme case of talent meeting opportunity: the Revolution destroyed the aristocratic ceiling above an artillery lieutenant from Ajaccio, and he rose through it faster than anyone in European history — then spent his last six years on Saint Helena dictating the version of the story the world would believe. The arc runs from Corsica to Notre-Dame (crowning himself, 2 December 1804) to the burning of Moscow to Longwood House, where the prisoner won the last campaign: the one for his own memory.
- **Main caution:** Napoleon is triply mythologized — by his own propaganda machine (bulletins, David's paintings, the Las Cases *Mémorial*), by British counter-propaganda ("Little Boney," the ogre), and by two centuries of French political memory. Nearly every famous quote and image needs provenance-checking. Keep the ledger honest in both directions: the Napoleonic Code and administrative modernization were real and durable; so were the 1802 reinstatement of slavery, the Haitian catastrophe, censorship and a police state, and wars that killed on the order of millions.

## Timeline Notes

- 1768–69: France buys/annexes Corsica from Genoa (Treaty of Versailles, 15 May 1768); French troops crush Pasquale Paoli's independence fighters at Ponte Nuovo, 9 May 1769. Napoleon's father Carlo (Charles) Buonaparte had been a Paoli partisan — then accommodated the French. Napoleon is born into a defeated cause three months later.
- 1769, August 15: Born Napoleone Buonaparte in Ajaccio, second surviving child of Carlo (1746–1785) and Letizia Ramolino (1750–1836); eight of twelve children survive (Joseph, Napoleon, Lucien, Elisa, Louis, Pauline, Caroline, Jérôme). Family nobility formally recognized 1771 — the ticket to a French military education. His first language is Corsican Italian.
- 1779–1785: Educated on scholarship — Brienne military school from 15 May 1779 (a school "designed for financially distressed nobility"; classmates mock his accent), École Militaire in Paris from October 1784. Commissioned artillery second lieutenant 1785, the same year his father dies of stomach cancer at 38 — a detail that returns at the end.
- 1789–1793: The Revolution opens every door. Napoleon shuttles to Corsica, backs the Revolution, feuds with Paoli; June 1793 the whole family flees Corsica for good — refugees in Toulon and Marseille. He publishes the pro-Jacobin pamphlet *Le souper de Beaucaire* (July 1793).
- 1793, September–December: Siege of Toulon — the break. Artillery commander of the republican force retaking the port from royalists and the British; his plan (take the hill fort, dominate the harbour) works on 16–17 December. Brigadier general at 24.
- 1794–1795: Nearly destroyed by politics — arrested for two weeks after Robespierre's fall (August 1794). Then 13 Vendémiaire (5 October 1795): disperses a royalist rising in Paris with the "whiff of grapeshot"; rewarded with command of the Army of Italy.
- 1796, March 9: Marries Joséphine de Beauharnais, widow of a guillotined general, two days before leaving for Italy. Passionate letters from him; infidelity from her (Hippolyte Charles); he learns of it in Egypt; they stay married anyway.
- 1796–1797: First Italian campaign — a ragged, unpaid army turned into a legend at Lodi, Arcola (the flag on the bridge — image partly manufactured), Rivoli. He negotiates the Treaty of Campo Formio himself (October 1797), loots Italian art for Paris, and pioneers the propaganda bulletin. Founds his own newspapers.
- 1798–1799: Egyptian expedition — Battle of the Pyramids (21 July 1798), then Nelson destroys the French fleet at the Nile (1–2 August); the army is stranded. The savants' *Description de l'Égypte* and the Rosetta Stone come out of it; so does the massacre at Jaffa (March 1799), where 3,000–5,000 Ottoman prisoners were executed, and the plague-hospital visit at Jaffa that his propaganda turned into an icon (Gros's painting). Abandons the army (August 1799) and sails home to a hero's welcome — the failures censored.
- 1799, November 9–10 (18–19 Brumaire): The coup. With Sieyès, Talleyrand, Fouché, and brother Lucien (president of the Council of Five Hundred), he overthrows the Directory. The day nearly collapses — jeered and jostled in the Council chamber, he froze; Lucien saved it by calling in the grenadiers. Provisional consul at 30; the Constitution of the Year VIII soon makes him First Consul, effectively ruler of France.
- 1800: Crosses the Alps; Marengo (14 June) secures the regime. The David painting of the crossing (calm on a rearing charger) is propaganda — he crossed on a mule.
- 1801–1802: Peacemaking and rebuilding — Concordat with Pius VII (1801) reconciles Church and Revolution; Peace of Amiens with Britain (March 1802); lycées founded; Legion of Honour created; Bank of France (1800); prefect system centralizes administration. August 1802: Consul for Life by plebiscite.
- 1802, May 20 (30 Floréal X): The law maintaining slavery — reversing the Revolution's 1794 abolition in practice. Secret instructions tell captains-general to restore slavery everywhere "when appropriate"; it is restored in Guadeloupe (Richepance's brutal repression) and Guyana. In Saint-Domingue, the Leclerc expedition (from December 1801, ~20,000+ troops) fights Toussaint Louverture, who is seized by trickery (June 1802) and dies in a French Alpine fortress (April 1803). Yellow fever and ferocious resistance destroy the French army; Dessalines declares Haitian independence 1 January 1804, then massacres the remaining white colonists. Caribbean wars 1802–1804: on the order of 100,000 dead, ~70% of them Black (Branda/Lentz figures).
- 1803: Sells Louisiana to the United States (~$15 million) to fund the coming war — doubling the size of the US. War with Britain resumes.
- 1804, March 21: Duc d'Enghien, kidnapped from neutral Baden, is shot at Vincennes after a drumhead trial — the crime that horrified Europe ("worse than a crime, a blunder"). The same day, the Civil Code is promulgated: equality before the law, religious toleration, property, end of feudal vestiges — and sharply reduced rights for women. Renamed Code Napoléon 1807; still the spine of civil law in much of the world.
- 1804, May 18–December 2: Emperor of the French by senatus-consulte and plebiscite; coronation at Notre-Dame with Pius VII present — Napoleon crowns himself, then crowns Joséphine (the self-crowning was pre-agreed, not an impulsive snub, but the symbolism was deliberate).
- 1805: Trafalgar (21 October) ends invasion hopes and gives Britain the seas; Austerlitz (2 December, the "Battle of the Three Emperors") — his masterpiece — shatters Austria and Russia. Holy Roman Empire dissolved 1806.
- 1806–1807: Jena–Auerstedt (October 1806) destroys Prussia in a day; the Berlin Decree launches the Continental System (economic war on Britain). Bloody Eylau (February 1807), victory at Friedland, then Tilsit (July 1807): Europe divided with Tsar Alexander I; the Grand Duchy of Warsaw created; brothers put on thrones (Joseph in Naples then Spain, Louis in Holland, Jérôme in Westphalia).
- 1808–1813: The Spanish ulcer. He decoys the Spanish Bourbons off the throne and installs Joseph; Spain rises (Dos de Mayo, 2 May 1808); Goya's *Third of May* fixes the reprisals. Guerrilla war and Wellington's Peninsular campaigns bleed France for five years — ~300,000+ French casualties and Iberian losses commonly put around 700,000.
- 1809: Wagram beats Austria again; Pius VII, who excommunicated him, is arrested and held for years — the anticlerical low point.
- 1809–1811: Dynasty over love — divorces Joséphine (December 1809/January 1810; she keeps the title and Malmaison), marries Archduchess Marie-Louise of Austria (April 1810); their son Napoléon François, "King of Rome," born 20 March 1811.
- 1812: Russia. Crosses the Niemen in June with a multinational army of ~600,000+; Borodino (7 September) is the bloodiest single day of the wars (~70,000 total casualties); Moscow, occupied 14 September, burns that night. The retreat — mud, then ice, Cossacks, the Berezina crossing (26–29 November) — annihilates the army: perhaps 100,000 effective survivors. The 29th Bulletin admits catastrophe but famously ends "His Majesty's health has never been better." He races home by sledge ahead of the army.
- 1813–1814: All Europe converges. Leipzig, the "Battle of the Nations" (16–19 October 1813, ~500,000+ engaged), breaks him in Germany; the brilliant defensive Campaign of France cannot stop the Allies taking Paris (31 March 1814). Marshals refuse to fight on; he abdicates at Fontainebleau (6 April 1814), attempts suicide by an old poison sachet that fails, and is granted the toy sovereignty of Elba. Farewell to the Old Guard, 20 April: soldiers weep.
- 1815: The Hundred Days. Escapes Elba (26 February), lands near Antibes (1 March) with ~1,000 men; at Laffrey, faces the 5th Line sent to stop him — opens his coat: "If any of you will shoot his Emperor, here I am." The regiment defects; Ney, sent to bring him back "in an iron cage," joins him; Paris without a shot (20 March). Liberal constitution (the Acte additionnel), abolition of the slave trade (28 March decree) — legend-polishing and politics at once. Waterloo, 18 June 1815: Wellington holds, Blücher's Prussians arrive, the Imperial Guard breaks. Second abdication 22 June; surrenders to HMS Bellerophon (15 July), writing to the Prince Regent that he comes "like Themistocles" to sit at the hearth of the British people. Britain ships him 7,900 km to Saint Helena instead.
- 1815–1821: Saint Helena. Arrives October 1815 with ~27 followers; from December 1815 lives at Longwood House — damp, rat-infested, windswept — under a 2,100-man garrison and a patrolling squadron. Petty war with Governor Hudson Lowe over the title "Emperor." Dictates memoirs and campaign commentaries; Las Cases takes down the conversations until his expulsion (late 1816). Health collapses from mid-1817; by March 1821 he is bedridden, vomiting, feverish (Bertrand's notebooks record it unsparingly, including his cruel rages at doctor Antommarchi and Mme Bertrand). Dictates his will 11–17 April — claiming he was "assassinated by the English oligarchy," leaving bequests to 97 legatees. Last rites 3 May; dies 5 May 1821, aged 51. Last words reported variously ("France, l'armée, tête d'armée, Joséphine" is the most repeated version — flag as reported). Autopsy (Antommarchi, with British observers): extensive stomach lesion; both reports conclude stomach cancer — his father's disease.
- 1821–1840: Buried in the Valley of the Geraniums under a nameless slab (the British would allow "Napoleon Bonaparte," the French only "Napoléon" — so no name at all). 1823: Las Cases publishes the *Mémorial de Sainte-Hélène*, the century's bestseller and the legend's gospel. 1840: the Retour des cendres — remains exhumed, found well preserved, and returned to Paris; state funeral 15 December 1840 with crowds of 700,000–1,000,000; entombed at Les Invalides (Visconti's porphyry-style sarcophagus, remains placed 1861).
- Posthumous: Napoleon III's Second Empire (1852–1870) rides the legend; Geyl's *Napoleon: For and Against* (1949) maps a century of duelling French verdicts; the 2021 bicentenary of his death reopens the slavery debate publicly (Macron commemorates while naming the 1802 reinstatement a "fault," France's history wars over memory of colonialism).

## Narrative Material

### Hook Candidates

- Notre-Dame, 2 December 1804: the second son of a minor Corsican family lifts the crown from the Pope's hands and places it on his own head — the Revolution ending in an emperor, or completing itself, depending who you ask.
- Laffrey, 7 March 1815: one man walks alone toward a battalion sent to arrest him, opens his grey coat, and invites them to shoot. They carry him to Paris on their shoulders instead. (Well attested in outline; exact words vary by account — phrase as "by the accounts of those present.")
- The 29th Bulletin, December 1812: after losing half a million men in the snow, the sentence "His Majesty's health has never been better" — the propaganda machine and its limits in one line.
- Longwood, April 1821: the dying man dictating his will — "assassinated by the English oligarchy" — and, through Las Cases' pages, drafting the version of his life the 19th century would believe. The last campaign was for memory, and he won it.
- The unnamed tombstone on Saint Helena: Britain and France unable to agree even on what to carve, so the slab says nothing.

### World Context

- The French Revolution as the engine: careers open to talent, mass conscription (the levée en masse), and a Europe of monarchies determined to strangle the Republic — the conditions that let an artillery officer become head of state in a decade.
- Corsica: newly, resentfully French; Napoleon grows up between identities and switches allegiance from Paoli's Corsica to revolutionary France in his early twenties.
- The Atlantic world: Saint-Domingue was the richest colony on earth (sugar, ~600,000 enslaved people of ~800,000 in the French Antilles); the Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) was the largest successful slave revolt in history, and Napoleon's attempt to reverse it produced the first Black republic.
- Britain: the invulnerable enemy — subsidizing coalition after coalition, ruling the seas after Trafalgar, absorbing the Continental System; the duel structures the entire period.
- The wars remade the map: end of the Holy Roman Empire, the Confederation of the Rhine seeding German unification, the Code exported to Italy, the Low Countries, Poland, and beyond; Latin American independence movements openings created by the Peninsular War.

### Key People

- Letizia Bonaparte ("Madame Mère"): the formidable mother; famously skeptical at the height of empire — "pourvu que ça dure" ("so long as it lasts" — attributed, phrase as reported).
- Joseph, Lucien, Louis, Jérôme, Elisa, Pauline, Caroline: the family placed on thrones — the empire as family business, and the brothers mostly failures at it. Lucien saved Brumaire, then broke with him.
- Joséphine de Beauharnais: the love, the infidelity, the partnership, the divorce for an heir; her name in the reported last words.
- Marie-Louise of Austria and the King of Rome (Napoleon II, d. 1832 in Vienna): the dynasty that evaporated.
- Talleyrand and Fouché: the indispensable, treacherous ministers — diplomacy and police; both eventually conspired against him ("merde dans un bas de soie" — attributed).
- Toussaint Louverture: the Black Consul of Saint-Domingue, kidnapped and left to die at Fort de Joux — Napoleon's dark mirror: another soldier of the Revolution who rose to rule, destroyed by him.
- Jean-Jacques Dessalines: finished the Haitian Revolution, proclaimed emperor of Haiti months after Napoleon's own coronation, ordered the 1804 massacres of white colonists.
- Tsar Alexander I: partner at Tilsit, enemy by 1812.
- Wellington and Blücher: Waterloo. Wellington: "the nearest run thing you ever saw in your life" (reported).
- Hudson Lowe: the rigid jailer of Saint Helena, cast by the legend as petty tormentor — a role he half deserved and half was assigned.
- Las Cases, Bertrand, Montholon, Marchand: the exile court — memorialist, loyal marshal (his notebooks the best deathbed source), courtier (once a suspect in the discredited poisoning theory), valet.
- Pope Pius VII: partner of the Concordat, prisoner after 1809 — the arc of Napoleon's dealings with legitimacy itself.

### Conflicts

- Revolution vs. crown: he consolidated legal equality, meritocracy, and secular administration — and strangled the Revolution's liberty: censorship, police state, hereditary throne, a new nobility.
- The Code vs. the chains: the same two years (1802–1804) produced the Civil Code and the reinstatement of slavery; the emancipator of Europe's serfs and Jews re-enslaved the Caribbean. This is the bio's moral hinge — neither fact cancels the other.
- Genius vs. gambler: the same traits — speed, audacity, certainty — that won Austerlitz produced Spain and Russia. "No plan survives him winning too often."
- France vs. Napoleon: at what point did the nation's wars become one man's? By 1813–14 even his marshals had had enough.
- The man vs. the myth: he authored his own legend from Italy onward and perfected it on Saint Helena; every scene needs checking against what the propaganda wanted seen.

### Character and Motivation

- Outsider's hunger: provincial accent mocked at Brienne; the drive of a man from the periphery conquering the center — then wanting the old monarchies' recognition badly enough to marry a Habsburg.
- Capacity: worked 14–16 hour days, dictated to multiple secretaries at once, mastered logistics and law as thoroughly as artillery; the Council of State minutes show him arguing points of the Civil Code line by line.
- Indifference as flaw: Lentz's verdict on the slavery decision — geopolitics and economics interested him keenly, "the human aspect" not at all. The same coldness shows at Jaffa and in the casualty arithmetic ("a man like me troubles himself little about a million deaths" — attributed via Metternich; treat as reported by an enemy).
- Fatalism and theatre: believed in his star; staged everything (the coat, the hat, the hand-in-waistcoat, the bulletins); on Saint Helena, turned even captivity into a stage — imperial etiquette in a rotting bungalow.
- The reported last words and the will show the persona held to the end: France, army, Joséphine; the "English oligarchy" as assassin.

### Climax Candidates

- The 1812 catastrophe (recommended for the fall): Moscow burning, the Berezina, 500,000 gone — hubris meeting winter; the empire never recovers.
- Waterloo, 18 June 1815: the final gamble, and the name that became the word for final defeat.
- The two coronations of 1804 (Paris and, months later, Dessalines' Haiti) as the ironic twin climax of the rise.
- Saint Helena as anti-climax turned true climax: the prisoner constructing immortality — the *Mémorial*, the will, the unnamed grave, the 1840 apotheosis at Les Invalides.

### Legacy

- Institutions that stuck: the Civil Code (still the basis of French and much of the world's private law), prefects, lycées, the Legion of Honour, the Bank of France, Concordat framework — the "masses of granite."
- The wars: on the order of a million French dead and 2+ million other European soldiers, civilians uncounted; the Congress of Vienna order built against his return governed Europe for a century.
- Haiti: the first Black republic, born defeating him — and crippled from 1825 by the indemnity France extorted for recognizing it (a later Bourbon act, but downstream of the 1802–1804 war).
- The legend: Las Cases' *Mémorial* recast the despot as a liberal martyr of European unity; Romanticism, Bonapartism, Napoleon III, and two centuries of art fed on it; Geyl showed each French generation refighting him.
- The reckoning: since the 2005 and 2021 anniversaries, the slavery reinstatement sits at the center of French public memory debates — the bio should model holding "modern state-builder" and "man who re-enslaved the Caribbean" in one frame.

## Controversies and Debates

- **The 1802 reinstatement of slavery and Haiti:** The law of 20 May 1802 formally "maintained" slavery only where the 1794 abolition had never taken effect (Martinique, Indian Ocean colonies) — but Bonaparte's secret instructions ordered captains-general to restore it everywhere at discretion, and they did (Guadeloupe, Guyana; attempted in Saint-Domingue). Debate: motive (colonial-lobby economics and Amiens geopolitics vs. racial animus) and weight (Lentz: real indifference to the human question, "a stain on the posterity of the consular regime"). The "genocide" thesis (Claude Ribbe, 2005, including claims Napoleon pioneered gas chambers in ships' holds) is rejected by specialists — Branda/Lentz found no archival basis for a plan of extermination, and even Pierre Nora denounced the book — but the mainstream accounting is grim enough: ~100,000 dead in the Caribbean wars of 1802–1804, ~70% of them Black, plus Toussaint Louverture's death in a French cell. Napoleon abolished the slave trade in 1815 — a Hundred Days bid for British liberal opinion as much as conviction.
- **War death tolls:** No settled figure. French military dead: Hargenvilliers' 1.7 million (1830s) long circulated; modern demographic work (Houdaille's registry sampling) puts French dead at ~900,000–1,000,000 (439,000 documented combat/hospital deaths plus most of 706,000 "uncertain" cases), ~half in 1812–1814 alone. Allied military dead: ~1–2.5 million, commonly ~2 million (Mikaberidze's breakdowns). Civilian deaths are essentially unquantifiable; aggregate "3.5–6 million" totals appear in the literature (Wikipedia's "six million" is the high end). Separate debate: how much blame is Napoleon's — expansionist choice (Bell, McLynn, Barnett) vs. a war the coalitions kept declaring (Roberts; note Britain declared war in 1803). The bio should give ranges and both framings.
- **Cause of death — cancer vs. arsenic:** Scholarly consensus: advanced gastric cancer, per both 1821 autopsy reports, the Lugli 2007 staging study (≥T3N1M0, tumour >10 cm), the 2011 interdisciplinary review, and Dawson 2016. The arsenic-poisoning theory (launched 1961 from hair analysis; murder usually pinned on Montholon or British agency) retains one genuine datum — authenticated hair really does carry massive, >97% inorganic arsenic (Kintz 2007, "consistent with chronic intoxication") — but comparable levels in his boyhood hair and in Joséphine's and his son's samples point to era-typical chronic exposure (medicines, cosmetics, possibly Longwood's arsenical-green wallpaper), not assassination. Present cancer as the verdict, arsenic as the long-lived myth with a real chemical residue; note the poetic detail that his father died of the same disease (hereditary link now doubted — Lugli suggests H. pylori).
- **The "short" myth:** Napoleon was ~1.68 m (5'6"–5'7" English measure) — average or slightly above for a Frenchman of his era. The myth grew from confusing French pre-metric units (5 pieds 2 pouces ≈ 1.68 m, misread as English 5'2"), from his Guard grenadiers' towering bearskins beside him, from the affectionate "petit caporal," and above all from British caricature (Gillray's "Little Boney"), history's most successful body-shaming campaign. "Napoleon complex" psychology is folklore built on a mistranslation.
- **Self-mythologizing and the *Mémorial*:** The Saint Helena account of a liberal, peace-loving unifier of Europe, betrayed by oligarchies, is retrospective image-making — and the 2004 rediscovery of Las Cases' original manuscript proves the published 1823 text was heavily rewritten and padded, with famous maxims ("What a novel my life is!") absent from the original. Napoleon's bulletins, commissioned paintings (David's Alps crossing vs. Delaroche's mule; Gros's Jaffa), and censored press mean iconic scenes must be checked one by one. "To lie like a bulletin" was already a proverb in his own army.
- **Enlightened reformer vs. despot (the Geyl problem):** One literature (Roberts; the French "golden legend") stresses the Code, meritocracy, religious emancipation of Jews and Protestants, durable institutions; another (Dwyer, Bell, McLynn; the "black legend") stresses the coup, censorship, plebiscitary autocracy, the executed Enghien, the police state, the exploitation of satellite states, plundered art, and the wars. Both are documented. Comparisons to twentieth-century dictators (Geyl 1947, Ribbe 2005) are generally rejected — no genocide, no mass political killing — but "the first modern dictator" (plebiscites, propaganda, police) is a serious scholarly position.
- **Smaller disputed items:** Jaffa massacre of 3,000–5,000 prisoners (well attested; Napoleon's defenders cite broken parole and impossibility of feeding them — a justification, not an erasure). Poisoning of his own plague-stricken soldiers at Jaffa (contested; opium doses discussed — phrase cautiously). The Laffrey "shoot your Emperor" words and most famous quips (including "an army marches on its stomach," "whiff of grapeshot" as his own phrase) vary by source or are later attributions — verify each before quoting as direct speech. Whether Britain deliberately hastened his death via Longwood conditions was alleged even in The Times in his lifetime; no evidence of intent, but the conditions were genuinely unhealthy.

## Drafting Handoff

- **Recommended hook:** The coronation, 2 December 1804 — open with the hands: the crown lifted from the altar, past the Pope, onto his own head; ten years earlier those hands belonged to an unemployed, half-suspect artillery officer whose family had fled Corsica as refugees. Alternative: open at Longwood in April 1821 with the dying man dictating his own legend, and tell the life as the story he was editing.
- **Central arc:** Rise-and-fall with a sting in the tail. Act 1: the outsider the Revolution made possible (Corsica → Toulon → Italy → Brumaire). Act 2: the builder-conqueror at his zenith — Code and coronation, Austerlitz — with the Caribbean betrayal of the Revolution's promise placed squarely in the middle, not a footnote. Act 3: the gambler's ruin — Spain, Russia, Leipzig, Waterloo. Act 4 (the sting): the prisoner wins the war for memory — *Mémorial*, Retour des cendres, and a legend Europe is still arguing about. Frame device: the man who edited his own story vs. the record.
- **Use caution with:** All direct quotes (bulletins, *Mémorial* maxims, Metternich's "million deaths" line — label attributed/reported); the last words (give the common version, flag variants); the height (state ~1.68 m and explain the myth); casualty totals (give ranges: ~1M French military dead, ~2M+ allied, civilians unknown, aggregate estimates 3.5–6M); the slavery law's legal fine print vs. its real effect (the law "maintained," the instructions restored — don't let legalism soften it, and don't inflate it to planned genocide); cause of death (cancer consensus, arsenic myth with its real chemistry); the self-crowning (pre-arranged with the Pope, not a snatch).
- **Strongest source base:** Chronology and the controversies (Fondation Napoléon pages verified this pass; the correspondence-based modern biographies); the death (peer-reviewed medical literature plus Bertrand's day-by-day notebooks); the legend's manufacture (the rediscovered Las Cases manuscript).
- **Weakest source base:** Interior life at the pivotal moments — his own testimony is systematically unreliable, and this pass consulted the major biographies (Roberts, Dwyer, Gueniffey, Mikaberidze, Branda/Lentz) from established knowledge of their arguments rather than page-level reading; precise Caribbean casualty figures rest on Branda/Lentz's estimates; Spanish war civilian losses are acknowledged even by specialists as unsettled.

## Open Questions

1. Best single formulation for total war deaths — adopt "on the order of a million French soldiers and at least two million other Europeans, with civilian deaths beyond counting; some estimates run much higher"?
2. Was the self-crowning gesture negotiated with Pius VII in advance in the exact form it took? (Widely stated as pre-agreed; a page-level source — Roberts or Gueniffey — should be checked before asserting flatly.)
3. The Laffrey speech's precise wording and witnesses — which memoir is the earliest source, and how much did the *Mémorial* embellish it?
4. Longwood wallpaper arsenic (Scheele's green) — supported by some analyses as an exposure source; worth one clause at most, and only as hypothesis.
5. The Jaffa opium/poisoning-of-the-sick episode — what do Dwyer and Bell conclude on the evidence? Needed before including it.
6. Whether to mention the macabre autopsy-relic lore (the removed and sold body part) — well-reported but tabloid-adjacent; recommend omitting or one dry sentence.
7. Haiti's 1825 indemnity is post-Napoleon (Charles X) — if used in the legacy section, phrase so it isn't attributed to Napoleon personally.
