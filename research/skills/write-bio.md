# Write Bio

Use this skill to draft each bite-size bio after `research/skills/research-bio.md` has produced `research/biographies/<initial>/<slug>/research-links.md`. Write the draft in `research/biographies/<initial>/<slug>/biography.md`.

Start from the shared dossier sections in `research-links.md`: Research Frame, Best Sources, Timeline Notes, Narrative Material, Controversies and Debates, Drafting Handoff, and Open Questions.

---

## Research Handoff

Before drafting, read the whole `research-links.md` dossier and map it into the article:

- Use **Research Frame** for the thesis, angle, and main caution.
- Use **Timeline Notes** to preserve chronology and avoid compressing cause and effect.
- Use **Narrative Material / Hook Candidates** for the opening scene.
- Use **World Context** in Early Life & Formation and The Rise / The Work.
- Use **Key People** to keep relationships, opposition, and influence visible.
- Use **Conflicts** and **Character and Motivation** to drive the middle sections.
- Use **Climax Candidates** to choose the defining moment.
- Use **Legacy** and **Controversies and Debates** for the reckoning.
- Use **Drafting Handoff** as the default plan unless the sources clearly point to a better structure.
- Keep **Open Questions** out of the final prose unless they can be answered or framed as uncertainty.

## Guiding Questions

### Hook & Frame
- What single moment or choice defines this person?
- Why should a modern reader care about them?
- What myth or misconception needs correcting?
- Does the hook match the dossier's recommended hook and main caution?

### Life & Context
- When and where did they live? What was the world like?
- What were the key turning points in their life?
- Who were the important people around them — allies, rivals, family?
- What did they want? What stood in their way?
- Which timeline notes and world-context details are essential for the reader to understand the stakes?

### Character & Motivation
- What drove them? (Ambition, ideology, trauma, love, duty?)
- What were their flaws or contradictions?
- How did they see themselves vs. how did others see them?
- Which claims are well sourced, and which need cautious language?

### Legacy & Relevance
- What did they leave behind?
- How are they remembered today — and is that memory fair?
- What can we learn from them now?
- Which controversies should be explained rather than smoothed over?

## Outline Format

Use this as the story structure, not as literal section titles. Published headings should sound specific to the person and moment, such as "The Machine That Talked Back" or "Harpers Ferry," rather than "The Hook" or "The Climax."

### 1. Opening scene (200–300 words)
- Open with a vivid scene, a bold claim, or a pivotal moment
- Establish stakes and why this person matters
- Draw from Hook Candidates and Research Frame

### 2. Formation (400–500 words)
- Birth, family, upbringing
- Key influences and formative experiences
- The world they entered — historical context
- Draw from Timeline Notes, World Context, and Key People

### 3. Central work (500–700 words)
- Their central project, mission, or struggle
- Key events, decisions, and turning points
- Conflicts, setbacks, and how they responded
- Draw from Conflicts, Character and Motivation, and the strongest source base

### 4. Defining turn (400–500 words)
- The defining moment — success, failure, or both
- Consequences and immediate aftermath
- Draw from Climax Candidates and preserve disputed details

### 5. Afterlife and reckoning (300–400 words)
- How they were remembered (or forgotten)
- Reassessment over time
- Why they still matter
- Draw from Legacy, Controversies and Debates, and Open Questions

### 6. Sources & Further Reading
- Key books, articles, archives
- Where to go deeper
- Include the best sources from the dossier, prioritizing primary, institutional, and scholarly sources

## Card Summary

The front matter `summary` (40–180 characters) is the card copy on the opening page. It is shown in full, so every character is visible — write it as a pitch, not a label:

- The card already shows the person's name and portrait. Don't spend characters re-introducing them, and never open with stock phrases like "A narrative biography of," "The story of," or "The life of."
- Lead with the life's central tension, a striking fact, a reversal, or a question.
- Vary the shape: check the summaries of neighboring published bios and don't reuse their opening move.
- Only use facts and framings that appear in the article itself.
- The bite's and nibble's summaries for the same person must take different angles, not reword each other.

---

## Notes

- Write for a curious general reader — not an academic specialist
- Use concrete scenes and specific details over abstraction
- Quote sparingly; when you do, make it count
- Flag anything speculative or disputed
- Do not invent connective tissue to hide gaps in the dossier
- If a section depends on weak or contested evidence, say so plainly or choose a better-supported angle
