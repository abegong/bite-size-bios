On December 2, 1859, a 59-year-old man with a long white beard climbed the gallows steps in Charles Town, Virginia, with the calm of someone going to church. John Brown was about to become the first American executed for treason against a state, for leading a raid on the federal armory at Harpers Ferry in hopes of sparking a slave rebellion. His final note read: "I, John Brown, am now quite certain that the crimes of this guilty land will never be purged away but with blood." Within eighteen months, the bloodiest war in American history had begun.

Born in 1800 in Connecticut to a fiercely abolitionist father, Brown dedicated himself at twelve — after watching an enslaved boy beaten with an iron shovel — to the cause of African Americans. He was a better moralist than businessman: his ventures kept collapsing into bankruptcy, but his Pennsylvania farm sheltered escaping slaves as an Underground Railroad station, and after the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 he founded the League of Gileadites, training free Black men to resist slave catchers. Dissatisfied with fellow abolitionists — "These men are all talk. What we need is action — action!" — he moved to Kansas, where in 1856 he and his sons dragged five unarmed pro-slavery settlers from their homes and hacked them to death at Pottawatomie Creek.

The Harpers Ferry raid of October 1859 failed almost completely: the local slaves did not rise, two of his sons died in the fighting, and U.S. Marines under Robert E. Lee stormed his position within two days. But Brown's serene dignity at trial and on the gallows transformed defeat into martyrdom. Union soldiers marched to "John Brown's Body"; the South saw proof that abolitionism meant race war; and the middle ground of gradual, compromised emancipation became impossible to sustain.

Was he a terrorist, a freedom fighter, a madman, or simply a man who refused to accept evil because it was legal? The debate has never been settled. What is certain is that Brown did as much as any single person to ensure the Civil War would come — and his shadow stretches across American history, haunting and unresolved.
