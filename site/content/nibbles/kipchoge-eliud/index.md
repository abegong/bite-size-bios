On the morning of October 12, 2019, in a Vienna park, Eliud Kipchoge ran the marathon distance in 1 hour, 59 minutes, 40 seconds — the first human ever under two hours. The time counts for nothing: the run was a staged exhibition with forty-one rotating pacemakers, a laser-guided pace car, and drinks handed up by bicycle, and no record book contains it. Kipchoge knew that before he started. He was not chasing a record he already held; he was trying to prove his own slogan — *No human is limited* — and the gap between "not a record" and "proof" is the story of the greatest marathoner who has ever lived.

He was born in 1984 in Kapsisiywa, in Kenya's Rift Valley, the youngest of four raised by a schoolteacher mother; he ran three kilometres to school and sold milk after finishing secondary school. At sixteen he asked local Olympic medalist Patrick Sang for a training program, then quietly completed every session — for months, Sang did not even know his name. At eighteen, Kipchoge outkicked the legendary Hicham El Guerrouj to win the 2003 world 5000m title by four hundredths of a second.

The marathon came later, after he failed to make Kenya's 2012 Olympic track team. From 2014 to 2019 he won ten marathons in a row, took Olympic gold in Rio in 2016 and again in Tokyo in 2021, and set world records of 2:01:39 (Berlin, 2018) and 2:01:09 (Berlin, 2022) — all while living in a spartan training camp where a multimillionaire took his turn scrubbing toilets. "Only the disciplined ones in life are free," he liked to say. Rivals called him The Philosopher.

The erosion, when it came, revealed as much as the dominance. His record fell to young Kelvin Kiptum, who died months later; at the 2024 Paris Olympics, Kipchoge recorded the first DNF of his life and walked off the course with hundreds of spectators walking beside him. Instead of retreating, he kept racing — and losing — finishing his majors career in New York in 2025 and launching a seven-continent charity marathon tour. His legacy is less a number than a demonstration: greatness is mostly a schedule kept for twenty-five years, and barriers are partly agreements a disciplined person can refuse to sign.
