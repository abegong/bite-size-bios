The day after the Armistice of 1918, a nearly blind painter of seventy-eight sat in his garden in Normandy and offered France a gift: not soldiers or flags, but water. Claude Monet proposed to give the state a room-scale cycle of his lily pond, with no horizon and no shore — light lying on water, "an endless whole." It was a strange gift from a man who had spent sixty years chasing the opposite thing: the passing instant, the sunrise that lasts minutes, light on the move. He had been mocked for it, gone hungry for it, and finally grown rich on it.

He was born in Paris in 1840 and raised beside the harbor at Le Havre, where a local painter, Eugène Boudin, dragged the reluctant teenage caricaturist outdoors to paint in the open air. "I owe everything" to him, Monet later said. In Paris he fell in with Renoir, Sisley, and Bazille, met Camille Doncieux — his model, companion, and the face of his early masterpieces — and endured years of real hardship, once even throwing himself into the Seine in a crisis he reported to a friend with an unsettling shrug.

Around November 1872 he dashed off a misty harbor view and, judging it too loose to pass for a proper scene, titled it *Impression, Sunrise*. When the artists mounted their own exhibition in 1874, a critic sneered at the "impression" — and, half by accident, named a movement. Monet went on to paint the same grainstacks, poplars, and cathedral façades over and over, chasing time and light itself; the 1891 grainstacks sold out in days, and the mocked outcast became a wealthy master of the market.

Then cataracts began stealing the very sight his art depended on, browning his world and forcing a dreaded operation. Half-blind, he raced to finish his water lilies, refusing to hand them over while he lived. He died in December 1926; five months later his encircling panels opened at the Orangerie to thin, indifferent crowds. Vindication took thirty years, until a later generation saw in them the Sistine Chapel of Impressionism. Monet spent his life trying to keep the instant — and left behind the one thing that has no end.
